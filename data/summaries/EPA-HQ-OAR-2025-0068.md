# Public Summary: EPA-HQ-OAR-2025-0068-0001

_Source file: `summaries/EPA-HQ-OAR-2025-0068-0001.md`_
_Detected type: `NPRM`_

## What This Document Does
- Proposes to consolidate air curtain incinerator (ACI) opacity rules into a single subpart and standardize monitoring (Method 9).
- Removes Title V permitting requirements for non‑major ACI sources.
- Allows temporary use of existing combustion units as CISWI during disasters without full §129 compliance.
- Retains existing opacity limits (reported in the record as 10% and 35% thresholds).
- Provides a legal framework using a four‑factor “unnecessarily burdensome” test to justify Title V removal and relies on statutory interpretation that some ACI units are not §129 solid waste incineration units.

## Why It Matters
- Environmental outcome: EPA asserts emissions will not change, but the rule acknowledges uncertainty and does not quantify potential emissions from increased ACI use.
- Oversight and transparency: Removing Title V for many ACI operations reduces permitting paperwork and (potentially) public visibility and tracking of operations.
- Disaster response: The rule aims to speed recovery by easing regulatory hurdles for temporary disaster burning, but the duration, tracking, and cumulative effects of such use are not fully specified.
- Incentives and behavior: Regulatory changes may encourage more use of ACIs or reclassification of activity as “temporary disaster use,” with possible unintended emissions increases.
- Legal and procedural risk: Key analytic gaps (emissions modeling, behavioral response, alternatives analysis) may be leverage points for public comment or later challenge.

## Who Is Affected
- Operators and owners of ACIs and other small combustion units.
- Waste management firms and emergency debris‑removal contractors.
- State and local permitting authorities and inspectors (responsibility shifts).
- Communities near ACI deployment sites (air quality and visibility impacts).
- Environmental and public‑health advocates monitoring aggregate emissions and emergency-use practices.
- Emergency planners and response agencies relying on faster permitting pathways.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Issue: Lack of quantified emissions analysis
   - Why it matters: The agency’s conclusion that emissions “do not change” is central to justifying deregulatory steps; without modeled bounds, the environmental tradeoffs are unclear.
   - Practical question to ask: Request that EPA provide activity‑level projections and an emissions sensitivity analysis showing upper and lower bounds on likely emissions under plausible increases in ACI use.

2. Issue: Behavioral response (increased ACI deployment) not analyzed
   - Why it matters: Removing Title V and easing disaster use may change operator behavior, increasing use beyond historical levels and changing cumulative impacts.
   - Practical question to ask: Ask EPA to model or at least qualitatively assess how Title V removal and disaster‑use provisions are likely to affect ACI deployment and classify evidence sources (surveys, contract data, past disaster activity).

3. Issue: Monitoring and enforcement sufficiency after Title V removal
   - Why it matters: The rule assumes opacity monitoring and existing enforcement mechanisms are adequate; reduced permitting could weaken oversight and public notice.
   - Practical question to ask: Request empirical enforcement and compliance data for current ACI opacity monitoring (Method 9) and evidence that enforcement capacity is adequate without Title V permits.

4. Issue: Disaster‑use guardrails are vague
   - Why it matters: “Temporary” disaster use can become de facto long‑term operation without clear duration limits, tracking, or aggregate caps.
   - Practical question to ask: Ask EPA to define maximum duration, documentation/notification requirements, cumulative annual hours or burn quantity limits, and reporting/tracking mechanisms for disaster‑use exemptions.

5. Issue: Missing intermediate regulatory alternatives
   - Why it matters: The rule compares keep vs full removal but gives limited attention to middle options (simplified permitting, activity thresholds) that could reduce burden while preserving oversight.
   - Practical question to ask: Propose and request analysis of intermediate options such as a streamlined Title V for ACIs, de minimis thresholds, or conditional simplified permits tied to emissions caps.
