# Public Summary: EPA-HQ-OLEM-2026-0001-0001

_Source file: `summaries/EPA-HQ-OLEM-2026-0001-0001.md`_

## What This Rule Does
- Proposes deletion of the site from the Superfund National Priorities List (NPL).
- States EPA’s determination that “no further response is appropriate” under CERCLA/NCP deletion criteria.
- Notes that the State has concurred and that EPA relied on site completion documentation in the docket (site completion report and related materials).

## Why It Matters
- Deletion signals EPA believes the remedial work is complete and the site no longer requires further Superfund response.
- Removing a site from the NPL can change public visibility, future oversight, and funding priorities for monitoring or enforcement.
- The NPRM itself provides limited technical detail; the protectiveness determination appears to rest on separate docket documents (completion report, monitoring data, five‑year review results).
- Informational note: this summary is for public information and does not constitute legal advice.

## Who Is Affected
- Nearby residents and community members concerned about ongoing exposure or land reuse.
- Property owners, current and prospective, with interests in the site or adjacent land.
- Potentially responsible parties and their insurers.
- State and local regulators responsible for enforcing any post‑remedy controls or monitoring.
- Downstream or off‑site receptors if contamination was present in groundwater or surface water (assessed via docket materials).

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Data transparency and public access to the technical record
   - Why it matters: The NPRM asserts cleanup is complete but does not summarize contamination levels, cleanup performance, or monitoring results. Commenters need the underlying data to evaluate EPA’s conclusion.
   - Practical question to ask: Please docket and summarize the final site completion report, sampling results that support protectiveness, and any outstanding data gaps.

2. Adequacy of monitoring and long‑term surveillance
   - Why it matters: Post‑removal monitoring is critical to detect contaminant rebound or plume migration. The NPRM refers to monitoring implicitly but does not describe its scope, frequency, or findings.
   - Practical question to ask: What monitoring program is in place (parameters, frequency, duration), and what are the most recent monitoring results showing stability or decline of contaminants?

3. Existence, scope, and enforceability of institutional controls (ICs)
   - Why it matters: If residual contamination remains, ICs (land‑use restrictions, deed notices) are often relied on to protect public health. The NPRM does not summarize ICs or enforcement mechanisms.
   - Practical question to ask: What ICs are currently in place, how are they enforced, and is there documentation (deeds, ordinances) in the docket?

4. Results and conclusions of the most recent five‑year review
   - Why it matters: Five‑year reviews confirm continued protectiveness of remedies that do not achieve unlimited use/unrestricted exposure. The NPRM does not quote or summarize any five‑year review.
   - Practical question to ask: Please provide the latest five‑year review and state whether it concluded the remedy remains protective, and if not, what follow‑up actions were recommended.

5. Nature and meaning of State concurrence
   - Why it matters: State concurrence is required for deletion, but the NPRM does not indicate whether the State conducted an independent technical review or simply concurred procedurally.
   - Practical question to ask: Did the State perform an independent technical evaluation of the completion report/monitoring data? Please provide the State’s supporting analysis or correspondence.
