## What This Document Does
- Proposes revising the Fish and Wildlife Service definition of “shellfish” in 50 CFR 10.12.
- Removes the morphological qualifier “having a shell” and replaces it with a taxa-based definition that expressly includes Mollusca (which the preamble cites to cover cephalopods such as squid, octopus, and cuttlefish), Order Decapoda, and “any part, product, egg or offspring thereof.”
- States the change is intended to clarify coverage for wildlife importation, exportation, declaration, inspection, and enforcement under FWS regulations.
- Characterizes the action as deregulatory and says no conforming amendments to related parts (e.g., parts 14 and 17) are necessary.

## Why It Matters
- Regulatory scope: A definition change can alter which seafood and wildlife products are treated as “shellfish” at U.S. ports and under FWS rules.
- Trade and enforcement effects: The agency claims the change will reduce uncertainty and compliance burden for importers/exporters and improve consistency in inspections — but the administrative record provided here contains little supporting evidence.
- Potential overbreadth: The proposed text substitutes a broad taxonomic label (Mollusca) and derivative-product language, which may cover organisms beyond the stated cephalopod problem and could create new interpretive questions.
- Procedural and legal risk: The docket materials reviewed are sparse (a thin record). Key claims (inconsistent port treatment, burden, and no need for conforming amendments) are asserted but not substantiated in the provided materials, which could be a procedural vulnerability if challenged.
- Drafting quality: The draft contains apparent drafting errors and imprecise phrasing that could affect implementation and enforcement.

## Who Is Affected
- Seafood importers and exporters (including brokers and distributors).
- Customs and border inspection personnel and FWS enforcement staff.
- Processors and trade in mixed or processed seafood products.
- Regulated parties who rely on existing definitions for declarations, permits, and paperwork.
- Interested stakeholders (industry trade groups, enforcement and compliance counsel, conservation organizations).
