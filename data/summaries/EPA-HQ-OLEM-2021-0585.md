# Public Summary: EPA-HQ-OLEM-2021-0585-0393

_Source file: `summaries/EPA-HQ-OLEM-2021-0585-0393.md`_
_Detected type: `NPRM`_

## What This Document Does
- Proposes to delay compliance with EPA’s 2024 hazardous-substance Facility Response Plan (FRP) rule for certain onshore non-transportation facilities by three years — from the prior June 1, 2027 date to June 1, 2030.  
- Proposes removal or revision of certain climate-change and environmental-justice (EJ) references in 40 CFR Part 118 while keeping the FRP program architecture and substantial-harm framework in place.  
- Provides a quantified estimate of cost savings from the delay, but states it cannot fully quantify the number, nature, or magnitude of covered discharges that may occur during the delay period.  
- Note for commenters: the administrative record available to reviewers contains a potential docket inconsistency — an economic support document (delay RIA) appears to analyze a five-year delay to 2032 while the NPRM text states a three-year delay to 2030. This mismatch should be clarified in the docket.

## Why It Matters
- Timing affects protection: delaying FRP compliance defers planning and preparedness actions that reduce the likelihood and harms of hazardous-substance discharges.  
- Benefit asymmetry: EPA quantifies cost savings from the delay but reports it cannot quantify foregone benefits, which risks an imbalanced decision record.  
- Policy content matters: removing explicit climate and EJ language may change how Regional Administrators and facilities prioritize risks, with potential effects on communities already disproportionately exposed.  
- Implementation incentives: a blanket multi-year delay can encourage industry “wait-and-see” behavior and may leave higher-risk facilities less prepared during the added years.  
- Docket integrity: inconsistent supporting analyses (3-year vs. 5-year) reduce transparency and frustrate meaningful comment and review.

## Who Is Affected
- Regulated facilities in the 2024 FRP universe (EPA’s baseline estimate: ~5,354 facilities).  
- Communities near covered facilities, including historically marginalized communities that EPA previously identified as facing disproportionate risk.  
- EPA regional offices responsible for applicability determinations and oversight.  
- State, local, and Tribal emergency planners and response entities that rely on FRPs for coordinated preparedness.  
- Industry stakeholders that must plan compliance spending and FRP development.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Issue: Whether a three‑year delay is justified and why that specific duration is required.  
   - Why it matters: The agency’s central justification is implementation and time to develop compliance-assistance tools, but the record does not clearly show why three years (rather than one, two, or a phased approach) is the appropriate remedy.  
   - Practical question to ask EPA: Provide the concrete implementation milestones and explain why those tasks require three full years; compare outcomes and costs for one-, two-, and three-year delays.

2. Issue: Apparent NPRM / RIA mismatch (three‑year proposal vs. RIA analyzing five‑year delay).  
   - Why it matters: Economic analysis must correspond to the action under review for meaningful notice and comment; a mismatch undermines transparency.  
   - Practical question to ask EPA: Confirm which economic analysis controls the proposal and update the docket so the RIA aligns with the proposed June 1, 2030 compliance date (or revise the proposal).

3. Issue: Unquantified foregone benefits and asymmetric treatment of costs vs. benefits.  
   - Why it matters: EPA reports it “cannot quantify” discharges during the delay period but has historical incident data that could support scenario or bounded analyses; leaving benefits largely qualitative biases the comparison.  
   - Practical question to ask EPA: Provide sensitivity or scenario analyses (bounded estimates) of potential avoided harms and monetized ranges for discharges that could occur during the delay.

4. Issue: Removal of climate‑specific references and EPA’s rationale that such focus “may distort” risk-based planning.  
   - Why it matters: The 2024 rule relied on considering climate-driven severe weather; removing that focus without technical support may reduce resilience to increasing climate-related hazards.  
   - Practical question to ask EPA: Supply technical analysis showing how climate references systematically skew planning, or explain how climate risks will still be incorporated operationally without explicit language.

5. Issue: Deletion of EJ‑specific language and reliance on generic “substantial harm” criteria and RA discretion.  
   - Why it matters: Explicit EJ language in the 2024 rule targeted disproportionate risks; claiming generic criteria suffice needs demonstration that protections for overburdened communities remain effective in practice.  
   - Practical question to ask EPA: Explain how removing EJ-specific provisions will not reduce attention to disproportionate exposures, and describe operational safeguards to ensure consistent consideration across Regions.

If you file comments, prioritize requests for concrete evidence on the duration choice, alignment of economic analysis with the proposed text, bounded estimates of foregone benefits, and technical support showing that removing climate/EJ language will not reduce protection in practice.
