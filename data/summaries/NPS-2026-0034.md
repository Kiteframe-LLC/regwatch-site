## What This Document Does
- Proposes amendments to 36 CFR Part 13 governing hunting and trapping in Alaska national preserves.
- Removes or revises provisions from prior NPS rules (2015, 2017, 2024), including deleting a regulatory definition of “Furbearer” and revising the definition of “Trapping.”
- Restores greater alignment between federal preserve rules and Alaska state wildlife regulations, allowing hunting and trapping subject to federal law and nondiscretionary, non-conflicting state law.
- Offers statutory and policy justifications (ANILCA, Executive Order 14153) for the changes in the preamble.

## Why It Matters
- The rule changes shift management from some prior federal restrictions toward greater reliance on state wildlife regulation—affecting how activities like bear baiting and trapping are treated on federal preserves.
- The agency bases important safety and ecological conclusions on historical management experience and statutory interpretation rather than presented empirical analyses.
- The preamble acknowledges this is a reversal of regulatory posture from earlier NPS rules, which triggers the need for a reasoned explanation and evidence to support the change.
- Missing analytical materials (no environmental analysis, economic analysis, monitoring data, or alternatives analysis visible in the provided materials) create opportunities for commenters to request the record needed for robust review.

## Who Is Affected
- Hunters and trappers who use Alaska national preserves.
- Alaska state wildlife agencies and local communities that rely on state regulations and subsistence regimes.
- NPS managers and law enforcement responsible for interpreting and enforcing preserve rules where federal and state law intersect.
- Stakeholders concerned with public safety, wildlife conservation, and subsistence rights under ANILCA.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Issue: Sufficiency of empirical evidence for safety and ecological claims  
   - Why it matters: The preamble relies on “experience managing public uses” to assert no public safety concerns and to justify removing restrictions (e.g., related to bear baiting). Without supporting data, the factual basis for rollback may be weak.  
   - Practical question to ask: Please provide the monitoring datasets, incident reports, and analysis that support the claim that authorized harvest practices have not presented public safety or ecological concerns.

2. Issue: Adequacy of alternatives analysis and consideration of partial or site-specific approaches  
   - Why it matters: The rule appears to be a broad rollback without documented evaluation of intermediate options (e.g., partial rollbacks, permit systems, site-specific restrictions). Administrative review expects discussion of reasonable alternatives.  
   - Practical question to ask: What alternatives were considered, and can the agency provide comparative analysis (feasibility, impacts) of partial or targeted regulatory approaches?

3. Issue: Clarity and enforcement where federal rules defer to state law  
   - Why it matters: Aligning preserve rules with state law could create enforcement ambiguity where state and federal interpretations or application differ. Clear enforcement responsibilities are important for compliance and public safety.  
   - Practical question to ask: How will NPS enforcement operate when state regulations apply on preserves, and what guidance will be provided to resolve conflicts or interpretive differences?

4. Issue: Justification for reversing prior NPS positions (policy reversal)  
   - Why it matters: The agency acknowledges a change from prior rules (2015, 2017, 2024). Under administrative law, reversals require a reasoned explanation showing why prior factual findings or policy choices are no longer valid.  
   - Practical question to ask: Please explain specifically why earlier factual findings or policy rationales no longer apply and provide evidence supporting the change in position.

5. Issue: Removal/revision of key definitions (e.g., “Trapping,” deletion of “Furbearer”)  
   - Why it matters: Removing definitions can increase ambiguity for regulated parties and enforcement staff, particularly if state and federal terms differ. Definitions affect scope and application of rules.  
   - Practical question to ask: Provide examples of how the revised definition of “Trapping” and deletion of “Furbearer” will operate in practice and any enforcement guidance that will accompany the change.
