# Public Summary: FTC-2026-0265-0001

_Source file: `summaries/FTC-2026-0265-0001.md`_
_Detected type: `NPRM`_

## What This Document Does
- Proposes to revise/modernize the FTC’s rule covering “prenotification” negative‑option plans (the NPRM seeks comment on updating the existing Rule).
- Relies largely on consumer complaints, enforcement actions, and the prior rulemaking record as the factual foundation.
- Does not, in the materials provided, include quantified harm estimates, datasets, cost‑benefit analysis, or a structured alternatives analysis in the public docket.
- Follows a prior rulemaking attempt that was vacated by a court for failing to include required analysis; this NPRM appears to be a rebuild using the earlier record and complaint/enforcement anecdotes.

## Why It Matters
- A rule change could affect how businesses structure negative‑option offers and how consumers are notified and can cancel them.
- The NPRM’s apparent analytical gaps—no disclosed complaint breakdown, no economic impact or alternatives evaluation—create procedural risk: a court could invalidate the final rule if the agency does not remedy the previous deficiencies.
- If the Rule remains narrowly focused on prenotification plans while the market mainly uses other negative‑option models (e.g., subscriptions, auto‑renewals), the Rule could miss the primary sources of consumer harm, reducing its effectiveness.
- The current framing shapes what evidence commenters can provide; targeted comments can force the agency to supply missing data or justify scope choices.

## Who Is Affected
- Consumers who encounter negative‑option marketing (especially those in prenotification plans).
- Businesses that offer prenotification negative‑option plans and potentially firms that might reclassify offers to avoid coverage.
- Regulators and courts evaluating whether the agency satisfied procedural requirements after the earlier vacatur.
- Parties interested in broader negative‑option practices (subscriptions, trials, auto‑renewal) that the NPRM may exclude.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Scope–Harm Mismatch
   - Why it matters: The NPRM limits coverage to prenotification plans but the record does not show that prenotification plans are the dominant source of current consumer harm.
   - Practical question to ask: Please provide complaint and enforcement data broken down by negative‑option category (prenotification vs. auto‑renewals vs. trials vs. other) and explain why the Rule targets prenotification plans rather than the types accounting for the most complaints.

2. Reuse of a Vacated Record / Analytical Adequacy
   - Why it matters: A prior version of the Rule was vacated for lack of required analysis; simply reusing the prior record without adding missing economic and alternatives analysis risks repeat vacatur.
   - Practical question to ask: How does the agency’s current analysis correct the deficiencies identified by the court in the vacatur decision? Provide any new or updated analyses and explain how they satisfy applicable procedural requirements.

3. Data Transparency and Evidence Quality
   - Why it matters: The NPRM cites “thousands of complaints” and enforcement cases but the docket lacks the underlying complaint datasets, trend analyses, or causal links to harms.
   - Practical question to ask: Publish the complaint dataset(s) (with appropriate privacy protections), describe how complaints were classified, and provide summary statistics and trend analysis that link complaints to actual consumer harm.

4. Missing Alternatives Analysis
   - Why it matters: The NPRM does not present structured alternatives (e.g., enforcement‑only approach, expanding coverage beyond prenotification, tiered disclosure/regulatory approaches), which the Administrative Procedure Act and precedent expect the agency to consider.
   - Practical question to ask: Provide a comparative analysis of feasible alternatives, including expected benefits, costs, and enforceability, and explain why the proposed scope and requirements are preferred.

5. Regulatory Incentives and Avoidance Risk
   - Why it matters: Narrow rule wording can create incentives for firms to redesign offers to fall outside the Rule without reducing consumer harm (e.g., shifting to non‑covered subscription models).
   - Practical question to ask: Assess and disclose the potential for regulatory arbitrage and describe safeguards (definitions, functional tests) the agency will use to prevent easy avoidance.
