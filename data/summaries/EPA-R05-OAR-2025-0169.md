## What This Document Does
- EPA proposes to approve part of Wisconsin's Moderate ozone SIP package for three Wisconsin areas under the 2015 ozone standard: Kenosha County, the Milwaukee area, and Sheboygan County.
- The proposed approvals cover five main pieces: the `2017` base-year emissions inventory, the `15%` reasonable-further-progress demonstration, the `2023` motor-vehicle emissions budgets, the motor-vehicle inspection and maintenance program certification, and the nonattainment new source review certification.
- EPA is also starting the transportation-conformity adequacy process for the `2023` budgets. If EPA later finds those budgets adequate, transportation agencies will have to use them when testing whether future plans and projects conform to the SIP.
- EPA is not deciding everything in Wisconsin's Moderate-area package here. The notice says the attainment demonstration, RACM demonstration, and contingency measures will be handled in a separate action.

## Why It Matters
- This is a partial approval with real practical consequences. Even though EPA is postponing some of the most outcome-oriented issues, the current notice would still approve the inventories and progress demonstration that support the plan, plus the budgets that can shape transportation conformity.
- The rule matters because southeastern Wisconsin ozone problems are not just a simple local-emissions story. The ingested Kenosha supporting plan describes a complicated Lake Michigan setting where transported pollution and lake-breeze dynamics can drive high ozone along the shoreline. That means commenters have a fair basis to ask whether EPA has explained clearly enough how much the approved local planning elements can accomplish on their own.
- The strongest concern is record clarity. EPA likely has substantial technical support behind this proposal, but the supplied review package surfaces the Kenosha support much more clearly than the Milwaukee and Sheboygan support, while still proposing area-wide approvals.
- The split structure also matters. EPA is asking the public to comment now on inventories, RFP, budgets, I/M, and NNSR, while saying attainment, RACM, and contingency measures will come later. That may be administratively normal enough, but it can make it harder for ordinary commenters to see the whole planning picture in one place.

## Who Is Affected
- Residents of shoreline and near-shore communities in the three Wisconsin ozone nonattainment areas, because the proposal affects the planning framework for ozone control.
- Metropolitan planning organizations, state transportation agencies, and federal transportation agencies, because the `2023` budgets may become the conformity benchmarks they must use.
- Facilities that may need permits under Wisconsin's ozone-related permitting program, because EPA is relying on the existing SIP-approved NNSR program as satisfying Moderate-area requirements.
- State and local air regulators, because this action would lock in EPA's approval of several planning elements while leaving other Moderate-area obligations for later.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. EPA should explain the split action more clearly
   - Why it matters: The notice approves several important planning elements now but postpones attainment, RACM, and contingency measures.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Why is it reasonable to approve these pieces now, and could the later action change the practical meaning of the approvals proposed here?

2. EPA should surface more Milwaukee- and Sheboygan-specific support
   - Why it matters: The supplied support is much more visible for Kenosha than for the other two areas, even though EPA is proposing all three approvals together.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Will EPA provide a clearer summary of the Milwaukee and Sheboygan technical basis so the public does not have to infer those areas from Kenosha alone?

3. EPA should provide a plain-language derivation of the `2023` budgets
   - Why it matters: Once found adequate, the budgets will affect transportation conformity decisions.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: What are the main assumptions and calculation steps behind each area's `2023` VOC and NOx budget?

4. EPA should explain how prior I/M and NNSR approvals map to the current Moderate obligations
   - Why it matters: EPA is relying on existing approved programs rather than presenting new program text in this action.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: How do the earlier approved I/M and NNSR programs match the revised nonattainment boundaries and current Moderate-area requirements?

5. EPA should discuss transport limits more directly
   - Why it matters: The supporting plan emphasizes regional transport and Lake Michigan meteorology, which can limit what local measures alone can do.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: How does EPA account for the transport-heavy nature of these ozone problems when deciding that the approved planning elements are sufficient for this stage?
