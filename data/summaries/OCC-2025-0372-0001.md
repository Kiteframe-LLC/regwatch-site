# Public Summary: OCC-2025-0372-0001

_Source file: `summaries/OCC-2025-0372-0001.md`_
_Detected type: `NPRM`_

## What This Document Does
- Proposes OCC regulations to implement parts of the GENIUS Act for payment stablecoins within OCC jurisdiction.
- Establishes rules and supervisory expectations on issuance, reserve composition/diversification, custody, applications/registrations, and capital for covered issuers.
- Presents two alternative approaches for reserve diversification: a principles-based requirement with an optional quantitative safe harbor (Option A) versus mandatory quantitative requirements for all issuers (Option B).
- Declines to codify certain statutory provisions the OCC characterizes as “self‑executing.”
- Announces economic/regulatory analyses and estimates (including a $187 million+ annual private‑sector expenditure) in supporting materials referenced but not supplied in the provided record.
- Solicits public comment on calibration (percentages, maturities, size-based thresholds) and other design questions.

## Why It Matters
- Prudential and market impact: The rule aims to shape how payment stablecoins are issued and backed, which affects liquidity, monetization channels, and financial stability risks.
- Economic tradeoffs: The OCC asserts the rule will increase market capitalization of payment stablecoins by reducing legal uncertainty — a causal claim with major bearing on claimed benefits.
- Compliance and costs: Proposed quantitative rules and calibration choices could impose substantial private‑sector costs and operational burdens, including across multiple regulators.
- Legal and procedural risk: Several key analytical choices (baseline selection, threshold justification, treatment of self‑executing provisions) are under‑explained in the visible record — potential grounds for substantive comment or litigation.

## Who Is Affected
- OCC‑regulated banks and national bank affiliates that issue or sponsor payment stablecoins.
- Non‑OCC banks and nonbank entities that would be permitted to issue payment stablecoins under the rule.
- Custodians and reserve managers (entities holding reserve assets).
- Other federal and state financial regulators (coordination/overlap implications).
- Private‑sector participants that provide custody, settlement, or monetization services tied to stablecoin reserves.
- Consumers and payments ecosystem participants indirectly affected by changes in stablecoin availability and design.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Baseline selection (New York law)
   - Why it matters: The OCC uses New York stablecoin requirements as the baseline for non‑OCC issuers; baseline choice drives measured incremental costs and benefits.
   - Practical question to ask: Please provide the empirical rationale and data showing why New York law accurately represents the “without‑rule” counterfactual for likely issuers.

2. Causal claim that “clarity” will increase issuance
   - Why it matters: The OCC’s central benefit claim (certainty → increased issuance) underpins projected gains; if incorrect, the benefit case weakens.
   - Practical question to ask: Provide empirical evidence or modeling that regulatory clarity — rather than other constraints (economics, AML, capital, bank risk appetite) — is the principal barrier to issuance.

3. Reserve diversification calibration (Option A vs Option B)
   - Why it matters: Percentages, maturities, and safe‑harbor design determine prudential stringency and private costs; weak justification risks arbitrary line‑drawing.
   - Practical question to ask: Supply the quantitative analysis and sensitivity testing that support the proposed reserve percentages/maturity limits and explain how thresholds were chosen.

4. Treatment of “self‑executing” statutory provisions
   - Why it matters: Not codifying sections the OCC deems self‑executing may leave interpretation and implementation uncertainty for regulated parties.
   - Practical question to ask: Explain why omission of regulatory text will not create operational ambiguity, and provide examples of how agencies or firms should operationalize those provisions.

5. Cross‑regulator operational feasibility and conflict risk
   - Why it matters: Custody, reserve, and monetization requirements may overlap with other regulators’ duties; inconsistent obligations could create untenable compliance costs or gaps.
   - Practical question to ask: Describe anticipated coordination mechanisms, identify potential conflicts with other regulators’ rules, and provide analyses showing how those conflicts would be resolved.
