## What This Document Does

* EPA proposes to approve Illinois' regional haze plan for the second implementation period and, in the same action, approve visibility-related portions of older Illinois infrastructure SIP submissions for the 2012 PM2.5 and 2015 ozone standards.
* The proposal says Illinois used a screening method based on emissions divided by distance to Class I areas (`Q/d`) to decide which sources needed deeper review.
* Illinois screened down to 30 units, excluded retired units and some units with fuel switches or existing enforceable controls, and then performed four-factor analyses for nine units at Prairie State, Southern Illinois Power Cooperative Marion, Rain CII Carbon, Holcim/Lafarge Grand Chain, and Archer Daniels Midland.
* EPA also says projected 2028 visibility conditions for the affected Class I areas are below both adjusted and unadjusted uniform-rate-of-progress glidepaths.

## Why It Matters

* Regional haze plans determine whether major industrial and power-sector sources must add new pollution controls to improve visibility in national parks and wilderness areas.
* This proposal is important because it is largely an approval action. EPA is saying Illinois has already done enough, or enough is already enforceable, for this planning period.
* For commenters, the leverage point is explanation quality. The docket has a real technical spine, but EPA still needs to show why its screening thresholds, existing-control findings, and cost-effectiveness calls are persuasive.
* If those judgments are thinly explained, commenters can push EPA to add analysis, sensitivity testing, and clearer comparisons before finalizing the approval.

## Who Is Affected

* Illinois power plants, industrial boilers, kilns, and similar sources that were screened for regional haze review.
* Owners and operators of units treated as already adequately controlled under Illinois' existing standards and permits.
* Facilities that received four-factor analyses, especially Prairie State, Southern Illinois Power Cooperative Marion, Rain CII Carbon, Holcim/Lafarge Grand Chain, and Archer Daniels Midland.
* People and organizations concerned with visibility at affected Class I areas in multiple states.
* Environmental groups, nearby communities, and technical commenters with expertise in source controls, emissions trends, and air-quality modeling.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)

* Issue: The proposal states the `Q/d` screening thresholds, but it does not clearly explain why those cutoffs are the right ones.
Why it matters: The thresholds determine which sources get a full reasonable-progress review. If the cutoff is underexplained, important sources may have been left out without a transparent reason.
Question to ask: What sensitivity analysis did EPA or Illinois perform to show that modestly different `Q/d` thresholds would not identify additional meaningful sources?

* Issue: The record gives cost-per-ton figures for several control options, but the decision rule for calling them not necessary is only lightly explained.
Why it matters: Cost-effectiveness is central to rejecting new controls. Commenters need to know whether EPA used a consistent benchmark or source-specific balancing framework.
Question to ask: What comparative benchmark or policy logic did EPA use to conclude that the listed control costs are too high to require for reasonable progress?

* Issue: Several units are treated as already effectively controlled based on existing standards and emissions history.
Why it matters: That can be reasonable, but only if recent unit-level performance really shows stable future control. Annual averages alone may hide variability or worsening conditions.
Question to ask: Can EPA provide recent unit-specific emissions-rate and compliance data showing why existing controls at Baldwin, Newton, Powerton, and Kincaid are sufficient through 2028?

* Issue: EPA says projected 2028 visibility conditions are below both adjusted and unadjusted glidepaths, but the public-facing uncertainty discussion is thin in the proposal text.
Why it matters: The favorable modeling outcome is a major support for approval. If that outcome depends on assumptions that are not easy to see, commenters should ask EPA to spell them out.
Question to ask: What are the key sensitivity or uncertainty drivers in the 2028 modeling, and how would less favorable assumptions affect the approval analysis?

* Issue: The proposal also approves visibility-related portions of older infrastructure SIP submissions, but the bridge from the regional haze record to those approvals is brief.
Why it matters: EPA should explain exactly how the current technical record resolves the separate visibility-prong obligations for the 2012 PM2.5 and 2015 ozone submissions.
Question to ask: What specific analysis in this docket supports the section 110(a)(2)(D)(i)(II) approvals, apart from the general regional haze discussion?

## Evidence Quotes

* “The EPA proposes to find that the Illinois regional haze SIP revision for the second implementation period meets the applicable statutory and regulatory requirements.”
* “Illinois EPA initially set a unit-level Q/d threshold of four to begin identifying point source emission units for potentially cost-effective control additions.”
* “This resulted in a list of 30 units, accounting for 71.4 percent of all emissions of SO2, NOX, NH3, and PM2.5 from units with Q/d greater than one in Illinois, including all the largest sources.”
* “Illinois EPA determined that additional control measures are not necessary to make reasonable progress in the second implementation period and that pursuing additional emission reductions through new emission control equipment or emissions limitations is not cost-effective.”
* “Illinois EPA received and responded to comments from FLMs and the public.”
