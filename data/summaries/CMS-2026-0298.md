# Public Summary: CMS-2026-0298-0001

_Source file: `summaries/CMS-2026-0298-0001.md`_
_Detected type: `NPRM`_

## What This Document Does
- Proposes Medicare Conditions of Participation that would require Medicare-participating hospitals to procure PPE and certain essential medicines sourced domestically (a “Made in America” procurement requirement).
- Frames the requirement as necessary to “ensure safety” and strengthen supply-chain security for the health system.
- Implements the requirement through hospital participation conditions rather than direct market controls.

## Why It Matters
- Affects hospital operations, supplier markets, and patient access to critical supplies during surges.
- The record provided is thin: the NPRM (as reviewed) lacks visible empirical support—no supply-chain data, cost modeling, capacity analysis, or alternatives evaluation are apparent in the docket.
- Without clear evidence and analysis, the rule risks unintended harms: higher costs, supplier concentration, quality variability, and possible shortages if domestic capacity cannot meet demand in crises.
- Procedural and legal risk: weak explanatory record and unaddressed alternatives increase the chance of successful challenge under the Administrative Procedure Act.

## Who Is Affected
- Medicare-participating hospitals (explicitly covered by the proposal).
- Medical suppliers and manufacturers that would supply domestically sourced PPE and medications.
- Patients and communities if hospital capacity or supply availability is affected.
- Taxpayers and purchasers if prices rise due to reduced competition or constrained supply.
- Unknown from provided text: detailed lists of specific product categories, compliance thresholds, phase-in timelines, or certification processes.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1) Lack of empirical basis for the central safety claim
- Why it matters: The rule’s core justification is that domestic sourcing improves supply-chain security and patient safety, but the record does not show quantified linkage or outcomes evidence.
- Practical question to ask: Provide the datasets and analyses showing how domestic-only or domestic-preferred sourcing measurably improved availability of PPE/essential medicines during past surges compared to diversified sourcing.

2) Absence of alternatives analysis
- Why it matters: The NPRM does not evaluate feasible, less-disruptive options (e.g., partial domestic thresholds, strategic stockpiles, supplier diversification, allied-country sourcing).
- Practical question to ask: Explain why partial sourcing targets, stockpiling, or reciprocal allied sourcing were rejected and provide comparative cost/benefit estimates.

3) Missing cost and hospital-impact modeling
- Why it matters: Mandates can raise procurement costs, which may reduce hospital service capacity or shift resources away from care.
- Practical question to ask: Provide projected cost impacts on hospitals (annual and per-patient), including sensitivity to price increases and supplier consolidation.

4) Feasibility and capacity concerns for domestic supply
- Why it matters: If domestic production cannot scale for surge demand, the requirement could worsen shortages or force lower-quality substitutes.
- Practical question to ask: Provide domestic production capacity estimates, surge ramp timelines, and plans to certify quality and reliability at required scale.

5) Scope and statutory authority questions
- Why it matters: Using Medicare participation conditions to effect industrial/supply-chain policy may exceed the program’s intended regulatory scope and invite legal challenge.
- Practical question to ask: Cite statutory authority and explain how this procurement mandate is a proper use of Medicare Conditions of Participation rather than an industrial policy requiring different statutory authorization.
