## What This Document Does
- FSIS is not proposing a finished rule yet. This document is an `ANPRM` asking whether the agency should change how it defines establishment size for meat, poultry, and egg-product plants.
- The current HACCP categories date to `1996`: `very small` means fewer than `10` employees or under `$2.5 million` in annual sales, `small` means `10-499` employees, and `large` means `500+` employees.
- FSIS says it already uses other size concepts in practice, especially production-volume thresholds for some sampling, labeling, and program purposes.
- The notice asks whether size should be measured by employee count, sales, production volume, slaughter volume, corporate ownership, or some combination.
- It also asks whether FSIS should create more categories, whether it should align with SBA standards, and how it could collect and verify size information without imposing too much burden.

## Why It Matters
- These labels are not just descriptive. FSIS uses size classifications in regulatory analysis, phased compliance, reduced-fee eligibility, sampling frequency decisions, and small-business assistance.
- That means a new definition could shift who gets regulatory flexibility, who gets treated as genuinely small, and how inspection-related burdens are distributed.
- The strongest part of the notice is that it identifies a real problem: one old set of categories may not fit all the jobs FSIS wants size definitions to do. A plant with `11` employees and one with `499` employees both count as `small` today, even though they may have very different resources.
- The weaker part is that FSIS does not yet explain how it will choose among competing metrics. The notice asks broad questions, but it does not give a clear decision framework for balancing simplicity, fairness, consistency with other federal programs, and ease of verification.
- This matters for comments because the record could become dominated by commenters with the best proprietary data unless FSIS makes it easier for smaller establishments to show how different classification systems would affect them.

## Who Is Affected
- Small, very small, and mid-sized meat, poultry, and egg-product establishments subject to FSIS oversight.
- Establishments that are locally small in headcount or output but affiliated with a larger parent company or network.
- Independent processors, slaughter facilities, and mixed-operation plants that may be classified differently depending on whether FSIS emphasizes employees, revenue, or production volume.
- USDA and other agencies that may rely on FSIS size labels for grants, assistance, contracting, or related program decisions.
- Consumers and regional food markets indirectly, if size definitions affect which facilities face lighter burdens or qualify for support.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
- **FSIS has not said whether one size definition should serve every agency purpose.**
Why it matters: Economic analysis, sampling, grants, and fee treatment may need different proxies. One all-purpose label could fit some uses badly.
Question a commenter can ask: Should FSIS use purpose-specific size definitions instead of forcing one classification system to govern every regulatory and assistance function?

- **Ownership and corporate support are important, but the notice is thin on administrability.**
Why it matters: A plant can look small on paper while benefiting from a large firm's capital, legal support, or market access. But affiliation rules can be hard to verify and easy to dispute.
Question a commenter can ask: What specific ownership or support indicators would FSIS use, and how would it verify them without creating a high-burden reporting system?

- **FSIS should show how candidate systems would reclassify real establishments.**
Why it matters: The notice discusses several metrics, but commenters cannot see how many plants would move categories under each one.
Question a commenter can ask: Can FSIS publish scenario tables comparing current HACCP categories with volume-based and hybrid ownership-sensitive systems across the current establishment population?

- **The ANPRM does not explain how future thresholds would be chosen.**
Why it matters: Even if commenters agree on the right variables, the line-drawing itself can be arbitrary unless FSIS explains what makes a breakpoint defensible.
Question a commenter can ask: What criteria will FSIS use to choose threshold cutoffs and to update them over time so they do not become outdated again?

- **Small commenters may be disadvantaged if FSIS relies on complex data requests.**
Why it matters: Large firms and trade groups may have internal datasets; small establishments may only have operating experience and limited records.
Question a commenter can ask: Will FSIS provide simple templates, public datasets, or example submissions so smaller establishments can respond meaningfully without proprietary analytics?
