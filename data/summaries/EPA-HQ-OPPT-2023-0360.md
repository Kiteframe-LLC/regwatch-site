## What This Document Does

* EPA proposes to move the deadline for a one-time TSCA section 8(d) health-and-safety reporting rule from May 22, 2026 to May 21, 2027.
* The underlying rule, finalized in December 2024, requires manufacturers and importers of 16 listed chemicals to submit certain unpublished health and safety studies to EPA.
* This new proposal does not change the list of chemicals or the basic reporting framework. It changes timing only.
* EPA says the extra year is needed because it is reconsidering the scope of the existing rule and does not want companies preparing submissions under a rule that may soon be revised.
* The supplied record also includes extracts from the earlier economic analyses for the 2024 rule, which show the rule was expected to cover 161 firms and 299 sites, with 71 firms expected to submit studies.

## Why It Matters

* This is a delay proposal, but it still matters because it postpones when EPA receives unpublished toxicity and safety studies that may inform TSCA section 6 work on these chemicals.
* EPA's basic theory is straightforward: if the agency may soon revise the reporting rule, it may be wasteful to force companies to keep preparing for a deadline that could soon change.
* The record is thinner on the next step in the argument. EPA says the current deadline is “infeasible,” but the proposal does not clearly describe what scope changes are under consideration or why they require a full extra year.
* That creates a useful comment opportunity. A commenter does not need to oppose all deadline relief to ask EPA for a more specific explanation, a comparison with narrower alternatives, and a clearer discussion of public-interest costs from delayed reporting.
* EPA also asks for comment on “reliance interests,” which signals that the agency knows this is an administrative-law issue. That is a practical opening for commenters to ask how the agency is weighing firms that prepared to comply, firms that sought more time, and members of the public who want the data sooner.

## Who Is Affected

* Manufacturers and importers of the 16 listed chemicals covered by the 2024 TSCA section 8(d) rule.
* Companies that may already be searching files, reviewing studies, handling confidential-business-information claims, or planning submissions.
* EPA program offices that expected to receive additional health and safety studies for these chemicals.
* Workers, downstream users, researchers, advocates, and communities interested in timely chemical-risk information.
* Trade groups and technical commenters who can speak to compliance burden, timing, study inventories, and the consequences of delay.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)

* Issue: EPA says a future scope-modification rulemaking would make the current deadline infeasible, but it does not clearly describe those contemplated scope changes.
Why it matters: That is the main reason offered for another delay. Without more detail, the public cannot easily test whether the problem is real, large, or urgent enough to justify a full year.
Question to ask: What specific scope changes is EPA considering, and how would each one interfere with compliance under the current May 22, 2026 deadline?

* Issue: The proposal picks a one-year extension, but the visible record does not explain why one year is the right amount.
Why it matters: A shorter extension, phased deadline, or narrower relief might reduce burden while still getting some studies to EPA sooner.
Question to ask: What alternatives to a full one-year extension did EPA consider, and why were they rejected?

* Issue: EPA says the extension provides “regulatory certainty” and avoids “duplicative or potentially inconsistent reporting,” but the proposal does not quantify those expected benefits.
Why it matters: That claim may be true, but commenters need to know whether firms are mostly at the planning stage or have already done substantial work that will not actually be avoided.
Question to ask: What compliance tasks has EPA identified as likely to be duplicated or wasted absent the extension, and for how many firms?

* Issue: EPA asks for comment on reliance interests, but the proposal does not summarize what reliance interests are already visible in the docket.
Why it matters: Agencies usually need to weigh reliance when changing course. The public record is stronger if EPA explains what it already knows instead of relying only on fresh comments.
Question to ask: What reliance interests has EPA already identified from prior extension requests, implementation planning, or earlier docket submissions?

* Issue: The proposal says little about the cost of delaying receipt of health and safety studies.
Why it matters: Delay can reduce private burden, but it can also postpone information relevant to chemical oversight and public understanding of risk.
Question to ask: How will delaying submissions until May 21, 2027 affect EPA's ongoing TSCA section 6 work for the covered chemicals?

## Evidence Quotes

* “The proposed extension is intended to delay compliance with this one-time reporting rule during EPA's ongoing reconsideration of the rule.”
* “EPA is considering a proposal to modify the scope of the TSCA section 8(d) Health and Safety Data Reporting Rule.”
* “Such a rulemaking would make infeasible the current reporting deadline of May 22, 2026.”
* “Rather than requiring businesses to prepare submissions under a rule that is in the process of being revised, this action provides regulatory certainty and prevents duplicative or potentially inconsistent reporting.”
* “EPA requests comment on the proposed reporting timeline and is soliciting comments on the nature and extent of any reliance interests that may have arisen from the rule.”
