# Public Summary: ATBCB-2026-0001-0001

_Source file: `summaries/ATBCB-2026-0001-0001.md`_
_Detected type: `NPRM`_

## What This Document Does
- Proposes regulatory standards to require universal (adult) changing tables in certain airport restrooms tied to federal grant eligibility.
- Frames technical choices the agency is considering, including:
  - Incorporating by reference section 613.4 of ICC A117.1 (an industry accessibility standard).
  - Requiring the changing-table height to be “continuously adjustable.”
  - Requiring that certain airport rooms “contain certain accessible elements” (scope not yet specified).
- Seeks public input at an early stage (ANPRM-style); the docket contains no supporting data or cost analyses in the record provided.

## Why It Matters
- Accessibility and safety: The agency frames the change as addressing situations where “individuals with disabilities will often have to lie on the floor,” which it says “risks injury to both individuals with disabilities and caregivers.”
- Compliance and funding: Airports designated as medium and large hubs that receive federal grants would likely need to meet whatever standard is adopted to remain eligible for funds.
- Design and cost consequences: Technical specs (height ranges, adjustability, room elements) will affect manufacturing, installation costs, airport space planning, and retrofit feasibility.
- Rulemaking risk: The proposal appears to rest on limited evidence in the current docket. Absent data on prevalence, injuries, cost, and engineering feasibility, the agency’s choices may be vulnerable to comments or litigation challenging the sufficiency of the administrative record.

## Who Is Affected
- People with disabilities and their caregivers who need adult changing facilities.
- Medium and large hub airports that receive federal grants.
- Airport owners/operators and designers responsible for restroom and room layouts.
- Manufacturers and suppliers of changing tables and related equipment.
- Disability rights and accessibility advocacy organizations.
- Federal grant program administrators who oversee compliance.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1) Problem Quantification Gap
- Why it matters: The agency’s safety and need claims are based on anecdotal framing. Without prevalence or injury data, the rule’s scope and technical stringency may be over- or under-inclusive.
- Practical question to ask: Provide or request data showing how often people are forced to use floors in airports, and any injury statistics for users or caregivers.

2) Choice to Incorporate ICC A117.1 Section 613.4
- Why it matters: Incorporating a private standard without comparative analysis can lock in design choices that may not meet federal policy goals or current needs.
- Practical question to ask: Ask the agency to publish a side‑by‑side comparison of ICC §613.4 with alternative standards and explain why incorporation (versus a federal standard or hybrid) best fulfills statutory objectives.

3) Justification for Continuous Adjustability
- Why it matters: Continuous adjustability may improve usability but could increase cost, complexity, and maintenance burdens; it needs technical and cost justification.
- Practical question to ask: Request engineering studies, maintenance-cost estimates, and usability testing across disability types to support continuous adjustability as a required feature.

4) Scope and Room Requirements
- Why it matters: The docket suggests rooms “would likely contain certain accessible elements” but does not specify which rooms or elements; unclear scope affects compliance costs and planning.
- Practical question to ask: Ask the agency to define which rooms are covered (e.g., single-user restrooms, family restrooms, gateside facilities) and provide rationale for that scope.

5) Missing Alternatives and Implementation Flexibility
- Why it matters: The record omits hybrid approaches (ICC baseline plus federal tweaks), tiered requirements by airport size, phased implementation, or performance-based options that could reduce costs while ensuring access.
- Practical question to ask: Suggest alternative regulatory frameworks (tiered, phased, or performance-based) and ask the agency to evaluate them against prescriptive options.

6) Cost and Feasibility Analysis Missing
- Why it matters: Airports differ in space constraints; without cost and spatial feasibility studies, requirements could be impractical or disproportionately costly.
- Practical question to ask: Request per‑installation cost estimates, spatial/engineering feasibility analyses, and data on retrofit constraints in typical airport layouts.
