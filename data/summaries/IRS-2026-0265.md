## What This Document Does

- The IRS and Treasury propose to delete an existing tax-reporting rule that treats certain partnership related-party basis adjustment transactions as "transactions of interest."
- If finalized, the proposal would remove the reporting obligations tied to that rule for transaction participants and material advisors.
- The agencies also say they intend to let people treat the repeal as effective back to January 14, 2025, so the rule could be treated as if it never took effect.
- The NPRM's main justification is burden reduction. IRS estimates the current rule would otherwise generate about 10,000 reportable basis adjustments per year and about $163 million in annual filing burden across Forms 8886 and 8918.

## Why It Matters

- This is a deregulatory move with real practical effect. It would eliminate a disclosure tool the IRS had only recently finalized in January 2025.
- The agency gives a concrete paperwork-savings estimate, which is useful, but the record provided here is still fairly thin. There is no separate supporting attachment or visible methodology appendix showing how the 10,000-transaction estimate was built.
- The proposal also makes a stronger move than simple repeal: it says the rule may be treated as though it never took effect. That raises transition and record questions for anyone who already complied, prepared to comply, or relied on Notice 2025-23.
- Public comments in the cached docket split in familiar ways. Business-oriented commenters support repeal as a correction of an overbroad, burdensome reporting regime. Several individual commenters oppose repeal because they see the rule as a basic anti-abuse disclosure tool. The NPRM itself does not do much to reconcile those competing views with evidence.

## Who Is Affected

- Partnerships and partners involved in basis-adjustment transactions covered by the existing rule.
- Material advisors, including law and accounting firms, that would otherwise file Form 8918 disclosures.
- Small businesses and family-owned enterprises that may face compliance costs if the reporting rule remains in place.
- Tax administration and enforcement staff, because repeal would reduce information the IRS might otherwise use to spot potentially abusive basis-shifting structures.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)

- **The $163 million burden-reduction estimate may be hard to test from the public record.**
Why it matters: The headline case for repeal depends on assumptions about how many transactions would be reported, how many parties each transaction affects, and what each filing costs.
Question to ask: What tax-return years, filters, and sensitivity ranges did IRS use to estimate 10,000 affected basis adjustments and the average participant/advisor counts?

- **The proposal says burden likely exceeds benefits, but it does not say much about the benefits side.**
Why it matters: Repealing a reporting rule is easier to evaluate when the agency explains what compliance costs bought in return, such as enforcement leads or deterrence.
Question to ask: What information or compliance benefits did the agency expect from the original transaction-of-interest rule, and why are those benefits now judged smaller than the burden?

- **The NPRM does not seriously analyze narrower alternatives to full repeal.**
Why it matters: The criticism described in the notice could support a narrower fix, such as higher thresholds, tighter scope, or prospective-only relief, instead of wiping out the rule entirely.
Question to ask: Why did Treasury and IRS not analyze targeted revisions that would preserve reporting for the highest-risk transactions while easing burden on ordinary-course deals?

- **Retroactive treatment deserves more explanation.**
Why it matters: The proposal says affected parties may treat the rule as if it never took effect, which can change expectations for records, prior filings, and enforcement treatment.
Question to ask: How will the final rule handle parties that already complied, prepared disclosures, or changed behavior in response to the January 14, 2025 final rule and Notice 2025-23?

- "The Treasury Department and the IRS have determined that most participants would choose to report any basis adjustment over the numeric thresholds..."
