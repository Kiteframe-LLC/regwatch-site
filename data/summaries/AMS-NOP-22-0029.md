## What This Document Does
- Proposes four USDA organic substance-rule changes for crop and livestock production.
- Adds synthetic carbon dioxide for two crop uses: lowering irrigation-water pH and adjusting greenhouse/indoor air conditions.
- Renews sodium nitrate for limited fertilizer use, keeping the rule that it may supply no more than 20 percent of a crop’s total nitrogen need.
- Adds meloxicam as an allowed livestock pain-treatment drug, but only with a veterinarian’s order and at least double the FDA withdrawal period.
- Removes current numeric limits on synthetic methionine in organic poultry feed, leaving producers with more flexibility and less recordkeeping.
- Asks whether the irrigation-water carbon-dioxide listing should also be limited to byproduct-sourced gas, and whether meloxicam needs stricter limits.

## Why It Matters
- The rule expands what synthetic inputs can be used in organic production while also loosening one existing poultry-feed restriction. That directly affects how the organic program balances flexibility against limits on synthetic substances.
- The carbon-dioxide proposal is the sharpest policy choice. AMS relies on a byproduct/recycling rationale and on claims that natural alternatives are not realistically available, but the visible NPRM text gives limited support for those claims.
- The meloxicam proposal matters because animal-welfare benefits may depend on a compliance standard that is still somewhat vague in practice.
- The methionine proposal is deregulatory and may help poultry producers, but the visible record is mostly narrative on why the current caps are burdensome and why full removal is the right answer.
- AMS also says the rule will not significantly affect small entities, yet the supplied text gives little concrete cost support for that conclusion.

## Who Is Affected
- Organic crop producers using irrigation systems, alkaline water sources, or greenhouse/indoor growing environments.
- Organic livestock producers using painful procedures or treatment decisions where meloxicam may become relevant.
- Organic poultry producers who currently track synthetic methionine use across the life of a flock.
- Certifying agents, inspectors, and certification-review staff who will have to interpret source restrictions, withdrawal periods, and amended livestock-feed rules.
- Input suppliers and related businesses serving the organic market.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Carbon-dioxide source restriction is only partly aligned across the two new listings.
Why it matters: AMS proposes a byproduct-source limit for the greenhouse listing, but is still asking whether the irrigation-water listing should carry the same limit. If byproduct sourcing is part of the environmental justification, inconsistent treatment could be hard to defend.
Practical question a commenter can ask: Should both carbon-dioxide listings require byproduct sourcing, and if so, what records should producers and certifiers use to verify compliance?

2. Natural-alternative and market-availability claims are not well shown in the visible text.
Why it matters: The rule repeatedly says natural carbon dioxide and other alternatives are not commercially adequate, but the supplied NPRM does not show much market evidence.
Practical question a commenter can ask: What current evidence shows that natural carbon dioxide, sulfur-burner approaches, or other allowed inputs are insufficient for the proposed uses?

3. Meloxicam’s withdrawal standard may be hard to administer.
Why it matters: “At least twice that required by FDA” sounds clear in principle, but producers and certifiers may still need species-specific examples, especially for bovine meat and dairy uses.
Practical question a commenter can ask: Will AMS provide minimum withdrawal examples or species-specific benchmarks so certifiers can verify meloxicam use consistently?

4. Methionine-limit removal may need clearer limiting principles.
Why it matters: AMS argues the current caps are burdensome and can harm poultry nutrition, but the NPRM offers little visible discussion of what guardrails remain once the caps are gone.
Practical question a commenter can ask: After numeric caps are removed, what standards or compliance expectations will ensure methionine remains limited to nutritional necessity?

5. The small-entity conclusion is underexplained.
Why it matters: AMS says the rule will not significantly affect small entities, but small farms and certifiers may still face real costs tied to sourcing verification, veterinary orders, and revised compliance practices.
Practical question a commenter can ask: What burden estimates or field examples support the conclusion that small-entity impacts are not significant?

## Evidence Quotes
- “AMS agrees with both NOSB recommendations to add synthetic carbon dioxide to 7 CFR 205.601(a) and 205.601(j), as these uses meet the requirements for adding a synthetic substance to the National List under OFPA at 7 U.S.C. 6517(c)(1)(A).”
- “AMS also agrees with the NOSB's conclusions that allowed natural and synthetic alternatives to the specific petitioned uses of synthetic carbon dioxide are not commercially available.”
- “AMS requests comments about whether the proposed listing in 7 CFR 205.601(a) should include the same annotation limiting the sources of carbon dioxide proposed in § 205.601(j).”
- “The proposed annotation further requires that meloxicam may only be used in organic production by the lawful written order of a licensed veterinarian, and the withdrawal period must be at least twice that required by FDA.”
- “AMS welcomes comments on whether a specified minimum withdrawal period is necessary, and if so, what specific duration is appropriate for meat- and milk-producing bovine species.”
- “AMS agrees with the NOSB recommendation to allow methionine as an organic poultry feed additive without limits on the amount of methionine that may be added to organic poultry rations.”
- “AMS does not expect the economic impact on entities affected by this rulemaking to be significant.”
