# Public Summary: FDIC-2026-0265-0001

_Source file: `summaries/FDIC-2026-0265-0001.md`_

## What This Rule Does
- Proposes procedures requiring an insured State nonmember bank or State savings association to obtain FDIC approval before issuing payment stablecoins through a subsidiary.
- Implements the GENIUS Act statutory framework as the FDIC’s authority for the approval regime.
- Focus is on supervisory approval processes for bank-affiliated stablecoin issuance; the NPRM text excerpt does not detail the quantitative analyses or alternatives behind that choice.

## Why It Matters
- Prior approval is a significant supervisory control that affects how banks structure stablecoin activities and when they may enter the market.
- The rule shapes risk management expectations for reserve backing, liquidity for redemptions, and operational resilience for bank-affiliated stablecoins.
- Gaps in the NPRM’s presented analysis (alternatives, empirical risk modeling, technical coherence with blockchain settlement) raise potential legal and practical challenges, including:
  - regulatory arbitrage if non‑subsidiary entities are used to evade supervision;
  - uncertainty for banks and fintech partners about permissible business structures;
  - litigation risk under the Administrative Procedure Act if statutory interpretation or alternatives analysis is inadequate.

## Who Is Affected
- Primary: FDIC‑supervised insured State nonmember banks and State savings associations that would issue payment stablecoins through subsidiaries.
- Secondary/related: those subsidiaries, potential fintech partners, and market participants whose structuring choices (e.g., offshore affiliates, joint ventures) could be shaped by or attempt to avoid the approval requirement.
- Unknown from provided text: specific thresholds, timelines, or quantitative compliance obligations (e.g., reserve ratios, liquidity requirements).

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Statutory Basis for Prior FDIC Approval
   - Why it matters: The NPRM relies on the GENIUS Act, but the record excerpt does not quote the specific statutory text that authorizes prior approval of subsidiary-issued stablecoins. Clear statutory grounding affects rule defensibility.
   - Practical question to ask: Please cite the specific GENIUS Act provisions the FDIC interprets as authorizing prior approval for subsidiaries and explain the statutory text supporting that interpretation.

2. Choice of an Approval Regime versus Less‑Restrictive Alternatives
   - Why it matters: The NPRM favors prior approval but does not show why alternatives (e.g., notice/notification regime, activity reporting, pilot programs, capital/liquidity thresholds) were rejected. Alternatives could be less disruptive while addressing risks.
   - Practical question to ask: Explain why FDIC selected a prior-approval approach rather than alternatives such as an activity notice regime, targeted capital/liquidity requirements, or a pilot authorization.

3. Empirical and Quantitative Risk Analysis
   - Why it matters: The excerpt lacks reserve adequacy models, redemption liquidity stress analysis, or data supporting the asserted risks from stablecoins. Quantitative analysis underpins appropriate supervisory requirements.
   - Practical question to ask: Provide the empirical data and modeling (e.g., reserve adequacy, redemption stress tests) used to assess the liquidity and systemic risks of bank-affiliated payment stablecoins.

4. Scope and Boundary Risks (Regulatory Arbitrage)
   - Why it matters: The definition of “subsidiary issuance” may leave gaps usable to avoid oversight (offshore affiliates, joint ventures, fintech partnerships), undermining the rule’s objective.
   - Practical question to ask: How does the FDIC intend to address stablecoin issuance structures (e.g., affiliates, joint ventures, fintech partnerships, offshore entities) that could evade the subsidiary-approval framework?

5. Technical Coherence with Crypto Settlement and Operations
   - Why it matters: Blockchain-based settlement, reserve segregation, and redemption mechanics differ from conventional bank operations. The NPRM excerpt does not show technical analysis linking supervisory expectations to these mechanics.
   - Practical question to ask: Provide analysis of how proposed supervisory requirements will apply to blockchain settlement, custody of reserves, and redemption processes, and describe the operational standards expected.
