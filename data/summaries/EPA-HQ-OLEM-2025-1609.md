# Public Summary: EPA-HQ-OLEM-2025-1609-0001

## What This Document Does
- Proposes to treat recovered abandoned scrap tires as a categorical non‑waste fuel for combustion (e.g., in cement kilns).
- Expands the scope of the “established tire collection program” to include recovered tires, shifting regulatory treatment of those tires from waste-incineration rules toward fuel-combustion rules.
- Relies on EPA’s determinations about physical/chemical equivalence between recovered and collected tires and on the idea that certain recovery activities satisfy processing/legitimacy criteria.

## Why It Matters
- Regulatory regime shift: reclassification moves combustion of these tires from CAA §129 (waste incineration) to a fuel-combustion framework with different emission controls and permitting consequences.
- Public health and environmental risk hinge on whether recovered tires are truly equivalent to collected tires; contaminated or highly variable material could increase emissions or localized risks.
- Enforcement and compliance depend on clear definitions and measurable criteria for “recovered,” “processed,” and “managed as a valuable commodity.” Vague boundaries could create avoidance incentives (e.g., minimal recovery to evade stricter rules).
- Economic and operational incentives for the waste and cement industries could change (reduced processing costs, new fuel sources), with downstream impacts on state programs and facility practices.

## Who Is Affected
- Cement kilns and other combustion facilities that might use recovered tires as fuel.
- Waste management and remediation firms involved in tire recovery.
- State and local regulators responsible for implementing RCRA/Clean Air Act interfaces.
- Communities near tire piles and combustion facilities (public health, air quality).
- Manufacturers and markets for tire-derived fuel and processing services.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Issue: Claim of material equivalence between recovered and collected tires
   - Why it matters: The categorical reclassification rests on the premise that recovery removes relevant contamination and that recovered tires are “largely physically and chemically identical” to collected tires. If that is not true across sites, some recovered loads could cause higher emissions or bring contaminants into combustion streams.
   - Practical question to ask: What empirical datasets (sample size, locations, laboratory methods) demonstrate equivalence between recovered and collected tires, and can EPA publish the raw comparison data and statistical analysis?

2. Issue: Definition and verification of “recovered” and “managed as a valuable commodity”
   - Why it matters: Vague or flexible definitions create room for inconsistent interpretation and potential abuse (minimal recovery claimed to meet the standard).
   - Practical question to ask: Provide an operational definition and measurable criteria (e.g., chain‑of‑custody records, time-from-excavation limits, contaminant thresholds) that facilities and inspectors can use to verify a tire is “recovered” and “managed as a valuable commodity.”

3. Issue: Removal of a processing/threshold requirement
   - Why it matters: EPA proposes that recovery (removal + exclusion of contaminants) satisfies processing requirements and that additional processing is “unnecessary and unreasonable.” If processing materially reduces emissions or variability, removing the requirement could increase risk.
   - Practical question to ask: Submit the comparative emissions testing and risk analyses that justify eliminating processing requirements—show emissions profiles for minimally recovered, processed, and standard collected tires.

4. Issue: Lack of tiered or conditional alternatives
   - Why it matters: The rule appears categorical and collapses a gradient of contamination into a single determination. A tiered approach (e.g., by contamination level or required handling) could better match regulatory control to risk.
   - Practical question to ask: Has EPA considered and modeled tiered classification or facility-specific allowances, and if so, provide that analysis; if not, invite consideration of a conditional classification based on contaminant thresholds.

5. Issue: Enforcement and state consistency risks
   - Why it matters: Ambiguities can create inconsistent state implementation and inspection outcomes, and could incentivize reshaping of recovery practices to avoid stricter rules.
   - Practical question to ask: Describe how EPA expects states to implement and enforce the new classification, including recommended inspection protocols, recordkeeping, and criteria for interstate enforcement consistency.
