# Public Summary: OPM-2025-0107-0001

_Source file: `summaries/OPM-2025-0107-0001.md`_

## What This Rule Does
- Revises federal Reduction in Force (RIF) regulations to prioritize employee performance over tenure and length of service when ranking who is retained during downsizing.
- Establishes a numerical “performance credit” scoring method to differentiate employees for retention decisions.
- Grants agencies discretion to give additional weight to performance-related actions when determining retention rankings.
- Adjusts related placement and displacement programs and processes, including:
  - Reemployment Priority List (RPL)
  - Career Transition Assistance Plan (CTAP) / Interagency Career Transition Assistance Plan (ICTAP)
  - Transfer of function
  - Furlough procedures
- Interprets existing statutory factors (tenure, veterans’ preference, length of service, and efficiency/performance) by shifting greater weight to performance within the retention process.

## Why It Matters
- Determines who stays and who is separated in a RIF, directly affecting federal employees’ jobs and agencies’ capacity.
- Moves away from long-standing seniority-focused approaches; could change workforce composition and perceived fairness.
- Relies on performance ratings and related actions that may vary by agency and manager; without evidence of reliability, outcomes could be inconsistent.
- Expands agency discretion, which can increase flexibility but may also raise concerns about bias or politicization if not accompanied by safeguards.
- The provided record appears analytically thin (no supporting studies or modeling identified), raising questions about empirical support and litigation risk.

## Who Is Affected
- Federal executive agencies that conduct RIFs and must re-rank employees for retention.
- Federal employees subject to RIF procedures, across tenure groups and including those with veterans’ preference.
- Agency HR offices and selecting officials implementing performance-credit scoring.
- Employees and representatives engaged with RPL, CTAP, and ICTAP processes that may be modified by the rule.
- Labor organizations and employee advocates monitoring RIF fairness and compliance.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1) Empirical support for the core claims
- Why it matters: The rule asserts that emphasizing performance will retain higher performers but cites no modeling or data in the provided materials.
- Practical question: What analyses (e.g., simulations comparing current vs. proposed systems) show that the new scoring increases retention of top performers across agencies?

2) Reliability and consistency of performance ratings
- Why it matters: If ratings are inflated, inconsistent, or influenced by non-performance factors, heavier weighting could mis-sort employees in a RIF.
- Practical question: What evidence demonstrates that performance ratings and related actions are comparable and reliable across agencies and work units for RIF purposes?

3) Rating compression and minimal differentiation
- Why it matters: If most employees cluster at similar rating levels, the scoring method may not meaningfully distinguish among employees, undermining the rule’s goal.
- Practical question: Did OPM analyze federal rating distributions and, if so, how will the rule address rating compression to ensure real differentiation?

4) Scope of agency discretion and safeguards
- Why it matters: The rule allows agencies, in their “sole and exclusive discretion,” to give more weight to performance-related actions, which may invite uneven application.
- Practical question: What guardrails (e.g., standardized criteria, audits, documentation, or appeal channels) will prevent inconsistent or biased use of discretionary weighting?

5) Consideration of alternatives and calibration mechanisms
- Why it matters: The provided record indicates limited alternatives analysis; other options (e.g., hybrid weighting of performance and tenure, rating normalization) could reduce risks.
- Practical question: Which alternative approaches did OPM evaluate (and with what results), and why were normalization or hybrid models rejected?

6) Statutory balance among factors
- Why it matters: 5 U.S.C. 3502 identifies tenure, veterans’ preference, length of service, and efficiency/performance; changing relative weightings can raise interpretive questions.
- Practical question: How does OPM’s weighting approach align with 5 U.S.C. 3502’s framework, and what legal analysis supports the revised balance among required factors?
