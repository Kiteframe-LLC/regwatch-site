## What This Document Does
- Proposes regulations implementing the Section 45Z Clean Fuel Production Credit.
- Establishes definitions, eligibility rules, emissions methodologies, annual emissions-rate tables, PER (petroleum-equivalent rate) procedures, registration/certification, anti-stacking and anti-abuse rules, and claimant/attribution rules.
- Adds related amendments to §§6417, 6418 and certain excise-tax registration rules.
- Relies heavily on prior Treasury/IRS notices (e.g., Notice 2025-10/11) and named lifecycle models (e.g., GREET for non-SAF fuels; CORSIA-like methods for SAF).

## Why It Matters
- The rule’s boundary and methodological choices determine who can claim credits, how large credits are, and how projects are financed.
- Key choices (what counts as “fuel,” who is the “producer,” which emissions model sets baseline, how annual tables are applied, and the scope of anti-abuse language) shape market incentives and potential transactional structures.
- The preamble often explains choices at a high level but the supplied docket record is thin on standalone technical or quantitative support, leaving commenters room to seek clearer justification or alternatives.

## Who Is Affected
- Domestic producers of clean transportation fuels (RNG, CNG/LCNG, hydrogen, SAF, other non-SAF fuels).
- Taxpayers claiming or transferring the Section 45Z credit and related producer credits.
- Project developers, lenders and investors relying on predictable credit valuation.
- Excise-tax registrants and entities in multi-party production/sale chains (processors, compressors, title-holders).

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Electricity exclusion
   - Issue: The proposal states “fuel” would not include electricity.
   - Why it matters: Excluding electricity categorically removes potential electric-vehicle charging pathways and may affect projects that substitute electricity for liquid/gaseous fuels.
   - Practical question to ask: Please explain the legal and economic basis for a categorical exclusion of electricity and provide quantitative examples of foregone credit pathways or alternative treatments considered.

2. Gasoline as the gallon-equivalent baseline
   - Issue: The rule proposes using gasoline as the baseline for gallon-equivalency calculations for non-liquid fuels.
   - Why it matters: A single gasoline baseline may misstate equivalence for fuels that more closely substitute for diesel, jet fuel, or natural gas, altering credit amounts.
   - Practical question to ask: Provide comparative analysis showing why a gasoline baseline is preferable to fuel-specific or energy-content baselines, and supply sensitivity results.

3. RNG “producer” identity (processor vs. compressor/title-holder)
   - Issue: The rule treats the processor as the “producer” because it is “the most active participant.”
   - Why it matters: RNG commercialization often divides functions across entities; this definition affects who claims credits, creates risk of double claims, and could disrupt common commercial contracts.
   - Practical question to ask: Supply industry-structure analysis and examples covering multi-entity chains; propose clearer rules or attribution tests for split arrangements.

4. Rejection of emissions-table lock-in (construction-year vs. production-year)
   - Issue: Treasury declines stakeholder requests to allow taxpayers to lock in an emissions-rate table tied to construction-start year.
   - Why it matters: Projects financed under particular assumptions face financing and pricing risk if annual tables change between construction and production.
   - Practical question to ask: Provide rationale and impact analysis for rejecting a transition or limited lock-in safe harbor; if rejected, explain what alternatives reduce financing risk.

5. Broad anti-abuse (“primary purpose” / “wasteful”) standard
   - Issue: The rule bars credits where the primary purpose of production/sale is to obtain credits in a “wasteful” manner, using a facts-and-circumstances test.
   - Why it matters: Vague standards can deter ordinary commercial conduct and create uncertainty for taxpayers and administrators.
   - Practical question to ask: Request illustrative examples, objective criteria, and proposed safe harbors so ordinary commercial transactions are not chilled.

6. Producer need not own the qualified facility
   - Issue: The preamble states there is no statutory requirement that the producer own the facility.
   - Why it matters: This increases flexibility but raises questions about attribution, transfers, and potential overlapping claims.
   - Practical question to ask: Explain limits or conditions on non-owner producers claiming credits and provide examples to prevent double claims or abusive structures.
