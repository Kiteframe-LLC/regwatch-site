# Public Summary: EPA-HQ-OLEM-2025-0182-0001

_Source file: `summaries/EPA-HQ-OLEM-2025-0182-0001.md`_

## What This Rule Does
- Proposes to delete six sites and partially delete two sites from the Superfund National Priorities List (NPL).
- Bases the proposed deletions on site-specific documentation: Final Close-Out Reports (FCORs) and Partial Deletion Justifications (PDJs).
- States that EPA and the relevant states have determined “all appropriate response actions” under CERCLA have been completed at the areas proposed for deletion.
- Clarifies that deletion (full or partial) does not prevent EPA from taking future Superfund actions if conditions warrant.
- Invites public comment; the substantive technical evidence is in the individual site dockets.

## Why It Matters
- Signals that EPA considers cleanup objectives met and the remedy protective for the areas proposed for deletion.
- Can influence long-term oversight, monitoring, and resource allocation for the sites.
- Affects community confidence, redevelopment plans, and property transactions in and around the sites.
- Maintains the accuracy of the NPL by removing (or partially removing) sites that no longer require priority federal attention.
- Ensures that, while deletion is not a barrier to future action, there is a clear administrative record showing cleanup endpoints and ongoing stewardship.

## Who Is Affected
- Residents and businesses near the eight affected Superfund sites (six full deletions; two partial deletions).
- State environmental agencies that must concur with deletion decisions and may oversee ongoing controls.
- Potentially Responsible Parties (PRPs) and property owners subject to or relying on institutional controls.
- Local governments, planners, and developers considering land reuse in formerly contaminated areas.
- Public health advocates and community groups tracking long-term protectiveness.
- Note: Specific site locations and stakeholders are in the site dockets; they are not listed in the provided text.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1) Whether cleanup endpoints and verification are sufficiently documented
- Why it matters: Deletion hinges on the conclusion that “all appropriate response actions” are complete and protective; weak or unclear evidence risks premature deletion.
- Practical question: For each site, can EPA provide a clear summary table of contaminants of concern, cleanup targets, post-remediation results, and the data demonstrating targets were achieved?

2) Compliance with 40 CFR 300.425(e) deletion criteria for each site/area
- Why it matters: The NCP requires specific findings (e.g., no further response needed, state concurrence). The administrative record should map evidence to each criterion.
- Practical question: For each site and each partially deleted area, where in the FCOR/PDJ does EPA document satisfaction of every criterion in 40 CFR 300.425(e)?

3) Adequacy and duration of post-remediation monitoring
- Why it matters: Contamination rebound, plume migration, or new exposure pathways can emerge over time; monitoring plans and triggers are central to protectiveness.
- Practical question: What is the monitoring schedule, data review process, and specific threshold that would trigger maintenance, optimization, or reinitiation of response actions?

4) Strength and enforceability of institutional controls (ICs)
- Why it matters: Many remedies rely on land or groundwater use restrictions. IC failure can reintroduce exposure risks after deletion.
- Practical question: What ICs are in place for each site, who enforces them, how is compliance tracked over time, and what contingency steps are planned for IC noncompliance?

5) Clarity and technical basis for partial deletion boundaries
- Why it matters: Partial deletion removes only discrete areas from the NPL; boundaries must reflect contaminant distribution and exposure potential.
- Practical question: For the two partial deletions, can EPA provide maps and rationale showing boundary selection, residual contamination outside boundaries, and how cross-boundary risks are managed?

6) Conditions for future actions or potential relisting
- Why it matters: While deletion does not preclude future action, communities benefit from transparent criteria for when EPA would intervene if conditions change.
- Practical question: What specific findings (e.g., exceedances, pathway activation) would prompt additional Superfund action or consideration of relisting, and how will the public be notified?

Note: These are informational prompts to help frame comments; they are not legal advice.
