# Public Summary: NHTSA-2026-0628-0001

_Source file: `summaries/NHTSA-2026-0628-0001.md`_
_Detected type: `NPRM`_

## What This Document Does
- Proposes to remove the Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standard (FMVSS No. 102) requirement that vehicles display the instantaneous transmission shift position for vehicles equipped with automated driving systems (ADS) and without human driving controls.
- Provides a deregulatory rationale (cost and burden reduction) in the preamble but does not supply technical, safety, or economic analyses in the docket.
- Leaves optionality for manufacturers to provide displays, but removes the regulatory baseline that required them for human-driven vehicles.

## Why It Matters
- The display requirement currently gives visible information about vehicle drive/gear state that can be observed by drivers and other stakeholders (passengers, emergency responders, maintenance personnel).
- Removing the requirement shifts the safety assumption from human-centered error prevention to reliance on ADS performance and manufacturers’ design choices.
- The NPRM makes strong safety conclusions (“no negative impact”) but the administrative record lacks the analyses (human factors, failure modes, cost-benefit) normally needed to support those conclusions.
- The rule could create real-world observability gaps in edge cases (ADS malfunction, towing/maintenance, emergency response) unless the agency documents how those risks are identified and addressed.

## Who Is Affected
- ADS-equipped vehicle manufacturers (compliance costs and design choices).
- Passengers, emergency responders, tow/repair personnel, and other non-driver stakeholders who may rely on visible gear-state signals.
- State/local authorities, first responders, and service industry actors whose procedures assume external or interior state indicators.
- Commenters seeking to shape the evidentiary record and any future legal challenges.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Missing empirical safety analysis
   - Why it matters: The agency asserts “no safety disbenefits” but provides no human factors testing, crash/failure modeling, or data to support that assertion.
   - Practical question to ask: Please provide any human-subject testing, failure-mode analysis, or quantitative modeling the agency used to conclude there are no safety disbenefits; if none exist, explain why the assertion is supported without such evidence.

2. Non-driver stakeholders and observability gaps
   - Why it matters: The NPRM appears driver-centric; passengers, emergency responders, and service personnel may rely on shift-state displays for situational awareness during malfunctions, crashes, towing, or maintenance.
   - Practical question to ask: Has NHTSA assessed the information needs of non-drivers (e.g., first responders, tow operators) and the consequences if ADS vehicles do not provide readily visible drive/gear-state signals?

3. Failure-mode and edge-case analysis
   - Why it matters: Removing a mandated display assumes ADS will always provide safe, observable states. ADS or powertrain failures could leave vehicle state ambiguous and increase risk.
   - Practical question to ask: Request a failure-mode analysis describing foreseeable ADS or transmission failure scenarios and how lack of a shift-position display could affect safety and response.

4. Lack of alternatives and conditional approaches
   - Why it matters: The proposal appears binary (requirement vs removal) with no evaluated middle paths (conditional requirements, emergency-only displays, non-visual signals).
   - Practical question to ask: Ask the agency to analyze and compare alternatives such as conditional display requirements (e.g., required display when vehicle is stopped, in limp mode, or during towing), non-visual signalling, or phased removal.

5. Missing cost and behavioral analysis
   - Why it matters: The NPRM claims deregulatory cost savings but provides no quantified costs, benefits, or analysis of how manufacturers will respond (e.g., will some retain displays, others remove them, or will liability drive reintroduction).
   - Practical question to ask: Request a quantified estimate of costs and benefits and empirical or modeled evidence predicting manufacturer and market responses to removal of the requirement.

6. Procedural adequacy under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA)
   - Why it matters: Conclusory claims without supporting analysis risk being arbitrary and capricious under the APA; a thin record increases legal vulnerability.
   - Practical question to ask: Ask NHTSA to identify the evidentiary basis that satisfies its APA obligation and to supply missing analyses before finalizing the rule.
