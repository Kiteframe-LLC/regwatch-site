# Public Summary: FCC-2026-0991-0001

_Source file: `summaries/FCC-2026-0991-0001.md`_
_Detected type: `RFI`_

## What This Document Does
- Requests input on modernizing internet-based Telecommunications Relay Services (TRS).
- Seeks data and comment on: compensation rates and classification, automated/ASR-based services, removal of legacy requirements (like the TRS Numbering Directory), development of objective performance metrics, possible support for VRS calls to U.S. embassies, and the need for ongoing compliance reporting.
- Frames changes as technology-driven updates to align TRS with IP-based tools while protecting the TRS Fund from misuse.

## Why It Matters
- Affects accuracy of billing and compensation from the TRS Fund; small classification shifts can create financial incentives that change provider behavior.
- Could change the actual accessibility and quality of services for people who are deaf, hard of hearing, or have speech disabilities, particularly if ASR/text-to-speech solutions perform worse for certain users or languages.
- May alter routing, emergency access, and interoperability if directory and legacy routing requirements are removed.
- Reducing reporting/oversight could limit FAA/agency visibility into misuse or service degradation.
- The Commission signals major policy shifts but, in the provided materials, offers limited empirical evidence or cost/benefit analysis to support those changes—making public input on data and practical impacts especially influential.

## Who Is Affected
- TRS providers (IP Relay, IP CTS, VRS) — business models and compensation.
- Users who rely on TRS, including Deaf, hard-of-hearing, and speech-disabled communities — service quality and accessibility.
- State and federal emergency services and 911 routing systems — routing and directory impacts.
- Consumer and disability advocates — advocacy and enforcement roles.
- Technology vendors (ASR, text-to-speech, VoIP signaling) — implementation and interoperability.
- Auditors, compliance officers, and the TRS Fund administrator — monitoring and fraud-detection functions.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Issue: The asserted fraud/misclassification risk from compensation differentials
   - Why it matters: The Commission cites incentives to misclassify minutes as a central rationale for reforms, but the record provided lacks quantified evidence of such fraud or its scale.
   - Practical question to ask: What empirical data, audits, or case studies can demonstrate the incidence and magnitude of misclassification or fraudulent billing tied to current compensation rates?

2. Issue: Functional equivalence of fully automated IP Relay and ASR-only IP CTS
   - Why it matters: Treating automated IP Relay as equivalent to ASR-only IP CTS would affect compensation and permitted technologies; but technical or user-experience parity is not documented.
   - Practical question to ask: Please provide comparative performance data (accuracy, latency, error types, language support, user satisfaction) for fully automated IP Relay vs. ASR-only IP CTS and CA-assisted services.

3. Issue: Proposal to remove TRS Numbering Directory requirements
   - Why it matters: Directory removal could change call routing, inter-provider connectivity, and emergency access—potentially creating edge-case failures for certain users or cross-network calls.
   - Practical question to ask: What technical analyses show that existing VoIP signaling, numbering, and routing will fully substitute for directory-based routing without loss of access or emergency capability?

4. Issue: Proposal to eliminate annual compliance reports and reduce oversight
   - Why it matters: Less routine reporting may reduce visibility into misuse, degrade fraud detection, and weaken enforcement while the Fund faces new structural changes.
   - Practical question to ask: If annual reports are removed, what alternative, timely monitoring mechanisms (e.g., targeted audits, event-triggered reporting, automated logs) will ensure continued transparency and fraud detection?

5. Issue: Lack of defined, objective metrics for performance and equivalence
   - Why it matters: The Commission asks for metrics but provides no baseline measures (e.g., ASR WER thresholds, latency caps, uptime, multilingual performance), making comparisons and enforcement unclear.
   - Practical question to ask: What specific, quantifiable metrics should the Commission adopt (and what measurement methods) to assess ASR/text-to-speech accuracy, latency, comprehension, and overall accessibility across user populations?
