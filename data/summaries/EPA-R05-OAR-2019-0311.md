## What This Document Does
- EPA proposes to approve Illinois' second implementation period regional haze plan and, in the same notice, approve the visibility-related portion of Illinois' older infrastructure SIP submissions for the 2012 PM2.5 and 2015 ozone standards.
- Illinois has no Class I areas inside the state, so the regional haze part is about whether Illinois sources are doing enough to avoid impairing visibility in protected areas in other states.
- Illinois screened sources using LADCO's `Q/d` method, selected certain units for full four-factor review, treated seven units as already having effective controls, and concluded that no additional controls were needed for reasonable progress in this period.

## Why It Matters
- This proposal is important because EPA is mostly approving Illinois' choice not to require stronger visibility controls at several major sources during this planning period.
- The key decision point is source selection. Illinois says it used a unit-level `Q/d` threshold of four and that this produced a list of 30 units accounting for `71.4 percent` of emissions from units with `Q/d` above one. If that threshold is too narrow, some potentially important sources may never get full review.
- EPA also accepts Illinois' judgment that seven units already have "existing effective controls" strong enough that a new full four-factor analysis would likely show no additional controls are needed.
- For the units that were analyzed, EPA gives some cost-effectiveness results and then concludes that additional measures are not necessary or not cost-effective. The visible record looks technically serious, but much of the supporting detail sits in appendices and technical support documents rather than in the surfaced text.
- The Prong 4 infrastructure approval is another place where clearer explanation would help. EPA says an approved regional haze SIP satisfies the visibility requirement for these infrastructure submissions, but the notice gives only a compact explanation of that bridge.

## Who Is Affected
- Major Illinois stationary sources that were screened or analyzed in the regional haze process, including units EPA discusses at Prairie State, Marion, Rain CII Carbon, Holcim/Joppa, ADM, Baldwin, Newton, Powerton, and Kincaid.
- Downwind states, federal land managers, and communities interested in visibility at Class I areas affected by Illinois emissions.
- Illinois regulators, because EPA would be approving the state's chosen long-term strategy rather than requiring a stronger alternative.
- Public-interest groups, neighboring jurisdictions, and technically oriented commenters reviewing the support for EPA's approval rationale.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. The source-selection threshold needs a clearer justification.
Why it matters: The `Q/d` cutoff determines which sources receive full four-factor analysis and which do not. That choice shapes the whole record.
Practical question a commenter can ask: What sensitivity analysis did EPA or Illinois perform to show that different `Q/d` thresholds would not materially change the set of sources needing review?

2. The "existing effective controls" finding may need more unit-specific explanation.
Why it matters: Seven units appear to have avoided fresh full four-factor analysis because Illinois and EPA concluded existing controls were already enough.
Practical question a commenter can ask: What unit-level evidence shows those controls remain sufficient through 2028 without a new full four-factor analysis?

3. The visible record is thin on comparison tables for rejected control options.
Why it matters: EPA gives some cost figures, but the prepared package does not show enough side-by-side engineering and cost information to test the "not cost-effective" conclusions.
Practical question a commenter can ask: Will EPA publish a table showing each control option considered, projected reductions, useful-life assumptions, and the reason it was rejected?

4. The docket is harder to navigate than the notice implies.
Why it matters: The most important regional haze support appears to sit in appendices, TSDs, or a parallel docket rather than in the prepared package.
Practical question a commenter can ask: Where can the public find clearly linked copies of Illinois' appendices, LADCO's TSD, and EPA's 2025 TSD?

5. The Prong 4 bridge could be more explicit.
Why it matters: EPA is using the regional haze approval to support the visibility part of the 2012 PM2.5 and 2015 ozone infrastructure SIP submissions.
Practical question a commenter can ask: How does the regional haze analysis in this rule specifically satisfy the visibility obligations for those two infrastructure submissions?

## Evidence Quotes
- "The EPA proposes to find that the Illinois regional haze SIP revision for the second implementation period meets the applicable statutory and regulatory requirements and thus proposes to approve the submission into Illinois' SIP."
- "Illinois EPA initially set a unit-level Q/d threshold of four to begin identifying point source emission units for potentially cost-effective control additions."
- "This resulted in a list of 30 units, accounting for 71.4 percent of all emissions of SO2, NOX, NH3, and PM2.5 from units with Q/d greater than one in Illinois, including all the largest sources."
- "Illinois EPA then determined that the existing controls on the following seven units are effective to the extent that a full four-factor analysis would likely result in a conclusion that no further controls are necessary."
- "For each unit, Illinois EPA determined that additional control measures are not necessary to make reasonable progress in the second implementation period and that pursuing additional emission reductions through new emission control equipment or emissions limitations is not cost-effective."
- "The EPA proposes to approve both submissions as meeting CAA section 110(a)(2)(D)(i)(II)."
