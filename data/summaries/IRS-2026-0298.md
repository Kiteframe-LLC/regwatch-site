# Public Summary: IRS-2026-0298-0001

_Source file: `summaries/IRS-2026-0298-0001.md`_
_Detected type: `NPRM`_

## What This Document Does
- Proposes rules implementing 26 U.S.C. 530A for opening an initial “Trump account” for minors and assigning a default responsible party.
- Specifies who may make the election to open an account (priority order), the form/process (Form 4547 or electronic application), and that only the first processed election will open an account.
- Reserves most operational rules (contributions, investments, distributions, reporting, trustee requirements) for later guidance.
- States the rule is “economically significant” per OIRA but also characterizes some costs as de minimis for EO 14192 purposes.

## Why It Matters
- The NPRM sets the gatekeeping rules that determine who controls a minor’s account during childhood. That control affects investment decisions, rollovers, and beneficiary outcomes.
- Because the beneficiary is a child, the default control rules are practically dispositive for account governance and can create incentives for strategic filings.
- The NPRM asserts broad scale (millions of children affected) and uses historical-return examples to justify earlier account opening, but it provides little evidence on how many additional accounts will open or how disputes will be resolved.
- Many substantive program features remain reserved for future rulemaking, meaning this entry-rule NPRM shapes outcomes before key operational details are finalized.

## Who Is Affected
- Eligible minors: those under age 18 by end of election year with an SSN (per NPRM).
- Adults prioritized to open an account: legal guardians, parents, adult siblings, grandparents (in that order).
- Any adult who files the election first and whose filing is processed by the IRS—because that person will generally be the default responsible party.
- Trustees, custodial account providers, state agencies handling foster/ward cases, and families with nontraditional custody arrangements.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Filer-as-default responsible party and race-to-file incentive
   - Why it matters: The rule makes the individual who makes the election the default responsible party and counts only the first processed election. That creates incentives for strategic, possibly inappropriate, filings and could misassign control.
   - Practical question: Ask the agency to justify why the filer should be the default rather than the legal guardian where one exists, and to propose safeguards (notice, verification, or transfer procedures).

2. Handling of foster children, wards of the state, and nontraditional custody
   - Why it matters: The priority order may not map to state custody arrangements or institutional caregivers, risking miscontrol or exclusion of eligible administrators.
   - Practical question: Request specific rules for state-custody cases and for persons with statutory guardianship orders (including notice and coordination with state agencies).

3. Lack of quantitative uptake and dispute analysis
   - Why it matters: The NPRM claims clarity will increase account openings, but provides no estimate of additional accounts or analysis of likely dispute rates and administrative burden.
   - Practical question: Ask for a quantitative estimate of expected additional accounts opened because of this rule and modeling of likely conflicting-election scenarios.

4. Rejection of Secretary-initiated openings and alternatives analysis
   - Why it matters: The agency dismisses a Secretary-initiated opening alternative on legal and administrative grounds without fully analyzing narrower or consent-based variations that might increase participation.
   - Practical question: Request explanation and legal analysis supporting rejection of Secretary-led or state-mediated opening approaches and whether any narrower consent models were considered.

5. Sequencing and reserved operational rules
   - Why it matters: Key program features are reserved for later, but this rule assigns control before those operating rules are set; that may produce mismatches or unintended consequences.
   - Practical question: Ask the agency to assess whether later rules on contributions, trustee availability, or reporting would materially change the benefits or risks of the chosen election/control framework.
