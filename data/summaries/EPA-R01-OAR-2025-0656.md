## What This Document Does
- EPA proposes to approve a Connecticut SIP revision that incorporates a source‑specific consent order establishing VOC controls intended to satisfy RACT for a stationary source.
- EPA also proposes to adopt a negative declaration for the rubber tire manufacturing CTG category in Connecticut (i.e., the State says there are no such sources in-state).
- The docket contains a narrative preamble but no supporting technical documents (no TSD, modeling memo, cost analysis, or raw emissions datasets appear in the EPA docket).

## Why It Matters
- RACT approvals affect how much VOC (ozone‑forming) pollution a source must control in a serious/severe ozone nonattainment area. Adequate controls can reduce local ozone and health impacts.
- The proposed approval mainly defers to the State’s analysis. The public record is thin on technical detail, so EPA’s decision rests largely on state representations rather than independent EPA modeling or datasets.
- Gaps in the record (emissions calculations, method detail, alternatives analysis, negative‑declaration search depth) are practical leverage points for commenters who want stronger assurance the controls meet the “reasonably available” and “best” criteria under the CAA.
- If the state’s assumptions are incomplete or misclassified (e.g., missed facilities or underestimated emissions), emissions could be under‑controlled despite a RACT finding.

## Who Is Affected
- The specific facility subject to the consent order and its operator.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1) Missing underlying emissions data and calculation methods
- Why it matters: Without raw data and calculation steps, commenters cannot verify the State’s claim that emissions are below applicability thresholds or that controls achieve RACT.
- Practical question to ask: Please provide the raw emissions calculations, assumptions, and measurement or engineering data used to estimate the source’s total VOC emissions and PTE.

2) Insufficient detail on the “three‑step” RACT determination
- Why it matters: The three‑step method is cited as the basis for concluding the consent order meets RACT, but the docket does not explain how steps were performed or weighted.
- Practical question to ask: Please provide a detailed description of each step of CT DEEP’s three‑step RACT process, including comparisons to CTG/NSPS benchmarks and how site‑specific factors were considered.

3) Reliance on thresholds (e.g., 6 tpy) without sensitivity analysis
- Why it matters: Thresholds can mask situations where small absolute emissions nonetheless offer high marginal reduction opportunities, or where variability could push a source above applicability.
- Practical question to ask: Please provide sensitivity or uncertainty analysis showing whether emissions variability or alternative assumptions could change applicability or control expectations.

4) Negative declaration completeness for rubber tire manufacturing
- Why it matters: The negative declaration rests on searches of NAICS/SIC codes, interviews, and web searches. Misclassification or small/intermittent operations could be missed.
- Practical question to ask: Please describe the search protocol in full (databases queried, date ranges, search terms, interviews conducted) and any follow‑up verification steps.

5) Lack of alternatives or cost‑effectiveness analysis
- Why it matters: RACT requires reasonably available controls; the record does not show an alternatives evaluation or why the chosen controls are the best feasible option for this facility.
- Practical question to ask: Please provide the alternatives considered (including enhanced LDAR or intermediate control levels), feasibility/cost comparisons, and justification for rejecting other options.
