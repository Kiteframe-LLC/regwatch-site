## What This Document Does
- EPA proposes to approve a bundle of Ohio source-specific air-pollution control decisions into the federally enforceable SIP.
- Most of the action concerns Cleveland-area major VOC and NOx sources that must meet RACT under the 2015 ozone standard.
- EPA also proposes two Cincinnati-area "SIP strengthening" measures even though Cincinnati is in a maintenance area, not a Moderate nonattainment area.
- The package includes existing-control approvals for PPG, Owens Corning, Akron Paint and Varnish, U.S. Steel, and Charter Steel; a new `4.6 lb/ton` kiln limit for Carmeuse; a `105 lb/hr` rolling NOx limit plus reevaluation trigger for Ross; SIP-strengthening measures for GE Aerospace Evendale and Tyson Foods; and removal of Formica's source-specific VOC rule in favor of a CTG-based rule.

## Why It Matters
- SIP approvals matter because they decide what pollution controls become federally enforceable.
- This rule is technically narrow, but it covers many different facilities and sets the practical standard for what EPA will accept as "reasonably available" control in Ohio.
- The main issue is not obvious legal authority. EPA clearly has authority to approve or reject state SIP choices.
- The real issue is record transparency. EPA repeatedly says the underlying RACT studies are in the docket, but the prepared review package did not include those studies, and prior attachment ingestion failed.
- Some parts are better surfaced than others. Owens Corning and Akron Paint and Varnish include more concrete control comparisons and cost figures, while PPG, Formica, Ross, and parts of U.S. Steel rely more heavily on summarized studies or non-public cost support.
- That makes this a strong candidate for comments asking EPA to put more of the technical and economic basis into the public record before finalizing the rule.

## Who Is Affected
- The named Ohio facilities, because their permit terms or rule obligations would become federally enforceable SIP requirements or SIP-strengthening measures.
- State regulators, because EPA would be approving Ohio's chosen source-specific approach rather than writing a fresh federal control program.
- Nearby communities and public-health advocates, because the proposal decides what level of VOC and NOx control EPA considers adequate for these sources.
- Other regulated facilities, because the final rule may signal how much proof EPA expects when a state argues that existing controls are already RACT or that tighter controls are not economically reasonable.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Missing public support for several engineering and cost conclusions
   - Why it matters: EPA often gives the bottom-line conclusion without the underlying study, calculation, or design assumptions needed to test it.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Will EPA add public summaries or supporting tables showing how each major feasibility and cost conclusion was calculated?

2. Reliance on hidden cost analyses for Ross and parts of U.S. Steel
   - Why it matters: If EPA relies on CBI to reject tighter NOx controls, the public may not be able to comment meaningfully on whether those controls are truly unreasonable.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: What non-CBI cost components, emissions-reduction assumptions, and retrofit scenarios can EPA disclose so the public can evaluate the RACT determination?

3. Formica's non-backsliding showing is too compressed
   - Why it matters: This portion removes a source-specific SIP rule and replaces it with a different VOC rule structure, so EPA should show the equivalence clearly.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Please provide the representative coating comparisons and metric-conversion analysis supporting the claim that the CTG-based rule is equivalent to or stricter than the old source-specific rule.

4. Existing-control determinations need clearer verification terms
   - Why it matters: PPG, Akron, U.S. Steel, and Charter Steel all depend heavily on the conclusion that existing controls remain the best practical option.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: What monitoring, inspection, and recordkeeping provisions ensure that the claimed control performance is actually maintained in routine operation?

5. Carmeuse's RACT limit-setting method deserves more explanation
   - Why it matters: EPA says the new kiln limit comes from the highest of five stack tests plus a `20%` contingency factor, but does not explain why that method defines RACT.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Why does "highest test plus contingency" represent RACT rather than simply preserving current performance with added cushion?

## Evidence Quotes
- "Installation of four RTOs is economically infeasible, with cost-effectiveness estimates ranging from $36,632 to $750,881 per ton of VOC removed."
- "Thermal oxidizers are technically feasible and could achieve up to 98% VOC destruction efficiency, but are not economically reasonable, with an estimated cost-effectiveness of $123,385 per ton of VOC removed."
- "Based on stack testing, Carmeuse calculated a revised NO X limit of 4.6 lb per ton of lime for P001 and P002, representing the highest value from five stack tests plus a 20% contingency factor."
- "Ross Incineration submitted a detailed cost calculation contained in a Confidential Business Information document, which the EPA relied on for its RACT analysis."
- "Based on Formica's analysis, Ohio determined that this revision does not constitute backsliding because both limits achieve equivalent VOC reductions and actual emissions will not increase."
