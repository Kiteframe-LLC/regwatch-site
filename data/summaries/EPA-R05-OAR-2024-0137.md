## What This Document Does
- EPA proposes to approve a large set of Michigan air rules into the federally enforceable SIP for the Western Michigan ozone nonattainment areas.
- The package covers VOC rules in Part 6 and NOx rules in Part 8, and EPA says these revisions satisfy Moderate-stage RACT requirements under the 2015 ozone standard.
- EPA also proposes to approve two VOC rules on consumer products and architectural and industrial maintenance coatings as SIP-strengthening measures, not as RACT rules.
- EPA is not approving every submitted provision. It excludes Rule `336.1601(a)`, excludes Rule `336.1632(9)` while replacing the rest of that rule, and defers action on Rule `336.1841(8)`, `336.1842(10)`, and `336.1844(9)`.

## Why It Matters
- This is the kind of EPA action that can look routine but still deserves comment because it decides what Michigan control measures become federally enforceable.
- The proposal matters most for two reasons. First, EPA says Western Michigan already moved from Moderate to Serious ozone nonattainment status, but this action still addresses only the earlier Moderate RACT package. Second, EPA's justification relies heavily on comparison-based analysis that is mostly described rather than shown here.
- EPA says Michigan and EPA performed due-diligence reviews comparing Michigan's rules to CTGs, ACT documents, and other states' rules. EPA then concludes Michigan's rules are "mostly consistent with" or "generally consistent with or more stringent than" those comparison points. That is the core reasoning behind the approval.
- The problem for commenters is transparency. The export package identifies supporting materials, including a Michigan technical support document and two Michigan submittals, but none were ingested for this run. The attachment manifest shows no required missing files, so the workflow can continue, but the public-facing basis is still thinner than EPA's conclusions suggest.
- The result is a proposal where EPA may be right on the merits, but the most commentable weakness is that key logic sits in unseen tables and supporting documents instead of the preamble itself.

## Who Is Affected
- Michigan regulators, because the approved state rules would become part of the federally enforceable SIP.
- Facilities in the Western Michigan nonattainment areas that are covered by Michigan's VOC and NOx rule categories.
- Businesses selling products covered by the consumer-product and AIM-coating rules, because those rules are part of Michigan's attainment strategy.
- Nearby communities and air-quality advocates, because the rule determines what level of existing-source control EPA is willing to accept for this area.
- Future commenters in related ozone SIP actions, because this proposal may signal how much explanation EPA thinks is enough when it relies on cross-state comparisons instead of fuller engineering analysis in the preamble.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. EPA should show the comparison tables it relies on
   - Why it matters: The proposal stands or falls on comparisons to CTGs, ACTs, and other states' rules, but those comparisons are not visible in this package.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Will EPA add a public side-by-side table showing where Michigan is less stringent, equivalent, or more stringent for each relevant source category?

2. The "no new reasonably available controls" conclusion is too compressed
   - Why it matters: EPA says it found no new reasonably available control technologies, but does not show what technologies were reviewed or how they were screened out.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: What technologies, source categories, and feasibility criteria did EPA review before concluding that no newer controls are reasonably available?

3. The `110(l)` noninterference showing for higher exemption thresholds needs more public detail
   - Why it matters: EPA says Michigan's higher exemption limits will not increase emissions, but the supplied text does not show the emissions scenarios behind that conclusion.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Please provide the representative facilities, baseline assumptions, and emissions comparisons supporting the claim that the revised exemptions do not increase emissions.

4. EPA should explain the carveouts and deferred NOx subrules more clearly
   - Why it matters: EPA is approving a package with exclusions and deferrals, which raises obvious questions about completeness and practical enforceability.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: What is the practical effect of excluding Rule `336.1601(a)` and Rule `336.1632(9)` and deferring action on the listed Part 8 subrules?

5. EPA should address the Moderate-versus-Serious timing issue directly
   - Why it matters: The preamble says Western Michigan is already a Serious area and that the Serious RACT SIP was due on January 1, 2026, yet this proposal still addresses only Moderate RACT.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: How does this approval fit with the area's current Serious-area obligations, and should commenters expect a follow-on action that revisits these same controls?

## Evidence Quotes
- "Michigan's Serious RACT SIP was due on January 1, 2026, but this action addresses RACT for Moderate nonattainment only."
- "Michigan's analysis shows that the higher exemption limits will not increase emissions."
- "The EPA found Michigan's rules to be mostly consistent with similar VOC RACT rules adopted by other States and with the relevant CTGs."
- "The EPA found Michigan's rules to be generally consistent with or more stringent than control measures in other States' regulations, CTGs, and ACT documents."
- "The EPA has not identified any new control technologies that are reasonably available considering technological and economic feasibility for these sources."
