# Public Summary: DOJ-OAG-2026-0034-0001

_Source file: `summaries/DOJ-OAG-2026-0034-0001.md`_
_Detected type: `NPRM`_

## What This Document Does
- Proposes revisions to DOJ regulations implementing 28 U.S.C. Chapter 154 (expedited federal habeas review for death‑sentenced prisoners).
- Deletes or narrows regulatory requirements that DOJ says are not “expressly stated” in the statute.
- Targets regulatory provisions covering: counsel competency standards, counsel compensation, timing of counsel appointment, definition of indigent prisoners, DOJ internal review processes, and certification finality.
- Declares changes severable and frames the goal as enabling more prompt decisions on States’ requests for certification.

## Why It Matters
- Chapter 154 is the statutory pathway for states to secure expedited federal habeas review in capital cases. How DOJ interprets and implements that statute determines whether states can receive certification.
- DOJ says expedited procedures have never been applied and attributes that to unauthorized regulatory barriers. If true, removing barriers could change federal habeas timelines in capital cases.
- The NPRM relies mainly on statutory interpretation and a descriptive statistic (average time on death row) but provides limited empirical analysis tying the regulatory requirements to the absence of certifications.
- The rule raises legal and procedural risks: courts may disagree about whether the statute bars DOJ from imposing interpretive regulatory criteria, and the agency’s causal case and alternatives analysis are not clearly documented in the record.

## Who Is Affected
- State governments and state public defense systems seeking Chapter 154 certification.
- Death‑sentenced prisoners whose federal habeas review could be affected by changes in certification frequency or timing.
- Defense counsel in capital cases (standards, compensation, appointment timing).
- Federal courts and DOJ components that process certification requests and implement expedited habeas procedures.
- Stakeholders concerned with counsel quality and procedural safeguards in capital litigation.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
- Issue 1 — Causal link between past regulations and zero certifications
  - Why it matters: The NPRM’s central justification is that prior regulatory requirements prevented certifications. If the real barrier was state noncompliance or litigation choices, removing regulations may not produce the intended results.
  - Practical question to ask: Please provide empirical evidence showing that regulatory criteria — rather than state practices or litigation factors — were the primary reason no state obtained Chapter 154 certification.

- Issue 2 — Lack of alternatives and analysis of options
  - Why it matters: The rule removes regulatory criteria wholesale rather than presenting and assessing narrower or intermediate approaches (e.g., guidance, partial modification, tiered certification).
  - Practical question to ask: What alternatives did the Department consider, and why were less‑disruptive options (guidance, partial modifications, oversight measures) rejected? Provide comparative analysis.

- Issue 3 — Potential effects on counsel quality and safeguards
  - Why it matters: Changes to competency, compensation, and appointment timing could affect the quality of capital defense—a core statutory concern underpinning Chapter 154 eligibility.
  - Practical question to ask: How will the Department ensure that removing these regulatory criteria will not reduce effective counsel or undermine the fairness of capital proceedings? Provide metrics or review standards DOJ will use.

- Issue 4 — Insufficient quantification of impacts
  - Why it matters: The NPRM cites the average time on death row but does not quantify how much faster habeas resolution would be if certifications increased.
  - Practical question to ask: Provide an estimate (with assumptions) of the expected reduction in federal habeas duration and the number of cases likely to be affected by the rule.

- Issue 5 — Legal interpretation and statutory authority
  - Why it matters: The proposal rests on the claim that the statute forbids regulatory requirements beyond what is “expressly stated.” Courts may disagree, creating litigation risk.
  - Practical question to ask: Provide the Department’s full statutory‑interpretation analysis and any legal memoranda supporting the view that the statute prohibits interpretive regulatory criteria.

## Evidence Quotes

- “Nearly 30 years after the original enactment of chapter 154, its expedited federal habeas corpus procedures have not been applied in federal habeas review of a single state capital case.”
- “There are no requirements for certification or for application of this chapter other than those expressly stated in this chapter.”
- “This rule would remove impediments to certification that chapter 154 does not authorize and would enable more prompt decisions on States' requests for certification.”
- “The proposed rule clarifies that each proposed change is severable.”
