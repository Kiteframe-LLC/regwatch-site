## What This Document Does
- Proposes regulatory changes to blacknose shark management under Magnuson‑Stevens Act/ATCA.
- Key proposals in the preamble/docket:
  - Remove the current geographic boundary for blacknose shark management.
  - Modify commercial retention limits (including in‑season adjustment authority).
  - Revise recreational size and retention limits.
- Agency support materials cited in the docket: draft Environmental Assessment (EA), Regulatory Impact Review (RIR), and Initial Regulatory Flexibility Analysis (IRFA).

## Why It Matters
- Shifts management approach from fixed spatial constraints toward greater flexibility intended to increase quota utilization.
- Could change where and when fishing effort occurs, with consequences for discard rates, bycatch, and local fishing economies.
- The rule’s rationale rests on empirical claims (e.g., underutilized quota) and asserted ecological benefits (e.g., fewer dead discards) that are not fully explained in the preamble.
- Several analytical gaps in the visible preamble raise procedural and evidentiary risks under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) if the record does not substantiate causal links or give adequate alternatives.
- Commenters can influence the administrative record by asking for specific data, models, and intermediate policy options.

## Who Is Affected
- Commercial blacknose shark permit holders and dealers (retention limit changes, in‑season adjustments).
- Recreational anglers (size and bag limit changes).
- Coastal communities dependent on shark fisheries and related seafood markets.
- Conservation organizations and fishery managers concerned about bycatch and discard mortality.
- Enforcement and monitoring agencies (changes in spatial enforcement demands).

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Issue: Causal link between underutilized quota and the current boundary
   - Why it matters: The rule’s main economic justification is that quota is underutilized (reported ~36% average harvest), but underutilization may be driven by market or operational factors, not the boundary. If NMFS misdiagnoses the cause, the rule may not achieve its goals.
   - Practical question to ask: Please provide the data and analysis demonstrating that the current boundary is a primary cause of underutilization, and explain how alternative explanations (market demand, fuel costs, fleet capacity) were ruled out.

2. Issue: Evidence for reduced discard mortality from boundary removal
   - Why it matters: The preamble asserts that maintaining the boundary “may increase the number of blacknose sharks discarded dead.” That ecological claim is a central conservation justification and requires quantitative support to satisfy National Standards and APA standards.
   - Practical question to ask: Request the discard mortality analysis by region and gear type that quantifies how boundary removal will change discard rates and mortality.

3. Issue: Lack of behavioral/fleet response modeling
   - Why it matters: The proposal assumes fishermen will change behavior in response to increased flexibility (leading to higher quota utilization). Without behavioral or sensitivity modeling, the agency cannot reliably predict outcomes or manage in‑season risks.
   - Practical question to ask: Ask NMFS to provide models or sensitivity analyses showing likely fleet responses (effort redistribution, timing) to the proposed retention and boundary changes.

4. Issue: Missing intermediate or mitigation alternatives
   - Why it matters: The record appears to omit partial, phased, or conditional boundary options (e.g., seasonal zones, tiered retention tied to stock indicators, permit‑based rules) that could reduce risk while testing flexibility.
   - Practical question to ask: Request evaluation of intermediate alternatives such as partial boundary adjustments, phased implementation, or retention tiers linked to stock status and bycatch metrics.

5. Issue: Safeguards for in‑season adjustments and quota control
   - Why it matters: Granting NMFS in‑season authority to change commercial limits creates incentives for effort concentration and potential quota overshoot without clear procedural safeguards.
   - Practical question to ask: Ask NMFS to describe specific triggers, monitoring data streams, and procedural limits that will prevent overfishing or uneven late‑season surges.
