## What This Document Does
- EPA proposes to approve Illinois' second-period regional haze plan, which is the state's visibility strategy for sources whose emissions affect protected national parks and wilderness areas in other states.
- Illinois has no in-state Class I areas, so the key question is whether Illinois sources are making enough "reasonable progress" for downwind visibility.
- Illinois screened sources using LADCO's `Q/d` method, chose nine units for fresh four-factor review, and treated seven other units as already having "existing effective controls."
- EPA also proposes to approve part of Illinois' older infrastructure SIP submissions for the 2012 PM2.5 and 2015 ozone standards on the theory that an adequate regional haze SIP also satisfies the visibility-protection requirement for those infrastructure submissions.

## Why It Matters
- This rule matters because it is mostly an approval of Illinois' decision not to require more controls at several major sources during the second regional haze planning period.
- EPA's visible reasoning depends heavily on threshold choices. Illinois says it used a unit-level `Q/d` threshold of four and a source-level threshold of ten, producing a list of 30 units that captured `71.4 percent` of emissions from units with `Q/d` above one.
- That screening choice drives everything that follows. If the threshold is too narrow, important sources may never get full review.
- EPA also uses a shortcut for seven units at Baldwin, Newton, Powerton, and Kincaid, saying their existing controls are already effective enough that full new four-factor analysis would likely show no further controls are needed.
- For the nine selected units, EPA gives a few cost examples and then concludes that additional controls are not necessary or not cost-effective. That may be true, but the prepared package mostly shows the summary, not the full engineering and cost work behind it.
- The result is a record that looks technically serious but is still hard for the public to test. The preamble keeps pointing to appendix A, appendix B, LADCO's 2021 TSD, and EPA's November 12, 2025 TSD for the real support.
- The visibility-related infrastructure approvals raise a similar issue. EPA says an approved second-period regional haze SIP satisfies Prong 4 for these older PM2.5 and ozone infrastructure submissions, but the explanation in the reviewed text is compressed.

## Who Is Affected
- Major Illinois sources covered by the source-selection and four-factor review process, including Prairie State, SIPC Marion, Rain CII Carbon, Holcim/Joppa, ADM, Baldwin, Newton, Powerton, and Kincaid.
- Downwind states and communities near Class I areas that may be affected by Illinois emissions.
- Illinois regulators, because EPA would be approving the state's chosen long-term strategy rather than requiring a stronger alternative.
- Public-interest commenters and neighboring jurisdictions, because they need enough public support in the record to evaluate whether EPA's approval is justified.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. The source-selection thresholds need clearer justification
   - Why it matters: The `Q/d` thresholds determine which sources get deeper review and which do not.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: What sensitivity testing did EPA or Illinois perform to show that different `Q/d` thresholds would not materially change the set of sources needing four-factor analysis?

2. The "existing effective controls" finding is doing a lot of work
   - Why it matters: Seven units avoided fresh full four-factor analysis because EPA accepted Illinois' claim that current controls were already enough.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: What unit-specific evidence shows those existing controls remain sufficient through 2028 without additional review?

3. No-additional-control conclusions are too summary-heavy in the surfaced record
   - Why it matters: EPA gives some cost figures, but the public-facing package does not show enough of the underlying engineering and cost assumptions to test the conclusions.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Will EPA place public summaries or tables in the docket showing control options considered, expected reductions, useful-life assumptions, and the benchmark for deciding a control is not cost-effective?

4. Appendix-level support is central but not easy to review from the prepared package
   - Why it matters: EPA repeatedly relies on Illinois' appendices, LADCO's TSD, and EPA's own TSD for the actual technical basis.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Where exactly can the public review the source-selection analysis, four-factor worksheets, and modeling support in a stable and accessible docket location?

5. The Prong 4 explanation could be more explicit
   - Why it matters: EPA is using the regional haze approval to satisfy the visibility-protection part of two infrastructure SIP submissions.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: How does the regional haze analysis in this rule specifically satisfy the visibility obligations for the 2012 PM2.5 and 2015 ozone infrastructure submissions, beyond the general guidance statement?

## Evidence Quotes
- "Illinois EPA initially set a unit-level Q/d threshold of four to begin identifying point source emission units for potentially cost-effective control additions."
- "This resulted in a list of 30 units, accounting for 71.4 percent of all emissions of SO2, NOX, NH3, and PM2.5 from units with Q/d greater than one in Illinois, including all the largest sources."
- "Illinois EPA then determined that the existing controls on the following seven units are effective to the extent that a full four-factor analysis would likely result in a conclusion that no further controls are necessary."
- "For each unit, Illinois EPA determined that additional control measures are not necessary to make reasonable progress in the second implementation period and that pursuing additional emission reductions through new emission control equipment or emissions limitations is not cost-effective."
- "The EPA proposes to approve both submissions as meeting CAA section 110(a)(2)(D)(i)(II)."
