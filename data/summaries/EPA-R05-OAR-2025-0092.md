## What This Document Does
- EPA proposes to approve an Indiana SIP revision for one facility, Keystone Automotive Industries dba Saturn Wheel in Huntington County, Indiana.
- The revision would let Keystone use an oil cover, identified as Aquastrip Fume Seal, as an alternative VOC control method for its heated cold-cleaner degreaser.
- EPA says the usual state options for this kind of degreaser include a `0.75` freeboard ratio, a water cover in limited circumstances, a refrigerated chiller, carbon adsorption, or other control equipment.
- EPA's basic conclusion is that, for this facility, the oil-cover approach should provide equivalent VOC control and therefore can be approved into the SIP.

## Why It Matters
- This is a narrow rule, but SIP approvals matter because they decide what counts as acceptable air-pollution control under federally enforceable state plans.
- EPA is not creating a new nationwide standard here. It is approving a facility-specific exception pathway based on a claim that the alternative still satisfies RACT-level control and does not interfere with Clean Air Act section `110(l)`.
- The proposal's main weakness is not obvious procedural irregularity. It is that several key technical and cost conclusions are summarized instead of shown.
- EPA says water cover would not work because the solvents are water miscible, that the freeboard option would require a `108-inch` height, and that refrigerated condensation and carbon adsorption are not feasible. But the reviewed materials do not include the underlying calculations, cost estimates, or performance comparisons.
- That creates a practical opening for public comments: ask EPA to put the engineering and emissions basis in the docket so the final approval rests on a more transparent record.

## Who Is Affected
- Keystone, which would gain a site-specific compliance path for its degreasing operation.
- Indiana regulators, because the Commissioner's Order would become part of the SIP and therefore part of the federally enforceable program.
- Nearby communities and air-quality stakeholders, because the approval depends on EPA's claim that the alternative control method is equivalent and causes no air-quality backsliding.
- Other facilities and practitioners watching site-specific RACT petitions, because this approval could signal how much support EPA expects for alternative-control requests.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Missing quantitative basis for the equivalency finding
   - Why it matters: EPA's core conclusion is that the oil cover will provide equivalent VOC control for this NMP/MEA solvent blend, but the reviewed text does not show the underlying test data or quantitative comparison.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: What facility-specific measurements, vendor data, or technical analysis show that the oil-cover method achieves VOC control equivalent to the listed controls under Keystone's real operating conditions?

2. Thin support for rejecting listed alternative controls
   - Why it matters: EPA rejects water cover, the freeboard-ratio option, refrigerated condensation, and carbon adsorption, but largely through summarized assertions rather than disclosed engineering and cost support.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Please provide the calculations and cost assumptions supporting the claim that a `0.75` freeboard ratio would require a `108-inch` height and that the other listed controls are infeasible or cost prohibitive.

3. Limited explanation of the section `110(l)` noninterference showing
   - Why it matters: EPA says the alternative will have no adverse impact on air quality because it is expected to provide equivalent control, but that legal conclusion rises or falls with the underlying technical support.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: What quantitative emissions comparison or enforceable operating conditions support EPA's conclusion that this SIP revision will not interfere with attainment or reasonable further progress?

4. Unclear monitoring and enforceability details
   - Why it matters: An oil-cover approach only works if the layer remains present and effective in actual operations, and the preamble does not clearly describe how that will be verified over time.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Will EPA or Indiana require measurable operating conditions, inspection frequency, and recordkeeping to verify that the one-inch oil layer is consistently maintained?

## Evidence Quotes
- "The oil cover controls VOC emissions from the dip tank by reducing solvent evaporation."
- "The solvents Keystone uses, NMP and MEA, are miscible in water, and attempts to use a water cover would fail to reduce VOC emissions."
- "To meet the freeboard ratio requirement of 0.75, Keystone would need to raise the freeboard height on its dip tank to 108 inches (9 feet)."
- "The cost of raising the roof or lowering the floor makes this option cost prohibitive."
- "Thus, it is reasonable to expect an oil cover will provide an equivalent control technique for VOC emissions from an NMP and MEA solvent blend."
- "The EPA concurs with the 110(l) demonstration that this action will not interfere with air quality standards and reasonable further progress requirements."
