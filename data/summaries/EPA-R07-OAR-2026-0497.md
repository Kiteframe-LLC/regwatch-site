## What This Document Does

EPA proposes to let Missouri run part of the federal chemical accident prevention program for a narrow set of facilities: agricultural anhydrous ammonia sites in Missouri.

If finalized:
- Missouri would take primary day-to-day responsibility for inspections, record reviews, audits, compliance assistance, and enforcement for that subset.
- EPA would still keep concurrent authority over those facilities.
- EPA would also keep sole authority over other regulated chemicals and over anhydrous ammonia uses that do not fit Missouri's agricultural definition.

The proposal is not a brand-new national safety standard. It is a delegation and incorporation action under Clean Air Act section 112(l), with Missouri adopting the federal Risk Management Program framework largely by reference.

## Why It Matters

This kind of rule can look administrative, but it still matters because it decides who enforces accident-prevention requirements and under what wording.

The key questions are:
- whether Missouri's modified rule is truly "no less stringent" than the federal baseline,
- whether the state has enough staff and procedures to run the program well,
- and whether the handoff between Missouri and EPA will be clear at facilities that handle more than just agricultural ammonia.

EPA's preamble gives a plausible reason to approve the request, but several important conclusions are stated at a high level rather than shown in detail. That creates a good opening for constructive comments that ask EPA to explain the record more clearly before finalizing.

## Who Is Affected

- Agricultural anhydrous ammonia facilities in Missouri that meet the program threshold.
- Operators of sites that may store agricultural ammonia alongside other regulated chemicals.
- Missouri regulators who would run the delegated portion of the program.
- EPA Region 7, which would remain a backstop and would still directly regulate the rest of the federal program.
- Nearby communities, workers, and emergency planners who depend on clear accident-prevention oversight.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)

1. **The state/federal line could be clearer at mixed-use facilities.**
Why it matters: EPA says Missouri would handle agricultural anhydrous ammonia facilities, while EPA would keep authority over other regulated chemicals and some nonqualifying ammonia uses. That sounds workable, but the notice does not give concrete examples of how this split would work at a site with multiple chemicals or mixed operations.
Question to ask: Can EPA add examples or a table showing which authority applies in common mixed-facility scenarios?

2. **The "equivalent or safer" alternative-standard pathway needs more explanation.**
Why it matters: Missouri replaces certain general engineering-practice language with a specific ANSI/CGA standard, but also allows other standards if the state director approves them as equivalent or safer. That flexibility may be reasonable, but the notice does not explain the criteria, evidence requirements, or transparency of that approval process.
Question to ask: What technical criteria and documentation will Missouri use to approve an alternative standard as "equivalent or safer," and will those approvals be publicly available?

3. **The staffing showing is thin for a program covering about 200 facilities.**
Why it matters: EPA says Missouri has three staff devoted almost entirely to implementation and plans to inspect about 40 facilities each year. That may be enough, but the notice does not show how plan review, technical assistance, incident follow-up, and enforcement work affect that estimate.
Question to ask: Can EPA provide more detail on workload assumptions, inspection prioritization, and how Missouri will handle non-inspection responsibilities with current staffing?

4. **The "no less stringent" finding would benefit from a more concrete side-by-side comparison.**
Why it matters: EPA repeatedly concludes that Missouri's changes are no less stringent than the federal rule, but the preamble gives only a fairly general explanation. Commenters can ask EPA to show how edge cases remain covered, rather than relying on assurance alone.
Question to ask: Can EPA include a short comparison table showing how Missouri's facility definition and engineering-standard substitution preserve equivalent coverage and protection in practice?

## Evidence Quotes

- "The EPA is proposing to approve the request submitted by MoDNR... to delegate a partial RMP for agricultural anhydrous ammonia facilities."
- "The EPA would retain authority for the RMP for all other regulated chemicals..."
- "We have reviewed the limited changes Missouri made to its regulation and have determined that the applicability criteria and program requirements for each affected source are no less stringent than 40 CFR part 68."
- "MoDNR currently has three employees devoted almost entirely to program implementation."
