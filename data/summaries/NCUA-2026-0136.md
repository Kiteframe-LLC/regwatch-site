# Public Summary: NCUA-2026-0136-0001

_Source file: `summaries/NCUA-2026-0136-0001.md`_
_Detected type: `NPRM`_

## What This Document Does
- Proposes removing 12 C.F.R. § 741.2 — the NCUA’s borrowing cap and waiver process that has applied to federally insured state-chartered credit unions (FISCUs).
- Keeps the statutory 50% borrowing limit for federally chartered credit unions (FCUs) intact.
- Explains the change as deregulatory: the agency says the rule is duplicative or unnecessary for FISCUs and that safety and soundness can be maintained through state law and NCUA’s risk-based supervision rather than a federal numeric cap.
- Docket appears limited to the NPRM text; no supporting technical memoranda, quantitative supervisory analysis, or detailed state-by-state comparison was provided in the materials reviewed.

## Why It Matters
- Removes a uniform federal borrowing cap for a class of institutions, shifting reliance to state regimes and supervisory oversight.
- Could change incentives for FISCUs and state regulators (e.g., borrowing expansion up to state limits; potential charter arbitrage).
- The agency’s core justifications rest on qualitative claims (few waiver requests, supervisory substitution) with little documented empirical support in the provided record.
- Absent comparative risk analysis or monitoring-timeliness evidence, commenters may influence whether the agency adequately considered safety, alternatives, and state heterogeneity before removing an ex ante limit.

## Who Is Affected
- Federally insured state-chartered credit unions (FISCUs): would no longer be subject to §741.2 federal borrowing cap or waiver process.
- Federally chartered credit unions (FCUs): remain subject to the statutory 50% borrowing limit.
- NCUA examiners and state regulators: greater reliance expected on state law and supervisory processes.
- Credit union members and the National Credit Union Share Insurance Fund: potentially affected indirectly through changes in borrowing practices and systemic risk exposure.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Missing empirical evidence on waiver usage
   - Why it matters: The NPRM cites “few waiver requests” as a key factual basis but provides no counts, trends, or outcomes; without this data, it’s hard to evaluate demand for higher borrowing or whether waivers were denied or granted.
   - Practical question to ask: Please provide counts, years, approval/denial rates, reasons, and outcomes for all §741.2 waiver requests since the rule’s adoption.

2. State regulatory heterogeneity not analyzed
   - Why it matters: State laws and limits vary; removing a federal floor could leave some FISCUs subject to weaker constraints, producing uneven risk across jurisdictions.
   - Practical question to ask: Please provide a state-by-state comparison of statutory or regulatory borrowing limits and oversight practices relevant to FISCUs.

3. Assertion that supervision can substitute for a rule lacks timeliness evidence
   - Why it matters: Examinations are periodic; rapid changes in leverage between exams could materialize without a quantitative cap.
   - Practical question to ask: What evidence shows NCUA’s supervisory processes detect and constrain rapid borrowing growth in real time (e.g., monitoring frequency, triggers, historical examples)?

4. Alternatives analysis appears inadequate
   - Why it matters: The record does not show consideration of middle-ground options (simplified waiver, tiered or conditional limits, higher thresholds) that could reduce burden while preserving safeguards.
   - Practical question to ask: Please analyze feasible alternatives (e.g., streamlined waiver, tiered limits based on capital ratios, conditional triggers) and explain why they were rejected.

5. Latent demand and waiver-friction risk
   - Why it matters: Low waiver usage could reflect procedural friction, not lack of need; removing the cap changes that dynamic without measuring latent demand.
   - Practical question to ask: Has NCUA evaluated whether waiver application costs or uncertainty suppressed requests? Provide any evidence or outreach findings.

6. Thin administrative record and procedural vulnerability
   - Why it matters: The docket appears limited to the NPRM text and lacks supporting analyses the agency relies on; that may affect the adequacy of the record for reasoned decisionmaking.
   - Practical question to ask: Please supply the supervisory, economic, and legal analyses that informed the decision, including any internal memos, risk assessments, or state coordination notes.
