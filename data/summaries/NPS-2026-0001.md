# Public Summary: NPS-2026-0001-0001

_Detected type: `NPRM`_

## What This Document Does
- Proposes amending Mammoth Cave National Park special regulations (36 CFR Part 7) to:
  - Allow bicycle use on approximately 37 miles of multi‑use trails in the park.
  - Allow horseback (equestrian) use on the 5.4‑mile Houchin Ferry North/Ollie Road.
- Relies on a park planning record: the Mammoth Cave Trails Plan, an Environmental Assessment (EA), and a Finding of No Significant Impact (FONSI).
- Describes the EA as having evaluated trail surface and soil suitability for bicycle use and concluded impacts are not significant.

## Why It Matters
- Access and recreation: The rule would expand permitted uses, increasing opportunities for bicyclists and equestrians.
- Resource and visitor‑experience risks: Mixed‑use trail expansion can raise risks of erosion, vegetation and wildlife impacts, and user conflicts (hikers vs. bikes vs. horses).
- Procedural posture: NPS issued an EA and FONSI rather than an EIS. The Federal Register summary cites those documents but provides limited quantitative detail (e.g., erosion modeling, visitor‑use projections, maintenance costs) in the preamble itself.
- Comment leverage: The agency’s reliance on the EA/FONSI means targeted requests for specific underlying analyses (modeling, monitoring plans, design standards, adaptive thresholds) are likely to be influential during the rulemaking record.

## Who Is Affected
- Park visitors:
  - Bicyclists (would gain access to ~37 miles of trails).
  - Equestrians (would gain access to Houchin Ferry North/Ollie Road).
  - Hikers and other trail users (may experience new mixed‑use interactions).
- Park resources and wildlife in trail corridors (potentially affected by trail wear, erosion, and disturbance).
- NPS operations and maintenance programs (responsible for monitoring, upkeep, and enforcement).

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Sufficiency of environmental analysis (erosion and resource impacts)
   - Why it matters: The EA/FONSI are the stated basis for the rule. If erosion, soil disturbance, or species‑specific impacts were not modeled in sufficient detail, the agency’s FONSI could be questioned.
   - Practical question to ask: Please provide the EA sections and supporting data showing quantitative erosion/soil‑loss modeling for trails proposed to open to bicycle use.

2. Visitor‑use projections and maintenance cost estimates
   - Why it matters: Increased bike and horse use may raise maintenance needs and budgetary impacts. Transparent projections help assess sustainability.
   - Practical question to ask: What are the projected annual visitor counts by use type for the newly opened routes, and what are projected annual maintenance costs and funding commitments?

3. User‑conflict mitigation and safety planning
   - Why it matters: Mixed‑use trails can generate conflicts and safety risks without clear management (signage, speed limits, separation, or rules).
   - Practical question to ask: What specific measures (signage, speed guidance, separation, enforcement, education) will NPS implement to reduce conflicts among hikers, bicyclists, and equestrians?

4. Monitoring, adaptive management, and thresholds for action
   - Why it matters: Post‑implementation monitoring and clear thresholds are essential to detect harm and trigger remedial action before impacts become significant.
   - Practical question to ask: Will NPS commit to a monitoring plan with specific metrics and pre‑defined thresholds that would prompt temporary or permanent restrictions, and can that plan be included in the administrative record?

5. Consideration of alternative approaches (seasonal access, pilots, single‑use routes)
   - Why it matters: Alternatives such as seasonal access, pilot programs, or separate bike‑only routes could reduce risks while testing impacts.
   - Practical question to ask: Were seasonal or pilot access alternatives (or bike‑only trail options) evaluated in the EA, and if so, please summarize findings or provide the relevant analysis.
