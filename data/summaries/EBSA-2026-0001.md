## What This Document Does

- Proposes a new ERISA disclosure rule for pharmacy benefit managers and affiliated brokers or consultants serving self-insured employer health plans.
- Would require advance disclosure of direct and indirect compensation so plan fiduciaries can judge whether service-provider arrangements are reasonable.
- Pairs disclosure with audit-related provisions so fiduciaries can try to verify whether what they were told is accurate.
- Focuses on self-insured plans, not the broader fully insured market.
- The NPRM text originally gave a March 31, 2026 deadline, but the docket later included an extension notice and the metadata now reflects April 16, 2026.

## Why It Matters

- PBMs sit between plans, drug manufacturers, and pharmacies, and the rule argues that their compensation can be hard for employers to see clearly.
- The Department's theory is straightforward: if plan fiduciaries can see more of the money flows, they can negotiate better contracts, detect conflicts, and challenge unreasonable compensation.
- That matters because the rule is aimed at employer-sponsored prescription-drug spending, where hidden spreads, affiliate payments, and rebate arrangements can distort incentives.
- The proposal is not a direct drug-price cap. It is a transparency-and-oversight rule meant to change bargaining behavior and monitoring.
- The record is strongest when describing why opacity exists. It is weaker when it tries to quantify large downstream benefits from that transparency.

## Who Is Affected

- Fiduciaries of self-insured ERISA group health plans that contract with PBMs or related consultants.
- PBMs themselves, including affiliated entities involved in pharmacy-benefit consulting or brokerage.
- Employers and plan sponsors that rely on outside expertise to negotiate or monitor pharmacy-benefit contracts.
- Workers and covered family members indirectly, because the Department expects better transparency to improve plan purchasing and possibly reduce costs over time.
- Smaller PBMs may be affected differently than large integrated firms, but the supplied record does not fully unpack those differences.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)

- Audit rights may be too vague to deliver real verification.
Why it matters: the rule's logic depends on fiduciaries being able to test whether disclosures are true, not just receive more paperwork.
Question to ask: What minimum records, affiliate data, time periods, and response times must be available for an audit to count as meaningful?

- The benefits case may be harder to reproduce than the NPRM suggests.
Why it matters: the Department presents major monetized benefits, but those appear to depend on a long chain from disclosure to bargaining changes to health and spending improvements.
Question to ask: What are the intermediate assumptions linking disclosure to lower prices, improved adherence, and the stated annualized benefits?

- Average burden estimates may hide big differences across PBMs.
Why it matters: the Department uses 73 PBMs as a baseline and gives average implementation costs, but smaller firms and vertically integrated firms may face very different system and affiliate-data burdens.
Question to ask: What sensitivity analysis did the Department perform for small PBMs, multi-affiliate PBMs, and firms with compensation data stored across separate systems?

- Narrower alternatives are not very visible in the supplied record.
Why it matters: the preamble discusses whether to cover fully insured plans, but commenters can still ask whether the rule should be phased, templated, or initially limited to the most opaque compensation streams.
Question to ask: Why not phase in the rule, standardize a disclosure template first, or target indirect compensation and spread-pricing disclosures before imposing the full package?

## Evidence Quotes

- "These disclosures are needed so that fiduciaries can assess the reasonableness of the contracts or arrangements with these service providers..."
- "The proposed regulation also includes audit provisions designed to ensure that the responsible plan fiduciaries... can verify the accuracy of the disclosures."
- "The spread pricing model presents challenges for responsible plan fiduciaries in evaluating costs because there is no agreed upon AWP for a given drug."
