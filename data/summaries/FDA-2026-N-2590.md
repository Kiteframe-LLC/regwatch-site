## What This Document Does
- FDA proposes moving a category of tuberculosis blood tests from the most burdensome device pathway, `PMA`, into `class II` with special controls and continued `510(k)` review.
- The tests covered here are qualitative immune-response assays used as an aid in diagnosing *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* infection. They can help detect infection, but they do not by themselves distinguish latent from active disease.
- FDA would create a new regulation, `21 CFR 866.3371`, and use that regulation to set the special controls future products must meet.
- Those controls are not trivial. They would require detailed labeling, explanation of how raw signals become reported results, critical-reagent documentation, lot-release criteria, analytical studies, and multisite clinical studies.
- FDA says the downgrade is justified by older PMA data it can legally use, multiple advisory-panel discussions, clinical guidelines, and postmarket experience, while also keeping FDA review of future products through `510(k)`.

## Why It Matters
- This is a meaningful oversight change. It lowers the entry pathway from PMA to `510(k)`, which can make it easier for new tests or modified tests to reach market.
- At the same time, FDA is trying to avoid a free pass. The proposal keeps review in place and tries to preserve rigor by writing detailed special controls into the regulation itself.
- That means the quality of those special controls matters a lot. If they are too vague, the downgrade could weaken evidence expectations in practice. If they are well defined, the rule could reduce unnecessary burden while still protecting patients.
- The highest-stakes issue is not whether FDA has authority to do this. It likely does. The real question is whether the public record clearly explains why these particular controls are enough for populations where test interpretation is hard, including immunosuppressed patients and patients with confounding conditions.
- This is also one of those rules where the public-facing record is thinner than the agency's internal or referenced evidence base. FDA cites PMAs, advisory panels, practice guidelines, and postmarket databases, but the proposal does not give readers a simple crosswalk showing how each source supports each special control.

## Who Is Affected
- Diagnostic manufacturers that sell or want to develop TB immune-response assays.
- Clinical laboratories and healthcare systems that rely on these tests as part of TB screening and diagnostic workups.
- Clinicians and public-health programs that use the tests for populations with different pre-test probabilities and different risks from false negatives, false positives, or indeterminate results.
- Patients in higher-risk groups, especially immunosuppressed people, people vaccinated with BCG, and people whose clinical picture could be confused by nontuberculous mycobacterial infections.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
- **Evidence-to-control mapping is too implicit.**
Why it matters: FDA says it has enough evidence from PMAs, panels, guidelines, and postmarket data, but it does not clearly show which evidence supports which special control.
Question a commenter can ask: Can FDA publish a table linking each cited evidence source to each numbered special control in proposed `21 CFR 866.3371`?

- **Subgroup validation expectations need more detail.**
Why it matters: The proposal correctly says clinical studies must include higher-risk or harder-to-interpret populations, but it does not say what level of evidence in those subgroups will actually be enough.
Question a commenter can ask: What minimum performance and sample expectations does FDA expect for immunosuppressed, BCG-vaccinated, and nontuberculous-mycobacterial populations in future `510(k)` submissions?

- **The postmarket safety discussion needs context.**
Why it matters: FDA relies on a low number of adverse-event reports and recalls as support for reduced premarket burden, but counts alone are hard to interpret without usage volume and product-specific context.
Question a commenter can ask: Can FDA provide denominator or market-exposure context for the `27` MDRs and explain how those reports were evaluated across the covered product codes?

- **The rule does not explain why stronger class II tools were unnecessary.**
Why it matters: FDA notes that class II special controls can include surveillance-type tools, but the proposal does not explain why labeling and validation requirements alone are the right package.
Question a commenter can ask: Why did FDA decide not to include stronger postmarket tools, such as surveillance or more explicit follow-up expectations, for higher-risk populations?
