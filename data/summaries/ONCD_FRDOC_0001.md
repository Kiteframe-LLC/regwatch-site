## What This Document Does
- ONCD is proposing its first formal `FOIA` and `Privacy Act` regulations.
- The draft would create new `32 CFR` parts `2200` and `2201` that explain how people can request records, appeal denials, seek fee waivers, ask for Privacy Act access or corrections, and get an accounting of disclosures.
- A lot of the proposal is standard agency housekeeping. It names the ONCD General Counsel as Chief FOIA Officer, provides for a FOIA Public Liaison, and sets out processing, appeal, and fee rules.
- The most important parts for commenters are the requester-facing restrictions built into the draft text itself. The rule says requests must be made in English, bars email attachments and web links, allows administrative closure if a requester does not answer a clarification request within `30` calendar days, and requires sensitive identity information to be submitted through ONCD-approved processes described outside the rule text.
- The proposal also contains inconsistent contact details across sections, including different email addresses and different mailing ZIP codes for related FOIA and Privacy Act functions.

## Why It Matters
- This is not a major economic or technical rule, but it directly affects how the public uses transparency and privacy rights against ONCD.
- Because the record is thin and there are no supporting attachments, the key question is not whether ONCD forgot to include a big study. The key question is whether the actual draft procedures are clear, justified, and workable for ordinary requesters.
- The strongest part of the proposal is that it is concrete. ONCD is not being vague about having procedures; it gives actual draft regulatory text.
- The weaker part is that several restrictive choices are stated without much explanation. The draft does not explain why all requests must be in English, why attachments and web links must be banned instead of handled through a safer channel, or what counts as an "approved" process for sending identity information.
- That matters because a requester-facing rule can become harder to use if those details are unclear. Even small ambiguities can lead to delay, misdirected requests, or avoidable privacy risk.

## Who Is Affected
- Journalists, researchers, watchdog groups, and members of the public who may seek ONCD records under FOIA.
- Individuals asking for access to, amendment of, or an accounting of records about themselves under the Privacy Act.
- Small organizations and solo requesters who may have less administrative capacity to navigate rigid intake rules.
- People with limited English proficiency or limited digital literacy who may be more affected by the proposal's access conditions.
- ONCD staff, because inconsistent contact instructions and undefined submission channels can also create internal processing confusion.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
- **The English-only request rule is thinly explained.**
Why it matters: A blanket language rule may deter or complicate requests from people who do not write comfortably in English, even when they are trying to exercise basic access rights.
Question a commenter can ask: Why is an English-only requirement necessary, and what lower-burden accommodations did ONCD consider for requesters with limited English proficiency?

- **The no-attachment and no-link rule may be broader than necessary.**
Why it matters: Some requesters need to submit detailed lists, authorizations, or other materials to describe records precisely. A categorical ban could make accurate requests harder.
Question a commenter can ask: Can ONCD explain the security rationale in more detail and consider a screened upload method or another secure alternative instead of an outright ban?

- **The rule relies on undefined "approved" identity-submission processes.**
Why it matters: Privacy Act requesters may need to send sensitive identity information, but the draft leaves key compliance instructions to an outside webpage rather than spelling out the minimum required channels in the rule.
Question a commenter can ask: What approved identity-verification processes will ONCD maintain, and how will requesters be protected if web instructions change or are unavailable?

- **The proposal contains inconsistent contact information.**
Why it matters: A requester-facing regulation should not make people guess which email address or mailing address is correct.
Question a commenter can ask: Will ONCD reconcile all addresses, phone numbers, and email contacts across the FOIA and Privacy Act sections before issuing a final rule?

- **The `30`-day administrative-closure rule deserves more explanation.**
Why it matters: Some requesters may miss clarification emails or need more time to respond, especially if they are unrepresented or dealing with complex records.
Question a commenter can ask: Why is `30` days the right deadline, and should ONCD provide a reminder notice or a more flexible cure process before closing a request?
