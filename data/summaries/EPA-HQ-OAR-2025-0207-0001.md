# Public Summary: EPA-HQ-OAR-2025-0207-0001

_Source file: `summaries/EPA-HQ-OAR-2025-0207-0001.md`_

## What This Rule Does
- Updates the Marine Tank Vessel Loading Operations (MTVLO) air toxics standards under Clean Air Act §112(d)(6) based on EPA’s technology review.
- Proposes:
  - Enhanced flare monitoring requirements.
  - Periodic performance testing and an annual relative accuracy test audit (RATA) requirement.
  - Electronic reporting of required compliance data.
  - Removal of startup, shutdown, and malfunction (SSM) exemptions.
- Maintains the existing applicability thresholds (10 or 25 tons per year of hazardous air pollutant emissions, depending on pollutant grouping).
- Proposes no change to the current vapor pressure exemption after finding tighter controls not cost‑reasonable.
- Evaluates alternatives for leak detection and repair (LDAR) and other monitoring approaches to determine whether to expand monitoring requirements.

## Why It Matters
- Compliance assurance: More monitoring, testing, and electronic reporting can improve verification that controls are working, potentially reducing hazardous air pollutant (HAP) emissions during marine vessel loading.
- Scope of regulation: Keeping current applicability thresholds and the vapor pressure exemption directly affects which terminals must install or upgrade controls.
- Costs and burdens: New testing (including annual RATA) and enhanced flare monitoring can add operational and reporting costs; EPA’s cost‑reasonableness determinations influence what requirements are adopted.
- Community health: Marine loading often occurs near ports and communities; improved monitoring and testing could reduce exposure risks if they lead to better control performance.
- Regulatory clarity: Removing SSM exemptions and standardizing electronic reporting modernize compliance and may reduce ambiguity.

## Who Is Affected
- Owners and operators of facilities that load marine tank vessels with cargoes containing hazardous air pollutants (e.g., marine terminals, tank farms with marine loading).
- Entities using flares or other control devices for marine loading emissions.
- Compliance staff, testing contractors, and monitoring equipment vendors supporting MTVLO facilities.
- State, local, and tribal air agencies receiving electronic reports.
- Nearby communities that may be exposed to HAP emissions from marine loading operations.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1) Cost benchmark for keeping the vapor pressure exemption unchanged
- Why it matters: EPA rejected tightening the exemption as “not reasonable” based on cost per ton of HAP reduced, but the benchmark and its consistency with other MACT rules are not clearly explained in the preamble text provided.
- Practical question: What cost-effectiveness threshold and assumptions did EPA use, and how does it compare with thresholds used in other recent §112 rules?

2) Basis for keeping the 10/25 tpy applicability thresholds
- Why it matters: Thresholds determine which facilities must comply; if emissions have shifted to smaller sources or costs have fallen, maintaining current thresholds could under‑regulate significant emissions.
- Practical question: What current emissions distribution and facility‑size data support retaining the thresholds, and did EPA analyze the impacts of alternative thresholds?

3) Quantified benefits of enhanced monitoring and periodic testing (including annual RATA)
- Why it matters: EPA proposes added testing and monitoring but the expected emissions reductions or compliance improvements are not clearly quantified in the provided text; without quantification, burden–benefit tradeoffs are hard to assess.
- Practical question: What emissions or compliance benefits does EPA project from the proposed flare monitoring, periodic performance tests, and annual RATA, and what data support the test frequency?

4) Consideration of intermediate or risk‑tiered alternatives
- Why it matters: The record indicates EPA evaluated options (e.g., LDAR approaches, monitoring), but it is not clear whether EPA fully assessed middle‑ground options (e.g., risk‑tiered monitoring, continuous monitoring for certain devices, shorter test intervals for higher emitters).
- Practical question: Did EPA analyze and compare intermediate options (such as tiered requirements by throughput or risk), and what were the results relative to the selected approach?

5) Transparency of data, modeling, and sensitivity analyses
- Why it matters: EPA references technology review, emissions analysis, and cost‑effectiveness, but full workbooks, modeling assumptions, and sensitivity tests are not visible in the provided text; transparency aids public review and reduces litigation risk.
- Practical question: Will EPA place the emissions inventory, cost workbooks, control efficiency assumptions, and sensitivity analyses in the docket, and identify how uncertainties affect conclusions?
