# Public Summary: OCC-2026-0001-0001

## What This Document Does
- The OCC proposes to replace its current guidance-based bank appeals process with formal regulatory text in 12 CFR part 4.
- The proposal would create an Appeals Board made up of the Chief National Bank Examiner and two nonrenewable term appointees, apply de novo review to appeals, allow stays in some cases, and move the Ombudsman into a liaison / anti-retaliation / reporting role.
- The OCC says these changes are meant to make appeals more independent, transparent, and credible for regulated institutions.

## Why It Matters
- This rule affects how banks challenge OCC supervisory determinations such as examination ratings, loan ratings, some fair-lending referrals, licensing decisions, and other material supervisory conclusions.
- The proposal is built around a real concern: the OCC says that in 2024 it supervised 1,040 institutions but only 11 appeals were filed.
- The main record question is not whether the OCC may reform its process. It is whether the OCC has shown that this specific design will actually improve independence and confidence.

## Who Is Affected
- National banks, federal savings associations, certain foreign bank branches and agencies, stablecoin issuers subject to OCC authority, and some institution-affiliated parties.
- Smaller community banks that may face different practical costs when deciding whether to appeal.
- OCC supervisory staff, the Ombudsman's office, and any future term appointees serving on the Appeals Board.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Issue: Whether low appeal volume really proves the current process is failing.
Why it matters: The OCC cites low usage and high uphold rates, but the docket packet does not clearly show whether banks avoid appeals because of unfairness, retaliation fears, informal resolution success, or some other reason.
Practical question to ask OCC: What evidence shows that current design defects, rather than normal supervisory dynamics, are the main reason so few appeals are filed?

2. Issue: Whether the proposed Appeals Board is meaningfully more independent than the current structure.
Why it matters: The proposal adds term appointees, but it also keeps the Chief National Bank Examiner on the board, gives the Comptroller appointment power, and lets the Comptroller overturn board decisions.
Practical question to ask OCC: Why is this board design more independent than a strengthened Ombudsman model or a board made entirely of non-current OCC decisionmakers?

3. Issue: Whether outside employment or short one-year terms for appointees create conflict or consistency problems.
Why it matters: The OCC asks whether term appointees might serve part time and hold outside jobs. That could undermine the proposal's own fairness rationale if ethics safeguards are weak.
Practical question to ask OCC: What disclosure, recusal, and outside-employment restrictions will apply, and why is a one-year nonrenewable term the right balance?

4. Issue: Whether publication of redacted decisions will create usable precedent.
Why it matters: Publishing opinions can improve transparency only if they are timely, sufficiently reasoned, and not redacted into uselessness.
Practical question to ask OCC: Will the agency commit to publication deadlines, searchable indexing, and minimum explanatory content for published decisions and dissents?

5. Issue: Whether the small-entity analysis is fully convincing.
Why it matters: The OCC predicts more appeals under the new system but also says the rule will not significantly burden a substantial number of small entities.
Practical question to ask OCC: Please provide a sensitivity analysis showing how additional appeals, legal costs, and management time would affect smaller institutions.

## Evidence Quotes
- “Indeed, in 2024, the OCC supervised 1,040 institutions and only 11 appeals were filed with the Ombudsman...”
- “An underlying reason for this perception could be that the OCC has not clearly articulated a de novo standard of review for appeals.”
- “This change is being proposed to increase confidence in the independence of the decision making on appeals.”
- “The proposal would require a redacted version of the Appeals Board's decision to be published.”
- “The Comptroller may overturn any decision by the Appeals Board.”

## Bottom Line
This looks like a legitimate and consequential process rule, not a trivial housekeeping action. The strongest public comments will probably be constructive rather than oppositional: ask the OCC to better prove why current appeals are underused, justify why this exact board structure is the right independence model, and show more clearly how the rule's expected increase in appeals will affect small institutions.
