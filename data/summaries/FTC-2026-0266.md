## What This Document Does
- The Commission is seeking evidence to inform possible rulemaking under FTC Act §18 on rental-housing pricing and fee disclosure.  
- The agency’s working policy direction is whether to require “clear and conspicuous disclosure of the total rent including all mandatory fees whenever consumers are quoted a price.”  
- The request targets market structure, how advertised rent is determined (including the meaning of “base rent”), prevalence of partial-price advertising, and the costs and mechanics of compliance for providers and platforms.  
- The agency explicitly asks commenters for data, prevalence estimates, examples, and cost information to shape rule design and enforcement.

## Why It Matters
- Pricing transparency affects everyday housing decisions for millions of renters who compare listings and budgets based on advertised rents.  
- If mandatory fees are routinely excluded from advertised rent, advertised price comparisons can be misleading and materially understate the cost of housing.  
- A federal rule could standardize disclosure nationwide (and enable FTC enforcement), but it may also change landlord pricing behavior and impose compliance costs—especially on small landlords—so the potential tradeoffs are important.  
- The record is likely to be shaped by parties with the best data (large landlords, listing platforms), creating evidence asymmetries that commenters should try to correct.

## Who Is Affected
- Renters and tenant advocates (consumers who rely on advertised prices).  
- Large institutional landlords, corporate property managers, REITs.  
- Small landlords and individual property owners (may face different compliance burdens).  
- Rental listing platforms and property-management software vendors (data holders and distribution channels).  
- State and local regulators (many have existing disclosure rules; interaction matters).  
- Housing economists and researchers who can supply prevalence and impact data.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Issue: Require total-rent disclosure (all mandatory fees included in advertised rent)  
   - Why it matters: Directly addresses the FTC’s stated problem that advertised prices may omit mandatory charges, improving apples-to-apples comparisons for consumers.  
   - Practical question to ask: Should the rule require that any quoted or advertised rent reflect the total recurring monthly payment including all mandatory recurring fees, and if not, what alternative standard would provide equivalent consumer clarity?

2. Issue: Definition and scope of “base rent” and “total rent”  
   - Why it matters: The rule’s effect depends on clear, operational definitions; inconsistent definitions would undermine enforcement and compliance.  
   - Practical question to ask: What specific items (recurring fees, one-time fees, utilities, deposits) should be included in the statutory definition of “total rent” or “base rent”?

3. Issue: Burden on small landlords and compliance cost differences  
   - Why it matters: Compliance costs and recordkeeping burdens may be asymmetric, potentially affecting small operators more heavily and influencing supply.  
   - Practical question to ask: What quantifiable capital and labor costs would small landlords incur to comply, and are safe-harbor templates or phased compliance appropriate?

4. Issue: Responsibility of listing platforms vs. originators of listings  
   - Why it matters: Platforms often host listings originating from landlords or managers; accountability and verification duties affect enforcement practicality.  
   - Practical question to ask: Should the rule assign primary disclosure responsibility to the listing originator, the platform, or both, and what verification obligations (if any) should platforms have?

5. Issue: Interaction with existing state and local disclosure laws  
   - Why it matters: Many jurisdictions already regulate rental fee disclosure; federal rule design should clarify preemption, overlap, or accommodation to avoid conflicts.  
   - Practical question to ask: How should a federal disclosure rule interact with state and local laws that already require or prohibit specific fee disclosures?

6. Issue: Potential market effects on rent-setting and housing supply (missing in record)  
   - Why it matters: Transparency mandates can influence pricing strategies and landlord behavior, with possible effects on housing availability and affordability; the ANPRM appears to solicit limited input on supply impacts.  
   - Practical question to ask: What empirical evidence shows how full-price disclosure affects rent levels, vacancy rates, and new supply decisions?
