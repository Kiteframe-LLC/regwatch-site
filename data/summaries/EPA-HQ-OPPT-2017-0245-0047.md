# Public Summary: EPA-HQ-OPPT-2017-0245-0047

_Source file: `summaries/EPA-HQ-OPPT-2017-0245-0047.md`_

## What This Rule Does
- Updates the Code of Federal Regulations to incorporate the current editions of several voluntary consensus standards used in the TSCA Title VI formaldehyde program, replacing outdated, superseded, or withdrawn versions.
- Adds ISO 12460-2:2024(en) as an additional small‑scale chamber quality‑control (QC) test method for composite wood products, citing similarity to existing QC methods and expanded analytical options (e.g., laser absorption spectroscopy).
- Requests public comment on whether to retain the British adoption (BS EN ISO 12460-3:2023) when an identical international version (ISO 12460-3:2023) is also proposed.
- States the action is not a “significant regulatory action” under Executive Order 12866, creates no new reporting or recordkeeping, and imposes no additional costs.

Informational only; not legal advice.

## Why It Matters
- Test method versions can change lab procedures, tolerances, or acceptance criteria. Even minor updates may affect QC workflows, training, SOPs, and comparability of results across methods and facilities.
- Adding a new optional method can improve flexibility but may raise comparability and enforcement questions if different instruments or analytical pathways (e.g., laser-based) yield systematically different results.
- Keeping both ISO and BSI versions of an identical standard can help some users but may create redundancy or confusion about interchangeability.
- EPA’s “no additional costs/no new burden” conclusion may be correct for many entities, but commenters may wish to document any transition, acquisition, validation, or training costs—especially for small entities and laboratories.

## Who Is Affected
- Entities in the composite wood products supply chain subject to TSCA Title VI, including:
  - Manufacturers, importers, fabricators, distributors, and retailers of covered composite wood products and finished goods.
  - Third-party certifiers and testing laboratories supporting formaldehyde compliance.
  - Quality managers and compliance staff at mills and downstream facilities.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1) Adequacy of “no additional costs” conclusion
- Why it matters: Transition steps (purchasing updated standards, updating SOPs, validating comparability, retraining, or qualifying new instruments) can create costs even when standards are “similar.”
- Practical question: What evidence (e.g., comparison of old vs. new procedures, estimated staff time, instrument changes) supports EPA’s conclusion that stakeholders will face no additional costs?

2) “Substantially similar” basis for updated standards
- Why it matters: This assertion underpins the small-entity impact and low-burden posture; without a side‑by‑side, it is hard to verify.
- Practical question: Will EPA provide a concise comparison or redline summary showing material versus non‑material changes between each previously incorporated standard and its updated edition?

3) Comparability and QA/QC for ISO 12460-2:2024(en)
- Why it matters: The new method allows a wider range of analytical approaches (including laser absorption spectroscopy). Without clear comparability guardrails, results may vary across labs and instruments.
- Practical question: What data or protocols will EPA rely on to ensure results from ISO 12460-2:2024(en) are comparable to existing approved QC methods for compliance and enforcement?

4) Whether to retain duplicate BSI and ISO standards
- Why it matters: Duplicating identical standards can create redundancy or ambiguity; removing one may simplify compliance and the CFR text.
- Practical question: If EPA retains both BS EN ISO 12460-3:2023 and ISO 12460-3:2023, how will the Agency ensure users treat them as fully interchangeable and avoid confusion?

5) Small-entity transition expectations
- Why it matters: Even minimal updates can be more burdensome for small businesses or small labs.
- Practical question: Will EPA clarify whether any transition period, guidance, or technical assistance is planned to help small entities adjust SOPs, training, or instrumentation if they adopt updated standards or the new ISO method?

## Evidence Quotes
- “The Agency is proposing this action to update the incorporation by reference for several voluntary consensus standards at 40 CFR 770.99 to their current editions to address outdated, superseded, and withdrawn standards that have been updated between 2022 and 2024.”
- “EPA anticipates no additional costs to stakeholders associated with this notice of proposed rulemaking for updated standards.”
- EPA proposes to add ISO 12460-2:2024(en) “due to its similarity to other standards currently incorporated by reference for quality control methods, and to allow regulated entities to make use of the wider range of analytical methods the standard allows, such as laser absorption spectroscopy.”
- “Industry has requested including the latest testing methodology which allows for greater industry flexibility in choosing quality control methods that best fit their circumstances.”
- “This action is not a significant regulatory action under Executive Order 12866….”
- “This action does not impose any new information collection burden under the PRA… This action does not create any new reporting or recordkeeping obligations.”
- “The updated versions of the standards are substantially similar to the previous versions.”
- EPA seeks comment on whether BS EN ISO 12460-3:2023 “should be retained in the final rule or whether BS EN ISO 12460-3 should be removed… given that an identical, international version (ISO 12460-3:2023) is also proposed.”
