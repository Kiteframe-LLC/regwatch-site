## What This Document Does
- EPA proposes to approve Michigan's Part 8 NOx rules into the federally enforceable SIP for the Western Michigan ozone nonattainment areas.
- EPA says these rules satisfy Moderate-stage NOx RACT requirements under the 2015 ozone standard.
- The covered rules address combustion engines, boilers, combustion turbines, hot mix asphalt plants, process heaters, engine test cells, lime kilns, and glass manufacturing.
- EPA is not approving every submitted Part 8 provision in this notice. It would approve `R 336.1840`, `336.1841(1)-(7)`, `336.1842(1)-336.1842(9)`, `336.1843`, `336.1844(1)-336.1844(8)`, and `336.1845`, while deferring `R 336.1841(8)`, `336.1842(10)`, and `336.1844(9)`.
- The same Federal Register notice also discusses the related Western Michigan VOC docket, but this docket is the NOx side of that combined action.

## Why It Matters
- This proposal determines which Michigan NOx rules become federally enforceable for Western Michigan. That matters for both regulated facilities and communities concerned about ozone-forming emissions.
- EPA's reasoning is heavily comparison-based. The preamble says Michigan analyzed other states' recent RACT regulations and combustion-unit operating profiles, and that EPA compared Michigan's rules to other states' NOx rules and ACT guidance.
- EPA's key conclusion is that Michigan's rules are "in some cases more stringent than other states' NOx RACT rules and consistent with the ACTs." EPA also says it has not identified any new reasonably available control technologies for these sources.
- The main issue is transparency, not obvious lack of authority. The export package shows a supporting TSD and state submittals in the docket, but none were ingested for this run. A recent attachment manifest exists and does not mark any supporting documents as required, so the workflow can proceed, but the public-facing package still does not show the comparison tables or benchmark logic behind EPA's core conclusion.
- The timing also matters. EPA says Western Michigan was reclassified to Serious nonattainment effective January 16, 2025, and that the Serious RACT SIP was due on January 1, 2026. Even so, this proposal addresses only Moderate-area RACT. That does not make the action invalid by itself, but it creates a fair question about whether EPA should explain more clearly how this approval fits with the area's current status.

## Who Is Affected
- Facilities in the Western Michigan nonattainment areas that operate combustion sources covered by Michigan's Part 8 rules.
- Michigan regulators, because approved state rules become part of the federally enforceable SIP.
- Nearby communities and public-health advocates, because the rule affects the level of NOx control EPA is willing to accept for ozone planning.
- Future commenters in related SIP actions, because this proposal shows how EPA may rely on cross-state comparisons and ACT guidance instead of a more detailed engineering discussion in the preamble.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. EPA should publish the comparison tables it relies on
   - Why it matters: EPA's approval depends on comparisons to other states' NOx rules and ACT guidance, but those comparisons are not visible here.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Will EPA add a public side-by-side table showing the source categories, the comparison benchmarks, and where Michigan is less stringent, equivalent, or more stringent?

2. The "no new reasonably available controls" conclusion is too compressed
   - Why it matters: EPA says no new control technologies were identified, but does not show what technologies were reviewed or how feasibility was judged.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: What technologies, source categories, and feasibility criteria did EPA review before concluding that no newer controls are reasonably available?

3. EPA should explain the deferred Part 8 subrules more clearly
   - Why it matters: EPA is not acting on three specific Part 8 provisions, which raises obvious questions about completeness and practical enforceability.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: What is the practical effect of deferring `R 336.1841(8)`, `R 336.1842(10)`, and `R 336.1844(9)`?

4. EPA should address the Moderate-versus-Serious timing issue directly
   - Why it matters: The preamble says the area is already Serious, but this proposal still addresses only Moderate RACT.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: How does this approval fit with Western Michigan's current Serious-area obligations, and should commenters expect a follow-on action for the newer SIP requirements?

5. EPA should clarify how alternative RACT pathways will work
   - Why it matters: Alternative or source-specific RACT pathways can be reasonable, but they need enough public explanation to ensure transparency and enforceability.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: When will source-specific or alternative NOx RACT determinations be required, and how will those determinations become part of the SIP?

## Evidence Quotes
- "Michigan's Serious RACT SIP was due on January 1, 2026, but this action addresses RACT for Moderate nonattainment only."
- "Michigan evaluated other States' recent RACT regulations and analyzed emissions and operational profiles of combustion units at major source facilities in Michigan to determine RACT requirements for these categories."
- "The EPA found Michigan's rules to be in some cases more stringent than other states' NO X RACT rules and consistent with the ACTs."
- "The EPA has not identified any new control technologies that are reasonably available considering technological and economic feasibility for these sources."
