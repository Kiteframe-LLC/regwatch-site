## What This Document Does
- Proposes to set an effective date for premarket approval (PMA) requirements that apply to "blood irradiators intended to prevent metastasis."
- Focuses on timing and compliance deadlines rather than changing device classification in the text provided.
- The public docket as provided does not include supporting technical, clinical, or economic analyses tied to the timing decision.

## Why It Matters
- PMA is the most burdensome FDA pathway for medical devices. Setting an effective date triggers significant preparation costs and regulatory timelines for affected firms.
- Without visible risk or impact analysis, the rationale for applying PMA-level review to this device subset and the chosen timeline is unclear.
- Possible real-world consequences include: supply disruptions, smaller manufacturers exiting the market, labeling changes to avoid the rule, and delays in patient access.
- Commenters can increase regulatory transparency by asking FDA to supply missing evidence and to consider less disruptive alternatives.

## Who Is Affected
- Manufacturers of blood irradiators, especially small and medium-sized firms that may lack PMA resources.
- Hospitals, blood centers, and clinicians who use blood irradiators to manage transfusion-related risks.
- Patients who rely on services that use these devices.
- FDA staff and reviewers tasked with PMA workload and review capacity.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Issue: Lack of explicit risk justification for PMA level

2. Issue: Ambiguity in scope — "intended to prevent metastasis"
   - Why it matters: A vague intended-use phrase can be narrowed or altered in labeling to evade regulation, or applied inconsistently across device variants.
   - Practical question to ask: How will FDA define and operationalize "intended to prevent metastasis" for enforcement and premarket decisions? Provide concrete examples and boundary tests.

3. Issue: Rationale for chosen effective date and compliance timeline
   - Why it matters: PMA preparation and submission are time- and resource-intensive. Unrealistic timelines could cause market exits or shortages.

4. Issue: Lack of alternatives analysis (phased, conditional, subclassification)

   - Practical question to ask: What alternatives did FDA consider (phased deadlines, conditional pathways, subclassification) and why were they rejected? Provide comparative impact analysis.
