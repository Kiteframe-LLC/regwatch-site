# Public Summary: EPA-HQ-OLEM-2025-3456-0001

## What This Document Does
- Proposes to eliminate acceptance of paper hazardous-waste manifests and require the use of EPA’s electronic manifest (e‑Manifest) system as the exclusive compliance pathway.
- Sets a single compliance deadline of 24 months after the rule’s effective date for all regulated parties to stop using paper manifests.
- Removes the existing manifest registry regulations and adds broker reporting obligations tied to the e‑Manifest system.
- Provides supporting materials including an Economic Assessment and an Information Collection Request; other analytical documents (e.g., technical support, adoption modeling, sensitivity analysis) were not provided in the materials reviewed.

## Why It Matters
- Converts an optional electronic system into the only lawful way to document hazardous-waste shipments, a major operational shift for generators, transporters, and treatment/disposal facilities.
- EPA asserts benefits (reduced administrative burden, better data quality, cost savings), but the record lacks quantified analysis demonstrating those benefits or how the 24‑month timeline is feasible given current low adoption.
- The transition could disproportionately affect small, rural, or low‑volume entities with limited IT capacity or connectivity, and creates operational risk if the electronic system is unavailable and no paper fallback remains.
- The strength of EPA’s justification and the adequacy of its analysis are central to commenters who want to influence rule design or challenge procedural adequacy.

## Who Is Affected
- Hazardous-waste generators of all sizes (large facilities and small/remote generators).
- Transporters and brokers who create, manage, or rely on manifests.
- Treatment, storage, and disposal facilities (TSDFs) that receive manifests.
- State agencies that implement manifest regulations and track shipments.
- Parties that currently rely on the manifest registry or paper-based fallback processes.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Issue: The 24‑month compliance timeline lacks documented analytical support.
   - Why it matters: Moving from reported <1% e‑Manifest use to 100% in two years is a major behavioral and technical shift; an unsupported deadline risks noncompliance and operational disruption.
   - Practical question to ask: Please provide the modeling, assumptions, and empirical basis showing how a 24‑month deadline produces near‑universal compliance across entity sizes and geographies.

2. Issue: Insufficient, disaggregated cost and burden analysis.
   - Why it matters: Aggregate cost claims do not show how costs fall on small vs. large generators, rural vs. urban entities, or on brokers, which affects fairness and feasibility.
   - Practical question to ask: Provide a breakdown of expected costs, by entity type and size, including one‑time onboarding costs and recurring expenses.

3. Issue: Missing phased, tiered, or conditional alternatives (e.g., phased implementation, exceptions).
   - Why it matters: A single nationwide sunset may not account for uneven readiness; phased approaches or conditional triggers could reduce disruption while still increasing e‑Manifest use.
   - Practical question to ask: Explain why EPA rejected phased or threshold‑triggered approaches and provide analysis showing those alternatives were assessed and found infeasible.

4. Issue: No documented plan for system outages, offline operations, or emergency/edge cases.
   - Why it matters: Eliminating paper without a robust offline/fallback policy risks gaps in tracking during outages, natural disasters, or cross‑jurisdictional movements.
   - Practical question to ask: Describe contingency procedures, acceptable offline workflows, and the impact assessment for system downtime.

5. Issue: Removal of the manifest registry is asserted without evidence of sufficiency of the e‑Manifest fallback.
   - Why it matters: Decommissioning the registry eliminates an established mechanism; without evidence the e‑Manifest fully covers registry functions, there may be data or administrative gaps.
   - Practical question to ask: Provide an analysis comparing current registry functions to e‑Manifest features and identify any residual functions that would be lost or need replacement.

6. Issue: Claimed reductions in administrative burden and cost savings lack quantified support.
   - Why it matters: Benefits are central to cost‑benefit justification; assertions without quantification undermine the rule’s evidentiary basis.
   - Practical question to ask: Submit the empirical basis (data, case studies, pilot results) used to estimate administrative burden reductions and net cost impacts.
