## What This Document Does
- EPA proposes to approve Arizona's revised Hayden SO2 nonattainment plan for both the 1971 and 2010 sulfur dioxide standards.
- The proposal says Arizona's updated package now covers the full required set of SIP elements: attainment demonstration, reasonable further progress, RACM/RACT, emissions inventories, nonattainment NSR, emissions limits, and contingency measures.
- EPA also proposes to approve amendments to revised Rule B1302 and Appendix 14, plus amendments to Rules 715 and 715.01 that would sunset older requirements.
- If finalized, EPA says the sanctions triggered by the 2020 disapprovals would end and the Hayden FIP obligation would also end.

## Why It Matters
- This proposal is the latest attempt to fix a regulatory failure EPA already identified. In 2020, EPA found Arizona's earlier Hayden plan and Rule B1302 deficient, especially because the rule did not include numeric limits and ongoing monitoring that matched the fugitive emissions used in the attainment modeling.
- The NPRM matters because it would close out a long enforcement and planning problem for the Hayden smelter area. EPA previously found Arizona missed deadlines, failed to attain the 2010 1-hour SO2 standard by the original date, and triggered both sanctions and a federal backstop obligation.
- The rule cites a real technical record, including a 2023 Hayden SO2 Plan, a 2025 supplement, and EPA/ADEQ technical support documents.
- The caution is that much of that support is cited rather than visible here, so some important details still need clearer public-facing explanation.
- The strongest comments are likely to be technical and procedural: whether the Arizona rule package really fixes the earlier defects and still provides for attainment if the smelter resumes operation.

## Who Is Affected
- ASARCO and the Hayden Smelter, which EPA identifies as the dominant SO2 source in the nonattainment area.
- Arizona environmental regulators, who need EPA approval for the revised state implementation plan.
- Residents and workers near Hayden who are affected by SO2 air quality and enforcement decisions.
- Commenters focused on SIP enforceability, technical modeling, and anti-backsliding protections.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Issue: EPA should show exactly how the revised rule cures the 2020 defects.
   - Why it matters: EPA says the old rule failed because it lacked numeric limits and monitoring tied to modeled fugitive emissions. The new NPRM says those problems are fixed, but the reviewed text does not provide a simple defect-to-fix crosswalk.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Please identify each deficiency EPA found in the 2020 Hayden actions and point to the exact revised Rule B1302 or Appendix 14 language that cures it.

2. Issue: The longer-term averaging approach needs more public-facing support.
   - Why it matters: EPA relies on 24-hour and 30-day adjustment factors to justify longer-term limits as comparably stringent to a 1-hour critical emission value, but the underlying dataset and sensitivity analysis are not visible here.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Please place in the docket the historical emissions data, exclusion criteria, and sensitivity analysis used to derive the 0.69 and 0.74 adjustment factors.

3. Issue: The smelter shutdown creates a representativeness question.
   - Why it matters: EPA notes the Hayden Smelter shut down on October 13, 2019 and had not resumed operations as of this NPRM. A long shutdown can weaken confidence that historical operating data still represent future restart conditions.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: What analysis shows that restart-era emissions behavior would remain within the assumptions used for the attainment demonstration and comparable-stringency limits?

4. Issue: EPA's anti-backsliding explanation for sunsetting Rules 715 and 715.01 is thin in the preamble.
   - Why it matters: EPA says those amendments will not relax SIP obligations because relevant conditions were moved into revised Rule B1302, but commenters need a clearer comparison to test that claim under Clean Air Act sections 110(l) and 193.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Please provide a side-by-side comparison showing why the sunset of Rules 715 and 715.01 preserves equivalent or greater emissions control obligations.

5. Issue: Enforcement-based contingency measures may deserve a harder look.
   - Why it matters: EPA proposes to treat Arizona's general enforcement program as an adequate contingency measure. If future violations occur after restart, ordinary enforcement may be slower or less predictable than a predefined contingency trigger.
   - Practical question a commenter can ask: Why did EPA conclude that general enforcement authorities are sufficient here instead of requiring source-specific contingency steps tied to monitored exceedances or restart events?
