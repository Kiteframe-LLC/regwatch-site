# Public Summary: WHD-2026-0001-0001

_Source file: `summaries/WHD-2026-0001-0001.md`_
_Detected type: `NPRM`_

## What This Document Does
- Proposes to rescind the Wage and Hour Division’s 2024 independent-contractor regulation (29 CFR part 795) and largely restore the 2021 framework.
- Retains a multi-factor “economic reality” test but treats two factors—control and opportunity for profit or loss—as the most probative (“core”) factors.
- Would apply the same part 795 analysis across the FLSA, FMLA, and MSPA by making conforming regulatory revisions.
- Presents a regulatory impact analysis that estimates an undiscounted annual cost savings of $682.7 million and cites WHD enforcement experience with the 2021 approach.

## Why It Matters
- The rule changes how agencies and courts would weigh factors when deciding whether a worker is an employee or an independent contractor—affecting rights, benefits, and liabilities for employers and workers.
- Reweighting factors (making control and profit/loss “core”) could alter classification outcomes in real-world cases and create incentives for businesses to redesign relationships.
- Extending the same framework to FMLA and MSPA could change outcomes in contexts with different statutory goals and enforcement practices.
- The economic and litigation savings claimed by the Department are large but rely on assumptions that are not fully visible in the supplied materials.

## Who Is Affected
- Workers who currently provide services as contractors or employees.
- Employers that engage independent contractors or that rely on classifications in hiring, contracting, and benefits decisions.
- Human resources, payroll, and compliance teams that administer pay and benefits.
- Attorneys, unions, and advocates who litigate or advise on employment status under FLSA, FMLA, and MSPA.
- Agencies and investigators applying WHD enforcement guidance.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Issue: The Department’s proposal to make control and opportunity “core” factors
   - Why it matters: This is the rule’s central interpretive move and changes how multi-factor balancing will be applied in many cases.
   - Question to ask: Please provide the case-selection method, corpus, and coding criteria the Department used to conclude these two factors are generally most probative.

2. Issue: Empirical basis for claimed chilling effect of the 2024 rule
   - Why it matters: The need for rescission is grounded on asserted confusion and a chill on lawful contracting; weak evidence would weaken the justification.
   - Question to ask: What empirical evidence demonstrates that the 2024 rule materially chilled lawful independent contracting or increased misclassification?

3. Issue: Transparency of the economic impact estimates (including the $682.7M annual savings)
   - Why it matters: Large quantified benefits affect the rule’s regulatory-significance analysis and the weight of its policy case.
   - Question to ask: Please disclose the RIA workbook, assumptions table, and sensitivity analysis used to generate the $682.7 million estimate.

4. Issue: Cross-statute harmonization to apply part 795 to FMLA and MSPA
   - Why it matters: Shared statutory language does not ensure identical interpretive or enforcement contexts; a one-size-fits-all approach could overlook statute-specific issues.
   - Question to ask: Provide statute-specific analysis showing why and how the part 795 approach should apply unchanged to FMLA and MSPA contexts.

5. Issue: Failure to analyze narrower alternatives that target clarity without full reversion
   - Why it matters: The agency considered three alternatives but did not evaluate intermediate fixes (e.g., added factor-weighting guidance, rebuttable presumptions, safe-harbors).
   - Question to ask: Explain why the Department rejected narrower options such as clarifying factor application or adding safe-harbor examples rather than reverting to the 2021 framework.

6. Issue: Missing transparent method for converting case law discussion into a weighted rule
   - Why it matters: The shift from doctrinal discussion to a regulatory weighting scheme requires a reproducible interpretive method.
   - Question to ask: Share internal memoranda or legal analyses describing the method used to translate precedent into the proposed “core factor” weighting.

If no of these issues are relevant to your perspective, state any specific factual examples, data, or case outcomes showing how the 2021 and 2024 formulations produce different results.
