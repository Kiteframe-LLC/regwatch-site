# Public Summary: EPA-R09-OAR-2025-0152-0001

_Source file: `summaries/EPA-R09-OAR-2025-0152-0001.md`_
_Detected type: `NPRM`_

## What This Document Does
- EPA proposes to partially approve and partially disapprove Hawaii’s second implementation-period regional haze plan.
- EPA would approve the parts dealing with visibility calculations, progress-to-date analysis, monitoring strategy, and progress-report requirements.
- EPA would disapprove the long-term strategy, the related reasonable progress goals, and certain Federal Land Manager consultation elements.
- The main dispute is over planned shutdowns of power-generation units at Kanoelehua-Hill and Kahului, plus possible shutdowns at Maalaea. EPA says those measures became legally problematic after Hawaiian Electric withdrew consent because of grid-reliability concerns.
- EPA also notes that Hawaii’s 2028 visibility goals are slower than the uniform rate of progress benchmark at both affected Class I areas.

## Why It Matters
- This is a visibility-protection rule, but the practical conflict is between emissions reductions and electric-grid reliability in Hawaii.
- EPA is not saying Hawaii did nothing. It is saying some technical parts of the SIP are acceptable, but the core long-term strategy no longer has enough legal support.
- The proposal matters because it could leave Hawaii without an approved path for some of the most important haze-reduction measures unless the record is strengthened or a replacement strategy is developed.
- The rule also highlights analytical uncertainty: volcanic emissions in Hawaii can overwhelm ordinary point-source emissions, and EPA acknowledges lingering uncertainty in the visibility projections used for the most-impaired days.
- For commenters, the strongest leverage is not broad opposition. It is asking EPA to explain exactly why the legal defect requires such a broad disapproval and what alternatives remain available.

## Who Is Affected
- Hawaiian Electric and facilities tied to the proposed shutdown measures.
- Hawaii regulators responsible for revising and implementing the regional haze SIP.
- Federal Land Managers for Haleakala National Park and Hawaii Volcanoes National Park.
- Communities affected by power reliability, air pollution, and visibility conditions in Hawaii.
- Environmental groups, local residents, and energy stakeholders who may want either stronger haze controls or more explicit reliability safeguards.

## Issues Worth Commenting On (0-6)
1. Issue: Why does the legal defect require such a broad disapproval?
   - Why it matters: EPA clearly objects to the unconsented shutdown measures, but the NPRM is less clear on why that problem necessarily collapses the broader long-term strategy, the reasonable progress goals, and related consultation elements.
   - Practical question to ask: Please explain why EPA could not approve unaffected portions of the long-term strategy or identify a narrower remedy tied only to the consent-dependent shutdown measures.

2. Issue: What is the actual evidence behind the grid-reliability claim?
   - Why it matters: EPA relies heavily on Hawaiian Electric’s withdrawal of consent and its concern about reserve shortfalls, but the reviewed materials do not include the underlying studies or a quantified reliability analysis.
   - Practical question to ask: Please place in the docket the August 29, 2025 utility letter and any supporting reserve-margin, reliability, or replacement-capacity analysis used to support the change in position.

3. Issue: What substitute measures were considered once shutdown consent changed?
   - Why it matters: Without substitute controls, the choice can look artificially binary: either force retirements or accept weaker haze progress.
   - Practical question to ask: What phased retirements, interim controls, or alternative emissions-reduction measures were evaluated for the affected units after the consent issue arose?

4. Issue: How stable are the visibility benchmarks in Hawaii’s volcanic setting?
   - Why it matters: EPA says volcanic emissions can exceed point-source emissions by one or two orders of magnitude and admits remaining uncertainty in the 2028 projections for the most-impaired days.
   - Practical question to ask: Please provide sensitivity analysis showing how volcanic-adjustment choices affect the uniform rate of progress, source screening, and the evaluation of reasonable progress goals.

5. Issue: How reliable was the source-screening process?
   - Why it matters: Hawaii used Q/d screening and then a WRAP weighted-emissions-potential/area-of-influence method to decide which facilities warranted deeper review. If those screens are unstable, important alternatives may have been missed.
   - Practical question to ask: Please disclose the screening thresholds, assumptions, and any sensitivity testing showing whether different meteorology or source-operation assumptions would have changed which facilities were selected.
