## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim: Illinois' source-selection screen captured the right sources

- **Underlying assumption:** A unit-level `Q/d` threshold of four and related source-level screening choices are reasonable proxies for visibility significance.
- **Failure scenario:** Different thresholds, newer operating conditions, or alternative screening metrics would have pulled more units into four-factor review.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can press EPA to show sensitivity testing or threshold-justification rather than only accepting the resulting source list.

### Claim: Existing controls at seven units made fresh four-factor analysis unnecessary

- **Underlying assumption:** Existing MPS, CPS, BART, and permit controls remain sufficiently protective through 2028.
- **Failure scenario:** Existing controls leave feasible headroom for additional reductions or rest on operating assumptions that may not hold in practice.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA may need to explain why "existing effective controls" is an evidence-based determination rather than a shortcut around fuller alternatives analysis.

### Claim: Additional controls for the selected units are not cost-effective

- **Underlying assumption:** Illinois' engineering assumptions, emissions-reduction estimates, useful-life assumptions, and cost inputs are accurate and not overly conservative.
- **Failure scenario:** Different retrofit configurations, dispatch assumptions, or remaining-useful-life estimates materially improve the cost-effectiveness picture.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters have leverage to request the worksheets, benchmark logic, and unit-level comparisons behind the summarized `$ / ton` conclusions.

### Claim: Illinois documented the technical basis adequately

- **Underlying assumption:** Appendix A, appendix B, LADCO materials, and EPA's 2025 TSD are accessible and sufficiently detailed for meaningful public review.
- **Failure scenario:** The public-facing record exposes only high-level summaries, making it difficult for commenters to test source selection, modeling, and cost assumptions during the comment period.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA's documentation finding may be vulnerable to requests for clearer docket organization and more transparent attachment-level support.

### Claim: A fully approved regional haze SIP satisfies Prong 4 here

- **Underlying assumption:** The regional haze analysis and the infrastructure visibility obligation are functionally coextensive for these submissions.
- **Failure scenario:** The regional haze record leaves unresolved visibility concerns or does not fully map onto the infrastructure-SIP requirement for each cited NAAQS.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask EPA to explain the specific analytical bridge instead of relying on a generalized guidance-based statement.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts

- EPA proposes to approve Illinois' June 3, 2024 regional haze SIP revision for the second implementation period.
- Illinois has no in-state mandatory Class I areas, so the proposal addresses Illinois emissions that affect Class I areas in other states.
- Illinois used LADCO's `Q/d` methodology to screen sources and then selected certain units for four-factor analysis while treating seven units as already effectively controlled.
- EPA proposes to find that Illinois satisfied source-selection, consultation, documentation, reasonable-progress-goal, monitoring, and progress-report requirements under `40 CFR 51.308(f)`.

## Step 9 — Failure Modes

- A source-screening threshold may exclude units that are modest individually but material in aggregate.
- A finding that existing controls are already sufficient may become stale if unit operations, dispatch, or fuel use change before 2028.
- Cost-effectiveness conclusions may depend heavily on assumptions about remaining useful life or achievable reduction levels.
- Public commenters may be unable to test EPA's reasoning if the most important support remains buried in appendices or cross-docket materials.
- The Prong 4 visibility approvals may appear underexplained if EPA does not show the concrete bridge from the haze analysis to the infrastructure obligations.

## Step 10 — Regulatory Incentives

- The proposal may incentivize states to rely heavily on threshold screens and "existing effective controls" categorizations to narrow the set of sources receiving full four-factor review.
- It may incentivize regulated sources to emphasize useful-life and marginal-cost arguments to avoid stronger visibility controls.
- It may also incentivize docket design that places core supporting materials in parallel dockets or appendices, which can reduce practical public scrutiny even when materials technically exist.
