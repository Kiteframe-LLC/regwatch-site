## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim

Unconsented source shutdowns make the long-term strategy legally defective.

Underlying assumption
EPA approval of a SIP containing those shutdowns would expose the federal or state program to a Takings Clause problem unless the state supplies stronger assurances.

Failure scenario
If the cited utility letter is more conditional than the preamble suggests, or if state law provides an alternative implementation path short of uncompensated forced closure, the legal premise for full disapproval of the long-term strategy may be weaker than stated.

Procedural implication
Commenters can ask EPA to identify the exact assurance standard it thinks CAA section 110(a)(2)(E)(i) requires here and whether narrower revisions could preserve more of Hawaii’s strategy.

### Claim

The source-selection method (Q/d followed by WEP/AOI) was adequate for screening facilities.

Underlying assumption
Those screening tools capture the facilities most relevant to visibility impairment in Hawaii’s Class I areas.

Failure scenario
Small changes in meteorology, episodic volcanic conditions, or source operation patterns could change which facilities appear most important.

Procedural implication
Commenters can request sensitivity analysis on screen thresholds and whether any plausible facilities were excluded too early.

### Claim

The URP/RPG comparison is meaningful despite volcanic noise.

Underlying assumption
Volcanic adjustment methods leave enough signal to support comparative planning judgments.

Failure scenario
If residual volcanic influence meaningfully distorts the most-impaired-days projections, then both the state’s glidepath and EPA’s criticism of it may be analytically unstable.

Procedural implication
Commenters can ask EPA to clarify how much decision weight it is placing on URP comparisons versus the legal defect in the long-term strategy.

### Claim

Disapproval of the long-term strategy necessarily forces disapproval of RPGs and FLM consultation elements.

Underlying assumption
Those downstream elements cannot be severed or partially approved once the strategy fails.

Failure scenario
Some portions of consultation or goal-setting may still retain value as informational or interim planning material.

Procedural implication
Commenters can ask EPA to explain whether a narrower approval path was considered and rejected.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts

* EPA proposes to approve Hawaii’s second-period regional haze SIP for baseline/current/natural visibility calculations, progress-to-date analysis, monitoring strategy, and progress-report elements.
* EPA proposes to disapprove the long-term strategy, the reasonable progress goals derived from that strategy, and FLM consultation requirements tied to those disapproved parts.
* The dispute centers on planned shutdowns of generating units at Kanoelehua-Hill and Kahului, plus possible shutdowns at Maalaea, after Hawaiian Electric withdrew consent because of grid reliability concerns.
* The authority cited is the Clean Air Act regional haze program and EPA’s Regional Haze Rule, especially 40 CFR 51.308 and CAA section 110(a)(2)(E)(i).

Evidence quote:

> “The EPA is proposing to approve the portions of Hawaii's submission relating to calculations of baseline, current, and natural visibility conditions... The EPA is proposing to disapprove the long-term strategy... Additionally, we are proposing to disapprove the portions of the submission relating to reasonable progress goals and FLM consultation requirements.”

### Inference

The practical issue for commenters is less whether Hawaii attempted a regional haze plan at all and more whether EPA’s legal response to the utility’s withdrawal of consent is overbroad, under-explained, or insufficiently paired with alternative pathways to visibility progress.

## Commenter-Oriented Bottom Line

### Observed Fact

The current record supports EPA’s view that Hawaii’s submitted plan changed materially once the utility withdrew consent to plant shutdowns.

### Inference

The most useful comments will not simply say “approve” or “disapprove.” They should ask EPA to show the exact legal boundary of the defect, disclose the reliability evidence, and explain what substitute measures or partial-approval paths remain available.

### Relevant Early Comment Pattern

The three cached public comments are all generally supportive, with two explicitly emphasizing the need to balance haze reduction against grid reliability. That suggests a practical opening for comments that demand a more concrete reconciliation of environmental progress and energy security rather than abstract support or opposition.
