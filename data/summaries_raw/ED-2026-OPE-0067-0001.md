## Administrative Rulemaking Structural Review

**Docket:** ED-2026-OPE-0067
**Document type:** Proposed interpretive rule
**Title:** *Clarification of the Appropriate Use of Terms “National” and “Regional” by Recognized Accrediting Agencies*  

## Bottom line

This is a **legally framed interpretive rule with a thin empirical record**. Its core theory is that continued use of “regional” is misleading, distorts competition, and may support misrepresentation enforcement. The rule supplies a substantial **statutory interpretation argument**, but its **behavioral, competitive, and reliance analysis is notably underdeveloped**. The highest-leverage comment angles are therefore **procedural adequacy, reliance interests, empirical support for claimed harms, and alternatives short of enforcement-backed interpretation**.

## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim A

**Claim**
Use of “regional” misleads students and the public.

**Underlying assumption**
A substantial share of relevant audiences interpret “regional” as a quality hierarchy or legal status.

**Failure scenario**
Audiences may understand “regional” as a historical shorthand rather than a present claim about superior quality or federal recognition.

**Procedural implication**
Without evidence of actual confusion, the agency’s central harm claim looks asserted rather than demonstrated.

---

### Claim B

**Claim**
The rule will make the accreditation market more competitive.

**Underlying assumption**
Terminology materially affects institutional competition and transfer/choice behavior.

**Failure scenario**
Competitive effects may instead be driven by employer norms, state licensure rules, transfer practices, institutional reputation, and CHEA/ecosystem behavior rather than ED-recognition terminology alone.

**Procedural implication**
A market-effect conclusion without identified evidence is vulnerable as unsupported reasoning.

---

### Claim C

**Claim**
“Regional” is legally improper under the HEA except as historical or grandfathered context.

**Underlying assumption**
The whole-text reading clearly defeats any alternative reading of Section 496(a)(1).

**Failure scenario**
Commenters may argue the statute permits recognition of agencies operating within a region and that the Department is moving from recognition terminology to de facto speech control without enough textual necessity.

**Procedural implication**
This is the agency’s strongest section, but still contestable because it moves from statutory interpretation to downstream descriptive restrictions.

---

### Claim D

**Claim**
Reliance interests are limited because institutions should already have updated after the 2020 final rule.

**Underlying assumption**
Affected parties had fair notice that continued use of “regional” could later be treated as misleading for enforcement purposes.

**Failure scenario**
The record itself acknowledges a proposed-rule statement that agencies “would not be prohibited from identifying themselves as they deem appropriate,” and also acknowledges continuing use in state laws, transfer policies, standards, and marketing. 

**Procedural implication**
Reliance looks real, foreseeable, and underanalyzed.

---

### Claim E

**Claim**
The rule is nonbinding, but may be used in enforcement.

**Underlying assumption**
That posture is procedurally sufficient because the rule only interprets existing law.

**Failure scenario**
If the interpretation becomes a practical enforcement benchmark, commenters may argue it functions like a substantive policy shift without adequate support or transition analysis.

**Procedural implication**
Potential APA vulnerability around practical effect and adequacy of explanation.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts

* The Department proposes an **interpretive rule** concerning use of “regional” and “national” by Department-recognized accrediting agencies. 
* It ties the interpretation to **HEA Section 496** and to anti-misrepresentation duties. 
* It states the Department ended recognition of accrediting agencies as “regional” effective **July 1, 2020**. 
* It says institutions and agencies still use “regional” in standards, marketing materials, and other texts. 
* It states the interpretation may be referenced in reviews and enforcement-related contexts. 

### Inference

* The practical target is not just agency nomenclature, but also **institutional marketing, transfer-credit policies, and state licensure references** that still rely on “regional.”
* The key regulatory change is not a new codified prohibition in the uploaded text, but a **formalized interpretation that could support future compliance and enforcement positions**.

## Step 3 — Agency Argument Tree

**Regulatory objective**
Reduce misleading terminology and align accreditation descriptions with the Department’s current recognition framework.

**Problem definition**
Use of “regional” allegedly misleads students, distorts quality perceptions, and perpetuates obsolete distinctions.

**Data used**
Mainly historical rulemaking discussion, statutory text, and a few anecdotal references such as transfer-credit policy examples. 

**Analytical method**
Whole-text statutory interpretation plus policy reasoning about confusion and competition.

**Alternatives considered**
Some alternative descriptive formulations are mentioned for agencies describing operating geography. 

**Agency conclusion**
Only “national,” “institutional,” or “programmatic” are proper descriptors for recognized scope, and “regional” should not be used as a recognition descriptor. 

### Missing links

* No identified evidence measuring **student confusion**.
* No identified evidence measuring **market competition effects**.
* No serious analysis of **reliance burdens** on institutions, agencies, and states.
* No meaningful exploration of **graduated alternatives** short of enforcement-backed interpretation.

## Step 5 — Missing Alternatives Detector

**Plausible alternatives not analyzed**

* Safe-harbor language, e.g. “historically regional, currently nationally recognized.”

## Step 7 — Legal Grounding

**Observed statutory grounding**

* HEA Sections 101, 102, and 496 are the main statutory anchors discussed in the preamble. 
* The Department’s reading is that “nationally recognized accrediting agency” is the operative status for Title IV purposes, and Section 496(a)(1) addresses permissible scope of agency operation, not creation of a separate recognition category. 

**Assessment**

* **Legal grounding appears substantial but contestable.**
* The strongest legal vulnerability is not the existence of a statutory argument, but the **jump from recognition terminology to broader claims of misleadingness and enforcement significance**.

**Uncertain**

* Whether the agency has adequately explained why this interpretation should extend from agency recognition terminology to all downstream institutional descriptive usage without more nuanced treatment.

## Step 18 — Decision Brief

### Leverage point 1

**Key NPRM sentence**
“Although this proposed interpretative rule is nonbinding on the Department and the public, the Department may refer to this interpretive rule when taking enforcement action.” 

**Routine description**
In administrative law, interpretive rules may clarify existing obligations, but when an agency signals enforcement use, commenters can reasonably ask how the interpretation will operate in practice and whether affected parties had sufficient notice.

**Regulatory effectiveness / statutory compliance / procedural adequacy**
This is the sharpest procedural seam in the rule. The Department needs to explain the practical boundary between interpretation and enforcement consequence.

**Real-world consequence of invoking it**
Could force the agency either to narrow the statement, add safe harbors, or explain more clearly what conduct actually triggers misrepresentation risk.

**Worth a comment?** **High**

---

### Leverage point 2

**Key NPRM sentence**
“The Department believes that this proposed interpretive rule will make the higher education market more competitive...” 

**Routine description**
A market-competition claim is an effects claim. Even in an interpretive rule, that sort of conclusion is stronger when tied to identifiable evidence rather than policy intuition.

**Regulatory effectiveness / statutory compliance / procedural adequacy**
This is a classic “show your work” point. The record, as reviewed, does not identify the underlying support.

**Real-world consequence of invoking it**
May force the agency to disclose evidence, soften the claim, or clarify that the point is policy judgment rather than demonstrated market analysis.

**Worth a comment?** **High**

---

### Leverage point 3

**Key NPRM sentence**
“The Department acknowledges that this interpretation may cause some institutions, programs, and accrediting agencies to change the way they refer to accreditation, and that such change may take time.” 

**Routine description**
The rule itself admits transition burdens and invites comment on reliance interests. That opens the door to targeted, constructive requests for phased implementation or safe-harbor language.

**Regulatory effectiveness / statutory compliance / procedural adequacy**
Strong procedural leverage because the Department expressly raised the issue.

**Real-world consequence of invoking it**
Could improve the final rule’s administrability and reduce unfair surprise.

**Worth a comment?** **High**

---

## Final assessment

This NPRM is **not analytically empty**, but it is **imbalanced**: the statutory interpretation is developed; the empirical and implementation analysis is not. The best constructive comments would not attack the Department for clarifying nomenclature in the abstract. They would ask the Department to **prove the real-world harms it asserts, define the enforcement boundary, and account more concretely for reliance and narrower alternatives**.
