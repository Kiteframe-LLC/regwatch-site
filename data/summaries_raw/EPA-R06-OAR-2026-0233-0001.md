## TL;DR

This looks like a **routine, thin-record NPRM** for a **state negative declaration**, not a substantive control-technology rule. EPA’s reasoning is basically: the 2025 OSWI emission guidelines changed applicability, Louisiana rechecked its sources, Louisiana certified that no covered existing OSWI units exist in the relevant jurisdiction, and EPA therefore proposes to amend Part 62 to reflect that negative declaration. The main procedural vulnerability is not missing cost analysis or alternatives analysis; it is the **thin evidentiary basis for the no-sources premise**. With the letter now attached, that premise is at least documented, but the record still does not explain **how** Louisiana reviewed its sources or what screening method it used. Under your rubric, that makes this a **moderate-value evidence-request comment opportunity**, not a high-value attack brief.

## Step 1 — Claim stress test

### Claim

Louisiana reviewed its sources and found no covered OSWI units.  

**Underlying assumption**
Louisiana used a review method capable of identifying all facilities newly covered after the 2025 OSWI revisions.

**Failure scenario**
A facility exists that is misclassified, newly covered under revised applicability, temporarily idle, municipally affiliated, or otherwise easy to miss in a cursory inventory review.

**Procedural implication**
EPA could accept a negative declaration where a state plan or federal plan is actually required.

### Claim

EPA must approve a compliant submission and this action merely accepts a state negative declaration. 

**Underlying assumption**
EPA has enough basis to determine the submission is compliant, not merely facially complete.

**Failure scenario**
EPA treats state certification as self-proving without enough explanation to show why the no-sources conclusion is reliable.

**Procedural implication**
The record becomes vulnerable as inadequately explained, even if the ultimate conclusion is true.

### Adversarial interpretation

A regulated entity could benefit from ambiguity about whether a marginal unit qualifies as an OSWI unit after the 2025 revisions. The thinner the public explanation of the state’s screening method, the easier it is for borderline cases to stay invisible.

## Step 2 — Essence of the rule

### Observed Facts

EPA says sections 111(d) and 129 require states to submit plans for existing covered solid waste incineration sources, and if no such sources exist, a state may instead submit a negative declaration. EPA says Louisiana submitted such a declaration, and EPA proposes to amend 40 CFR part 62, subpart T, to reflect receipt of that declaration.  

The state’s attached letter says LDEQ “recently reviewed its sources” and “determined that no sources within Louisiana are subject to the OSWI Emission Guidelines.”  

### Inference

The rule’s real function is administrative housekeeping for federal enforceability architecture: EPA is updating the SIP/FIP framework to reflect that, at least on the present record, Louisiana has no existing covered OSWI units requiring a section 111(d)/129 plan.

### Step 16 — Evidence requests

* clarification whether the review specifically accounted for the 2025 revised OSWI applicability universe
* confirmation whether any potentially covered inactive, seasonal, municipal, or institutional incineration units were screened out and why

### Step 17 — High-leverage comment angles

1. Ask EPA to summarize the factual basis for concluding that no Louisiana units are subject to the revised 2025 OSWI EG.
2. Ask whether LDEQ’s review included a search of permit inventories and any source categories newly implicated by the 2025 revisions.
3. Ask EPA to clarify what “specified jurisdictions in Louisiana” excludes, so the public understands the geographic and regulatory scope of the negative declaration.
4. Ask EPA to add a short record memorandum or supplement explaining the screening method, even if no further substantive analysis is needed.

### Step 18 — Decision brief

**Strongest leverage point:** the record states the conclusion but not the method.

**Routine description:** In a negative-declaration rulemaking, the public is not really evaluating control costs or emissions modeling. The key question is whether the state’s no-sources determination is transparent enough to support EPA’s acceptance of it.

**Regulatory effectiveness / statutory compliance / procedural adequacy:**
The action may well be legally proper if no covered units exist. But procedural adequacy improves if EPA or LDEQ briefly explains how that determination was made.

**Real-world consequence:**
If the screening was too cursory, a covered facility could remain outside the required 111(d)/129 planning framework.

**Worth a comment?** Moderate value.

**Comment posture:** evidence request

## Bottom line

This is not the sort of NPRM where the big move is “EPA failed to do a TSD.” The constructive opening is narrower: **ask EPA to make the no-sources determination more legible.** The rule’s logic is straightforward, but the factual bridge from “we reviewed our sources” to “there are no covered units” is still mostly hidden in plain sight.
