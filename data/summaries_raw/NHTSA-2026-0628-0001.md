## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim: “No safety disbenefits”

* **Underlying assumption**

  * ADS eliminates all risks tied to gear-state awareness

* **Failure scenario**

  * Occupants, responders, or bystanders lack visibility into vehicle state
  * Failure modes: stuck-in-drive, rollback, maintenance states

* **Adversarial interpretation**

  * Manufacturers omit all gear/state signaling—even when useful

* **Procedural implication**

  * Agency may have **failed to consider foreseeable safety use cases**

---

### Claim: “Display provides no safety benefit”

* **Underlying assumption**

  * Only *drivers* need gear-state awareness

* **Counterfactual**

  * Non-driver stakeholders:

    * passengers
    * emergency responders
    * service personnel

* **Failure scenario**

  * Lack of situational awareness in edge conditions (crash, tow, malfunction)

* **Implication**

  * **Scope definition may be too narrow (driver-centric)**

---

### Claim: “No costs / deregulatory”

* **Underlying assumption**

  * Removing requirement has zero offset costs

* **Failure scenario**

  * New UI/UX signaling required (even if optional)
  * Liability-driven reintroduction of displays

* **Implication**

  * **Cost analysis incomplete**

---

### Claim: “Exemptions would otherwise be used”

* **Underlying assumption**

  * Firms would uniformly pursue exemptions

* **Failure scenario**

  * Some firms comply instead
  * Exemption process friction varies

* **Implication**

  * **Behavioral prediction unsupported**

# Bottom Line

This is a **clean but analytically thin deregulatory rule**.

The core vulnerability is not the policy direction—it is the **absence of demonstrated reasoning depth**, especially around:

* safety in non-driver contexts
* failure modes
* alternatives

The strongest comments will not oppose the rule—they will **force the agency to define what “no safety impact” actually means in a driverless system.**
