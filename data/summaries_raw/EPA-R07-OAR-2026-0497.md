## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Major Claim A: Missouri's substitutions are no less stringent
- Underlying assumption: the narrowed state term and the ANSI/CGA substitution cover the same practical facility universe and safety performance as the federal RMP baseline.
- Failure scenario: mixed-use sites, non-fertilizer ammonia uses, or equivalency approvals create edge cases where a facility or safety measure receives lighter scrutiny than under the federal wording.
- Procedural implication: commenters can ask EPA to explain boundary cases and equivalency criteria instead of relying on broad assurance language.

### Major Claim B: Three staff and a five-year cycle are adequate
- Underlying assumption: the facility universe is stable, inspections are well targeted, and review demands outside onsite inspections are modest.
- Failure scenario: incident response, plan review, technical assistance, and enforcement consume more staff time than the preamble suggests, weakening oversight cadence.
- Procedural implication: commenters can request workload, prioritization, and backlog details.

### Major Claim C: Split federal/state authority will work cleanly
- Underlying assumption: regulated parties and inspectors can reliably distinguish delegated agricultural ammonia obligations from retained federal authority over other chemicals and nonqualifying ammonia uses.
- Failure scenario: facilities with multiple regulated chemicals face confusion over who enforces what, especially during inspections or incidents.
- Procedural implication: commenters can ask for a coordination protocol or examples of how overlapping jurisdiction will operate.

### Major Claim D: Equivalent-or-safer alternatives can be approved safely
- Underlying assumption: Missouri has a transparent, technically rigorous process for deciding whether alternatives to ANSI/CGA G-2.1-2023 are truly equivalent or safer.
- Failure scenario: the approval process becomes discretionary and opaque, producing inconsistent or weak substitutions.
- Procedural implication: commenters can ask EPA to specify review criteria, documentation expectations, and public availability of approvals.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts
- EPA proposes to approve Missouri's request for delegation of a partial Risk Management Program for agricultural anhydrous ammonia facilities.
- Missouri adopted 40 CFR part 68 by reference with two changes: a narrower facility term and replacement of certain "recognized and generally accepted good engineering practices" references with ANSI/CGA G-2.1-2023, while allowing equivalent-or-safer alternatives approved by the MoDNR director.
- EPA would retain concurrent authority over the delegated slice and sole authority over other regulated chemicals and nonqualifying ammonia uses.
- The NPRM says Missouri has about 200 covered facilities and plans to inspect about 40 each year.

### Inference
This is not a classic deregulatory repeal, but it is not purely clerical either. It reallocates day-to-day implementation authority and approves state-specific wording choices that could matter in edge cases, so the best comments are targeted clarification and implementation-governance comments.

### Evidence Quotes
- "The EPA is proposing to approve the request submitted by MoDNR... to delegate a partial RMP for agricultural anhydrous ammonia facilities."
- "The EPA would retain authority for the RMP for all other regulated chemicals..."
- "We have reviewed the limited changes Missouri made to its regulation and have determined that the applicability criteria and program requirements for each affected source are no less stringent..."
