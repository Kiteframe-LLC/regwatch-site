## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim: Wisconsin's package achieves at least 15% RFP

- **Underlying assumption:** The emissions reductions credited between 2017 and 2023 are permanent, enforceable, and properly attributed to the nonattainment areas.
- **Failure scenario:** Area-specific reductions, especially for Milwaukee and Sheboygan, may rely on modeling assumptions or control-credit logic that commenters cannot fully inspect from the supplied record.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA may need to expose more of the area-by-area support tables and control-credit basis so commenters can verify that the 15% finding is not over-compressed.

### Claim: The 2023 budgets are suitable for transportation conformity

- **Underlying assumption:** The budgets are both numerically sound and appropriately tied to the approved RFP showing.
- **Failure scenario:** If the RFP logic is incomplete, or if area-specific travel assumptions are underexplained, the budgets may look precisely quantified without being fully transparent.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask EPA to provide clearer area-specific budget derivations, especially for Milwaukee and Sheboygan, before the adequacy finding hardens into conformity constraints.

### Claim: Existing approved I/M and NNSR structures satisfy Moderate-area requirements

- **Underlying assumption:** Wisconsin's previously approved programs still align cleanly with the 2015 ozone Moderate requirements for each affected area.
- **Failure scenario:** The programs may be legally sufficient in general while still leaving underexplained questions about geographic coverage, timing, or whether the older approvals map cleanly onto the revised 2021 nonattainment boundaries.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA should explain the crosswalk between older approvals and the newly reclassified areas more explicitly.

### Claim: It is acceptable to approve inventories, RFP, budgets, I/M, and NNSR now while deferring attainment, RACM, and contingency measures

- **Underlying assumption:** The segmented approval does not obscure how the omitted elements could affect the practical soundness of the current approvals.
- **Failure scenario:** Commenters may struggle to evaluate whether the approved pieces are robust without seeing how EPA intends to treat RACM, contingency measures, and the attainment showing in the separate action.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA may need to explain why the record is ripe for partial approval now and how the remaining action could bear on the same underlying emissions and transport issues.

### Claim: The base-year inventory satisfies both Marginal and Moderate requirements

- **Underlying assumption:** The 2017 inventory methods, partial-county allocation methods, and ozone-season-day assumptions are adequate for all three areas.
- **Failure scenario:** Population-based allocations or source-specific assumptions could materially affect one or more areas, especially where lake-transport dynamics complicate local-source attribution.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask EPA to summarize any material uncertainty around partial-county allocation and transport-sensitive source categories.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

- EPA proposes to approve portions of Wisconsin's April 2, 2025 SIP submission for the Kenosha, Milwaukee, and Sheboygan 2015 ozone Moderate nonattainment areas.

## Step 6 — Technical Coherence

The rule is technically coherent at a high level. EPA's structure is recognizable for ozone SIP approvals: establish the nonattainment history, review the base-year inventory, test the RFP demonstration, derive conformity budgets, and confirm that previously approved I/M and NNSR programs still satisfy applicable requirements.

The main coherence issue is compression rather than contradiction. The Kenosha supporting text underscores a technically complicated transport setting in which local emissions controls coexist with persistent interstate and lake-breeze-driven ozone formation. The NPRM still reaches straightforward approval conclusions for the inventories, RFP showing, and budgets. That can be reasonable, but the visible record would be stronger if EPA more plainly connected the transport-sensitive problem definition to the local planning tools it is approving.

### Observed Fact

### Inference
