## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim

Current definition creates ambiguity about whether cephalopods qualify as shellfish.

**Underlying assumption**
The phrase “having a shell” is materially driving inconsistent regulatory treatment.

**Failure scenario**
If field practice already treats cephalopods consistently under existing authorities, the problem may be overstated or merely hypothetical.

**Procedural implication**
The rule would still be clarifying, but the administrative record would be thin on actual need.

---

### Claim

Ambiguity has caused inconsistent port treatment and unnecessary burden.

**Underlying assumption**
There are identifiable examples, enforcement records, or compliance incidents showing this.

**Failure scenario**
No documented examples exist, or the issue is infrequent and localized.

**Procedural implication**
A commenter could reasonably request incident examples, internal guidance, or data supporting the claimed burden.

---

### Claim

No conforming amendments are necessary to parts 14 and 17.

**Underlying assumption**
All downstream provisions using or depending on “shellfish” remain coherent after the redefinition.

**Failure scenario**
Other provisions, forms, instructions, or enforcement manuals still assume shell-bearing organisms or contain examples inconsistent with the new text.

**Procedural implication**
A missing crosswalk or legal mapping could weaken the “no conforming amendments” conclusion.

---

### Claim

The change is deregulatory and imposes no new obligations.

**Underlying assumption**
Expanded definitional clarity will only reduce burden, not alter jurisdictional reach in any consequential way.

**Failure scenario**
Including all Mollusca plus Decapoda, plus “any part, product, egg or offspring thereof,” could broaden the set of items clearly covered and thus sharpen enforcement authority in ways some entities experience as added burden.

**Procedural implication**
The agency should explain why clarifying coverage is not, in at least some applications, an expansion of enforceable scope.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts

* The rule would revise 50 CFR 10.12 so that “Shellfish means an aquatic invertebrate animal of the following taxa: (1) Mollusca ... and (2) Order Decapoda ... and (3) Any part, product, egg or offspring thereof...” 
* The stated purpose is to remove the phrase “having a shell” and clarify inclusion of cephalopods. 
* The affected regulatory area is wildlife import/export, declaration, inspection, and enforcement under FWS jurisdiction. 
* Authority cited includes the Lacey Act and multiple wildlife statutes. 

### Inference

This is presented as a definitional clarification, but it also functions as a scope-specification rule for regulated wildlife products at the border.

## Step 6 — Technical Coherence

**Assessment:** Mixed

The biological premise that cephalopods may lack external shells is coherent. But the final text raises drafting and coherence questions:

* “Mollusca” is broader than cephalopods and may include organisms not discussed in the preamble.
* The phrase “including but not limited to” plus broad taxa plus broad derivative-products language creates an expansive structure not fully justified by the narrower stated problem.
* The proposed text appears to contain drafting errors such as “an oysters” and “a lobsters,” suggesting limited editorial hardening in the current draft.
