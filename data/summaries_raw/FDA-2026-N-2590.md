## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim: Sufficient information exists to move from PMA to class II
- **Underlying assumption:** The cited PMA history, advisory-panel deliberations, and postmarket data are collectively enough to replace PMA review with special controls plus 510(k).
- **Failure scenario:** The public record does not clearly show how those sources map onto each chosen special control, especially for high-risk subgroups and indeterminate results.
- **Procedural implication:** FDA should better connect each evidence source to each control so commenters can test whether the downgrade is fully justified.

### Claim: Low MDR and recall counts show no significant postmarket safety signal
- **Underlying assumption:** The number and severity of reported events are low enough, and representative enough, to support reduced premarket scrutiny.
- **Failure scenario:** Underreporting, small market size, or aggregation across product codes could make low counts less informative than FDA suggests.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask for denominators, product-specific context, and subgroup-specific event interpretation.

### Claim: Proposed special controls are enough to mitigate identified risks
- **Underlying assumption:** Labeling plus design-verification, analytical studies, and multisite clinical studies will reliably capture the main failure modes.
- **Failure scenario:** Manufacturers satisfy the listed study categories without generating robust evidence for immunosuppressed patients, BCG-vaccinated patients, or patients with nontuberculous mycobacterial infections.
- **Procedural implication:** FDA may need clearer minimum subgroup expectations or clearer discussion of how such evidence will be judged in future 510(k)s.

### Claim: Class II should still require 510(k), not exemption
- **Underlying assumption:** General controls and special controls alone are adequate, but FDA review of each marketing submission remains necessary.
- **Failure scenario:** FDA has not fully explained why 510(k) review is needed while also treating the risk profile as manageable without PMA, leaving uncertainty about the precise boundary between ordinary and higher-risk changes.
- **Procedural implication:** A more explicit explanation would strengthen the record and clarify compliance expectations for future sponsors.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

**Observed Facts**
- FDA proposes to create a new classification regulation, 21 CFR 866.3371, for "Qualitative Mycobacterium tuberculosis cell-mediated immune response assay."
- The proposal would move Mycobacterium tuberculosis cell-mediated immunity tests and immune response enzyme-linked immunospot tests from class III (PMA) to class II (special controls).
- FDA proposes to keep these assays subject to premarket notification rather than exempt them from 510(k).
- The proposed special controls require detailed labeling, critical-reagent characterization, lot-release criteria, risk analysis, analytical studies, and multisite clinical studies with specified patient cohorts.
- The proposal relies on FD&C Act sections 513(f)(3), 513(a)(1)(B), 520(h)(4), and related device-classification authorities.

**Evidence quotes**
- "FDA is proposing to reclassify the qualitative TB immune response assays ... from class III to class II, subject to premarket notification (510(k)) requirements."
- "FDA does not believe that the general controls applicable to the devices are sufficient..."
- "If this proposed order is finalized, persons who intend to market this type of device will have to submit to FDA a premarket notification..."

**Inference**
- The practical policy move is not simple deregulation. FDA is replacing PMA review with a more standardized class II pathway while attempting to preserve rigorous validation expectations through special controls.

## Step 6 — Technical Coherence

**Observed Fact**
- The rule is technically coherent in one important respect: FDA identifies concrete failure modes and then pairs them with corresponding categories of labeling, analytical studies, and clinical validation.
- The proposed regulation requires documentation of the computational path from raw data to the reported result, critical-reagent characterization, lot-release criteria, stability studies, analytical studies, and a multisite clinical study with defined cohorts.

**Inference**

## Step 9 — Failure Modes

- Future 510(k) submissions could technically include high-risk cohorts while still providing thin evidence on those subgroups.
- Labeling may describe limitations clearly, but real-world users may still overread negative results in immunosuppressed patients or other hard-to-test populations.
- Sponsors may rely heavily on comparator-method framing without enough transparency about discordant or indeterminate results.
- The 30-day effective date for a final order may be workable, but the record does not explain whether any transition frictions could affect sponsor planning or market continuity.

## Step 10 — Regulatory Incentives

- The proposal creates a strong incentive for manufacturers to pursue the lower-burden 510(k) pathway instead of PMA.
- It may incentivize sponsors to optimize study design around the minimum cohorts explicitly named in the special controls rather than broader evidence generation.
- It may also incentivize FDA and sponsors to rely on labeling limitations to manage some risk differences that might otherwise be addressed through more explicit performance standards or postmarket follow-up.

## Step 11 — Evidentiary Strength

- **Regulatory effectiveness, statutory compliance, procedural adequacy:** The pathway appears statutorily available and procedurally ordinary, but the record would be stronger if FDA more clearly connected each evidence source to each selected special control.
- **Real-world consequence of invoking the leverage:** A clearer crosswalk would help commenters identify whether future 510(k) entrants might slip through with weaker evidence than FDA intends.
- **Worth a comment?** High

### Leverage Point 2: Protection for high-risk subpopulations
- **Key sentence from the NPRM:** "Enrolled subjects must include individuals who are immunosuppressed, individuals who have received the bacille Calmette-Guerin vaccine, or individuals with nontuberculous mycobacterial infections, as applicable."
- **Routine description situated in context:** FDA is correctly identifying the populations where these assays can be most difficult to evaluate, but the proposal does not specify how much evidence in those groups will count as adequate.
- **Regulatory effectiveness, statutory compliance, procedural adequacy:** The proposed control is directionally strong, yet it leaves procedural ambiguity about minimum subgroup evidence expectations.
- **Real-world consequence of invoking the leverage:** Clearer subgroup expectations could reduce the risk that future devices are cleared on thin evidence for the very populations most vulnerable to false reassurance or confusing results.
- **Worth a comment?** High

### Leverage Point 3: Meaning of "low" postmarket safety signals
- **Key sentence from the NPRM:** "These facts, coupled with the low number of MDRs that could have caused patient harm, indicate a lack of significant postmarket safety signals for this device class."
- **Routine description situated in context:** FDA often considers postmarket evidence when reclassifying devices, but the interpretive force of event counts depends on usage volume, reporting patterns, and product-specific context.
- **Regulatory effectiveness, statutory compliance, procedural adequacy:** The agency's conclusion may be reasonable, but the explanation would be more durable if FDA disclosed more context for why these numbers support a downgrade.
- **Real-world consequence of invoking the leverage:** Better context would let commenters test whether apparent safety is genuine or partly an artifact of reporting limitations.
- **Worth a comment?** Moderate to high
