## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim: Existing PPG controls remain the most effective feasible option

- **Underlying assumption:** EPA and Ohio have fully evaluated whether tighter capture, building segmentation, or more targeted lab controls could improve over the current building-wide RTO approach.
- **Failure scenario:** The complex lab layout does not actually preclude selective higher-capture controls, or the no-cost-effectiveness estimate decision masked potentially reasonable incremental improvements.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask EPA to show why no greater reductions are achievable before simply carrying forward an older source-specific RACT regime.

### Claim: Owens Corning's broader asphalt-fume control would be economically infeasible

- **Underlying assumption:** The plant-layout assumptions, number of required RTOs, and cost-per-ton analysis are accurate and representative.
- **Failure scenario:** Fewer than four RTOs, staged capture, or narrower line-specific retrofits could materially change the cost-effectiveness range.
- **Procedural implication:** The agency may have rejected potentially meaningful alternatives without showing the design basis or sensitivity analysis.

### Claim: Akron's mixer covers plus a facility-wide limit satisfy RACT

- **Underlying assumption:** The claimed `over 99%` control from covers is real under portable, frequently moved, custom-batch mixing operations.
- **Failure scenario:** Real-world openings, maintenance lapses, and mixer movement reduce effective control below the narrative claim, while the facility-wide cap masks unit-level underperformance.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can press for verification, inspection frequency, and evidence showing the cover-based control rate under actual use.

### Claim: Carmeuse's `4.6 lb/ton` limit is a defensible RACT threshold

- **Underlying assumption:** Five stack tests plus a `20%` contingency factor reasonably capture variable kiln conditions.
- **Failure scenario:** The selected tests were not representative, or a highest-test-plus-contingency method simply memorializes existing performance rather than probing whether lower feasible limits exist.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA may need to explain why this method reflects RACT, not just current operations.

### Claim: Ross's post-combustion controls are not economically reasonable

- **Underlying assumption:** The CBI cost calculations are robust, reproducible, and not materially dependent on hidden assumptions.
- **Failure scenario:** The public record lacks enough detail to test cost components, retrofit assumptions, or whether emissions reductions were understated.
- **Procedural implication:** Reliance on undisclosed cost support weakens the public's ability to comment meaningfully on the RACT conclusion.

### Claim: Formica's rule substitution causes no backsliding

- **Underlying assumption:** The conversion between `0.9 lb VOC/gal` and `0.08 lb VOC/lb` is genuinely equivalent across relevant coating formulations.
- **Failure scenario:** The equivalence depends heavily on coating composition, and the record does not expose enough representative formulations or operational cases.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA's noninterference conclusion may be underexplained for a revision that removes a source-specific SIP rule.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts

- EPA proposes to approve Ohio source-specific RACT decisions for Cleveland-area major VOC and NOx sources under the 2015 ozone standard.
- EPA also proposes Cincinnati-area SIP-strengthening measures for GE Aerospace Evendale and Tyson Foods, even though Cincinnati is in a maintenance area and not subject to mandatory RACT.
- The facilities and measures vary substantially: continuing older RTO-based controls at PPG, throughput and equipment-specific controls at Owens Corning, a facility-wide `99.9 tpy` VOC cap at Akron Paint and Varnish, existing low-NOx burner limits at U.S. Steel and Charter Steel, a new `4.6 lb/ton` NOx limit at Carmeuse, a `105 lb/hr` rolling NOx limit plus reevaluation trigger at Ross, and a Formica shift from a source-specific rule to a presumptive CTG-based rule.
- EPA repeatedly states that Ohio's underlying RACT studies are available in the docket, while the provided review package does not include them.
- EPA cites Clean Air Act sections `182(b)(2)`, `182(f)`, and `110(l)` and the SIP approval framework under `42 U.S.C. 7410(k)`.

### Inference

- This is a technically dense EPA SIP approval that functions as a bundle of facility-by-facility judgments about what counts as "reasonably available" control.
- The biggest review question is not whether EPA has legal authority to approve state choices; it is whether EPA has exposed enough engineering and cost support in the public-facing record to justify each source-specific conclusion.

### Evidence Quotes

- "The EPA is proposing to approve source-specific State Implementation Plan (SIP) revisions submitted by Ohio."
- "Ohio adopted OAC rules 3745-21-11 and 3745-110-03(J), which require major VOC and NO X sources that are not covered by a CTG to submit detailed source-specific RACT studies analyzing the technological and economic feasibility of each available control measure."
- "The EPA concurs with Ohio's RACT determination."
- "The EPA has determined that this SIP revision will not interfere with attainment or maintenance of the NAAQS in accordance with section 110(l) of the CAA."

## Step 9 — Failure Modes

- EPA may finalize multiple source-specific RACT approvals without giving commenters enough public detail to test engineering and cost assumptions.
- A court or commenter could view some facility analyses as ratifying existing controls rather than rigorously identifying the lowest feasible emission limit.
- Formica's rule substitution could be criticized if EPA does not show the metric conversion and representative-formulation comparison more transparently.
- Ross's use of CBI-supported cost analysis could leave the final rule vulnerable to claims that meaningful public comment was impaired.
- Facility-wide or permit-based approaches may prove hard to evaluate in practice if the final record does not clearly identify monitoring, recordkeeping, and verification provisions.

## Step 10 — Regulatory Incentives

- Ohio and EPA have an incentive to preserve workable, existing plant configurations where retrofits are costly or complex.
- Facilities have an incentive to present current controls as already near-RACT and alternatives as operationally disruptive.
- EPA has an incentive to rely on state permit terms and source studies rather than recreate the engineering record in the preamble.
- Commenters therefore have the most leverage when they ask for the hidden assumptions, representative test data, and clearer public explanation rather than merely objecting in the abstract.
