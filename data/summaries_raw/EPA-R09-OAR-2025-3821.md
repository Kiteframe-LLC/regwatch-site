## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Major Claim A: The revised Arizona plan now cures the earlier approval defects
- Underlying assumption: the revised Rule B1302 and Appendix 14 now contain numeric emissions limits, monitoring, and enforceable requirements that align with the modeled fugitive and stack emissions.
- Failure scenario: the revised state rule still leaves operational ambiguity about fugitive emissions or does not map cleanly onto the emissions profile used in the attainment modeling.
- Procedural implication: commenters should ask EPA to identify the specific rule text and monitoring provisions that fix each defect found in 2020.

### Major Claim B: Longer-term average limits are comparably stringent to the modeled 1-hour CEV
- Underlying assumption: the selected adjustment factors reflect real smelter variability and still protect against hourly exceedances in meteorologically adverse conditions.
- Failure scenario: historical variability used to derive the adjustment factors does not represent restart conditions, changed feed mix, or upset operations.
- Procedural implication: commenters should request the historical dataset, exclusion criteria, and sensitivity analysis behind the 30-day and 24-hour adjustment factors.

### Major Claim C: The modeling analysis demonstrates attainment by January 31, 2027
- Underlying assumption: the selected meteorological years, background concentrations, receptor grid, and emissions assumptions are representative enough to support a forward-looking attainment finding.
- Failure scenario: background concentrations or source-operation assumptions understate worst-case conditions, especially for a complex smelter with fugitive and process variability.
- Procedural implication: commenters should ask whether EPA tested alternate background assumptions, alternate meteorological years, and higher-emission restart scenarios.

### Major Claim D: Enforcement authorities can serve as adequate contingency measures
- Underlying assumption: a robust enforcement program can identify violations quickly enough and drive corrective action without a separately specified contingency emission reduction measure.
- Failure scenario: the area experiences renewed violations after restart, but enforcement timelines are slower or less predictable than a predefined contingency trigger.
- Procedural implication: commenters should press EPA on why general enforcement authorities are sufficient here instead of supplemental automatic contingency steps tied to monitored exceedances.

### Major Claim E: Approval should terminate sanctions and the FIP obligation
- Underlying assumption: the plan is complete enough that no residual deficiency remains material under section 110(k).
- Failure scenario: EPA approves while key technical details remain underexplained in the public record, creating renewed vulnerability later.
- Procedural implication: commenters should request a clean issue-by-issue closure table linking each old deficiency to the new supporting record.

- Enforcement-only contingency weakness: if violations recur, ordinary enforcement tools may act more slowly than a predefined contingency mechanism.
- Anti-backsliding documentation gap: if the record does not clearly map old obligations to new ones, approval of rule sunsets could become vulnerable.
