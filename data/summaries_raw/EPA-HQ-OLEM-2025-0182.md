# Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

## Claim

All appropriate response actions have been completed.

### Underlying assumption

Remediation objectives were correctly defined and achieved.

### Failure scenario

Contamination remains but:

* monitoring thresholds were mis-specified
* plume migration underestimated
* new exposure pathways appear

### Procedural implication

Deletion could be challenged as **premature if remediation evidence is incomplete**.

---

## Claim

Deletion complies with 40 CFR 300.425(e).

### Underlying assumption

Deletion criteria satisfied:

* cleanup complete
* remedy protective
* state concurrence obtained

### Failure scenario

Incomplete remediation or unresolved institutional controls.

### Procedural implication

Administrative record must show compliance with **each criterion in the regulation**.

---

## Claim

Deletion does not preclude future action.

### Underlying assumption

Remaining risks are low but not impossible.

### Adversarial interpretation

Deletion might be used politically to claim **site cleanup success even if monitoring continues**.

> “All appropriate response actions… have been completed.” 

### Context

Under CERCLA, NPL deletion requires EPA to demonstrate:

* cleanup complete
* remedy protective
* no further response needed.

### Procedural adequacy question

The NPRM does not summarize the remediation evidence.

### Real-world consequence

If the record lacks clear remediation verification, deletion could:

* reduce monitoring
* obscure remaining contamination risk

### Worth a comment?

**Low to Moderate**

Only worth commenting if:

* the FCOR shows weak remediation evidence
* contamination migration risk exists
* local stakeholders dispute cleanup.
