## Administrative Rulemaking Structural Review

**Docket:** IRS-2026-0133 — *Section 45Z Clean Fuel Production Credit* 
**Metadata note:** the supplied metadata lists `supporting_related_material_count: 0` for this docket entry. 

## Phase 1 — Docket Integrity Check

**Classification:** **Partial analytical spine**

**Observed fact**
The proposed rule is substantial and contains a long preamble, statutory background, explanation of provisions, and a “Special Analyses” section with a qualitative economic discussion. It also relies heavily on prior guidance, especially Notice 2025-10 and Notice 2025-11, and repeatedly says public feedback on those notices informed the proposed regulations.  

**Observed fact**
In the supplied docket metadata, no supporting related materials are listed. 

**Inference**
For an IRS tax-credit rulemaking, absence of EPA-style technical memoranda or engineering workbooks is not itself abnormal. But for a rule that makes consequential methodological choices about lifecycle emissions, credit eligibility boundaries, and anti-abuse standards, the supplied record is analytically thinner than the substance of the rule would suggest.

**Bottom line**
This is not a “thin record” in the sense of no reasoning at all. But it is **partial** because important analytical support appears to be incorporated by reference, assumed from prior notices, or left qualitative rather than demonstrated in the supplied materials.

## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim

Electricity is excluded from “fuel.”

**Underlying assumption**
Congress meant §45Z to replace prior liquid/gaseous fuel incentives, not reach electricity. 

**Failure scenario**
If statutory text is read more functionally, some commenters could argue electricity used in transportation should not be categorically excluded without a more explicit textual or economic justification.

**Procedural implication**
The rule may need a tighter explanation of why overlap with §45Y compels exclusion, rather than merely suggests it.

---

### Claim

Processor, not compressor, is the RNG producer.

**Underlying assumption**
The processor is “the most active participant,” and compression after interchangeability is only post-production. 

**Failure scenario**
Real-world chains may split processing, upgrading, conditioning, compression, title transfer, and sale across multiple entities in ways not captured by the processor/compressor binary.

**Procedural implication**
Edge cases could produce disputes over claimant identity, double claims, or no claim at all.

---

### Claim

Gasoline is the right gallon-equivalent baseline.

**Underlying assumption**
Gasoline is the most common transportation fuel and an appropriate comparator for non-liquid fuels. 

**Failure scenario**
For fuels substituting more directly for diesel, jet fuel, or natural gas, gasoline may distort equivalence in ways that affect credit amounts.

**Procedural implication**
Commenters can press for justification of why a single gasoline baseline is superior to fuel-specific baselines or an energy-only neutral baseline.

---

### Claim

Annual emissions-rate tables should attach to production year, not construction-start year.

**Underlying assumption**
The statute’s annual-publication language forecloses stakeholder requests for a lock-in rule. 

**Failure scenario**
Large capital projects face regulatory uncertainty if methodology or table values shift materially between financing and production.

**Procedural implication**
The rejection of a lock-in or transition alternative looks underexplained.

---

### Claim

Broad anti-abuse rule based on “primary purpose” and “wasteful” production is administrable.

**Underlying assumption**
Facts-and-circumstances enforcement will be workable and predictable enough. 

**Failure scenario**
Taxpayers may not know ex ante what counts as “productive use,” “wasteful,” or impermissible credit-churning.

**Procedural implication**
This creates notice and administrability concerns unless examples or limiting principles are added.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts

The rule would implement §45Z clean fuel production credit rules, including eligibility, emissions rates, PER procedures, registration, certification, anti-stacking, anti-abuse, ownership/attribution, and claim procedures. It also amends related §§6417, 6418, and excise-tax registration rules.  

Affected entities include domestic producers of clean transportation fuel, taxpayers claiming related producer credits, and excise tax registrants. 

The statutory authority is principally Internal Revenue Code §45Z, plus related authority under §§4101, 6417, 6418, 6001, and 7805(a). 

### Inference

The most consequential regulatory choices are not just administrative mechanics. They are boundary-drawing choices about who counts as a producer, what counts as fuel, which emissions methodologies control, how uncertainty is handled through PER and annual tables, and how anti-abuse standards will police the credit.

## Step 3 — Agency Argument Tree

**Regulatory objective**
Implement the new clean fuel production credit.

**Problem definition**
The statute leaves key terms, emissions methodologies, claimant identity, registration, and anti-abuse issues unresolved. 

**Data used**
Prior notices, public feedback, DOE 45ZCF-GREET, CORSIA methodologies, statutory text, and qualitative economic analysis.  

**Analytical method**
Interpretive preamble reasoning plus adoption of specified lifecycle models and annual emissions-rate tables; PER fills gaps. 

**Alternatives considered**
At least some stakeholder alternatives were considered, such as lock-in of emissions tables and narrower sale definitions; some were accepted, others rejected.  

**Agency conclusion**
The proposed definitions and methods best implement §45Z and congressional purpose.

### Missing links / logical jumps

The main jump is from **purpose-based reasoning** to **specific operational choices** without much quantified comparison. That is especially visible for electricity exclusion, RNG producer identity, gallon-equivalent baseline, anti-abuse breadth, and rejection of construction-year lock-in.

## Step 6 — Technical Coherence

**Assessment:** mixed but incomplete

The rule is technically coherent at a high level: it ties non-SAF to GREET, SAF to CORSIA or similar methods, annual tables to production year, and PER to uncategorized fuels. 

But the coherence is partly asserted rather than demonstrated. The weak points are:

* model-selection consequences are not deeply shown in the supplied record,
* the shift excluding indirect land-use change after December 31, 2025 is consequential but not technically unpacked here,
* PER appears to solve edge cases, but the record here does not fully show timing, burden, or consistency controls.

## Step 17 — High-Leverage Comment Angles

1. **Ask for a clearer methodology record**
   Request a more explicit explanation of how model choice, annual table timing, and PER administration ensure consistency across similarly situated taxpayers.

2. **Press the RNG producer-definition edge cases**
   Argue that the processor/compressor distinction is underexplained for real-world multi-entity arrangements and needs clearer examples or a more nuanced attribution framework.

3. **Challenge the breadth of anti-abuse language**
   Request objective standards, safe harbors, and examples so ordinary commercial conduct is not chilled by vague purpose tests.

4. **Question categorical electricity exclusion**
   Not necessarily to demand inclusion, but to require a tighter legal and economic explanation for exclusion.

5. **Push transition / certainty alternatives**
   Ask Treasury to analyze whether projects should receive construction-year or placed-in-service certainty for emissions tables, at least for limited periods.

## Step 18 — Decision Brief

### Leverage Point 1: Electricity exclusion

**Key sentence**
“Therefore, for purposes of section 45Z, the term ‘fuel’ would not include electricity.” 

**Routine description**
This is a consequential eligibility boundary. Treasury relies on replacement-history logic, anti-stacking structure, and the existence of §45Y to conclude electricity is out. But the supplied rule does not show much comparative analysis of alternative readings or practical consequences.

**Regulatory effectiveness / statutory compliance / procedural adequacy**
Statutory compliance may be plausible, but procedural adequacy looks only moderate because the explanation is categorical and high-level.

**Real-world consequence of invoking the leverage**
Could force Treasury to better explain the line or narrow the rationale, improving the record even if the exclusion remains.

**Worth a comment?** **High**

---

### Leverage Point 2: RNG producer identity

**Key sentence**
The processor is the producer because it is “the most active participant in the production process.” 

**Routine description**
This is a practical tax-claimant rule disguised as a definitional clarification. It matters because real RNG commercialization often splits functions across entities.

**Regulatory effectiveness / statutory compliance / procedural adequacy**
Effectiveness depends on whether actual commercial arrangements fit the agency’s binary. The supplied record does not fully demonstrate that.

**Real-world consequence of invoking the leverage**
Could produce clearer examples, safer attribution rules, or reduced claimant disputes.

**Worth a comment?** **High**

---

### Leverage Point 3: Broad anti-abuse standard

**Key sentence**
“No section 45Z credit is determined if the primary purpose … is to obtain the benefit of the section 45Z credit in a manner that is wasteful …” 

**Routine description**
Treasury is trying to prevent churning and sham production. But the standard is broad and facts-and-circumstances based.

**Regulatory effectiveness / statutory compliance / procedural adequacy**
Potentially useful for enforcement, but procedurally vulnerable if taxpayers cannot tell what is prohibited.

**Real-world consequence of invoking the leverage**
A comment could force Treasury to define “productive use,” add examples, or provide safe harbors.

**Worth a comment?** **High**

---

### Leverage Point 4: Rejection of emissions-table lock-in

**Key sentence**
“The proposed regulations would not adopt this suggestion.” 

**Routine description**
Treasury rejected a predictability-oriented alternative for capital projects.

**Regulatory effectiveness / statutory compliance / procedural adequacy**
The agency may be right legally, but the explanation shown here is thin relative to the financing and planning consequences.

**Real-world consequence of invoking the leverage**
Could yield a transition rule, safe harbor, or more explicit justification.

**Worth a comment?** **Moderate to high**
