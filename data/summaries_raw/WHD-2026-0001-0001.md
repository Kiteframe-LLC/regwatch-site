## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim A

**Claim**
The 2024 rule is too unclear and unpredictable.

**Underlying assumption**
Ambiguity in factor weighting causes real classification uncertainty.

**Failure scenario**
If regulated parties and courts can in practice apply the 2024 rule predictably enough, the need for rescission weakens.

**Procedural implication**
The rule may need stronger evidence of actual confusion, inconsistent outcomes, compliance burdens, or litigation effects.

---

### Claim B

**Claim**
Making control and opportunity the “core” factors better reflects the law.

**Underlying assumption**
The Supreme Court and appellate cases functionally prioritize those two factors often enough to justify regulatory weighting.

**Failure scenario**
If the case law is materially mixed, or if factor salience varies by industry and fact pattern, the proposed weighting could look under-explained rather than clarifying.

**Procedural implication**
A commenter could ask for the case-selection method, corpus, and coding logic used to infer the “core factor” conclusion.

---

### Claim C

**Claim**
The 2024 rule may chill lawful independent contracting.

**Underlying assumption**
Businesses are avoiding bona fide contractor relationships because of perceived legal risk under the 2024 formulation.

**Failure scenario**
If that chilling effect is mostly anecdotal or perception-based, the rule’s policy rationale outruns demonstrated evidence.

**Procedural implication**
Agency may need better empirical support linking regulatory text to market behavior.

---

### Claim D

**Claim**
Uniformity across FLSA, FMLA, and MSPA is warranted because they share the FLSA definition of “employ.”

**Underlying assumption**
Shared statutory language is enough to justify a unified regulatory analysis despite different statutory contexts.

**Failure scenario**
If FMLA or MSPA case law, enforcement context, or statutory purpose introduces meaningful differences, wholesale harmonization may be under-analyzed.

**Procedural implication**
Potential opening for requests for statute-specific analysis.

---

### Claim E

**Claim**
The proposal will generate substantial clarity-related cost savings and litigation reductions.

**Underlying assumption**
The assumptions used in the RIA reasonably predict real behavioral change.

**Failure scenario**
If time-saved assumptions, reclassification assumptions, and relationship-volume assumptions are weak, quantified benefits may be overstated.

**Procedural implication**
Economic analysis becomes vulnerable as speculative or insufficiently transparent.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts

The NPRM proposes to:

* rescind the current 2024 independent-contractor analysis in 29 CFR part 795;
* largely restore the 2021 framework;
* retain a five-factor economic reality test with **control** and **opportunity for profit or loss** treated as the most probative factors;
* apply that analysis not only to the FLSA but also to FMLA and MSPA regulations through conforming revisions. 

The rule relies on the FLSA’s “suffer or permit” employment definition and extensive discussion of Supreme Court and appellate precedent in the preamble.

### Inference

This is a **legal-interpretive and policy-structuring rule** more than a data-driven technical rule. Its real hinge is not whether the economic reality test exists, but **how much regulatory weight the Department assigns to particular factors and how much certainty it promises regulated parties**.

## Step 3 — Agency Argument Tree

**Regulatory objective**
Provide clearer, more predictable independent-contractor guidance.

**Problem definition**
The 2024 rule is too open-ended, repetitive, and restrictive in practice.

**Data used**
Case law history, prior WHD guidance, public comments from prior rulemakings, WHD enforcement experience, and the Department’s own labor-market/economic assumptions.

**Analytical method**
Interpretive synthesis of case law plus economic impact estimation.

**Alternatives considered**
Common-law control test; current WHD enforcement policy; ABC test.

**Agency conclusion**
Rescind the 2024 rule; largely restore the 2021 rule; extend the same approach to FMLA and MSPA.

### Missing links / logical jumps

1. **Empirical jump** from “commenters said the 2024 rule is confusing” to “the 2024 rule materially chills lawful independent contracting.”
2. **Method jump** from case discussion to the conclusion that two factors should generally carry more weight, without a transparent case-coding method.
3. **Scope jump** from shared statutory language to cross-statute uniformity without a deep statute-specific analysis for FMLA and MSPA.
4. **Economic jump** from asserted clarity to quantified litigation and compliance savings.

## Bottom Line

This NPRM is **analytically serious enough to matter**, but not so complete that it is “nothing to see here.” Its main vulnerabilities are not on basic legal authority. They are on the **quality of explanation**:

* why two factors deserve systematic priority,
* what evidence shows the 2024 rule caused the harms claimed,
* why narrower alternatives were not enough,
* and how the quantified benefits were derived.

That is fertile ground for **constructive comments aimed at strengthening the administrative record**, especially by demanding method, evidence, and narrower-alternative analysis.
