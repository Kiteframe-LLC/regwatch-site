## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim: Michigan's revised VOC rules are still representative of RACT

- **Underlying assumption:** Cross-state comparisons and CTG consistency are enough to show that no tighter reasonably available controls now exist for the covered source categories.
- **Failure scenario:** Michigan's rules lag newer control practices, or the comparison set omits more recent or more stringent approaches that would materially change the RACT analysis.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA may need to disclose the comparison table and benchmark logic more fully so commenters can test whether the "mostly consistent" conclusion is robust.

### Claim: No new reasonably available controls have emerged

- **Underlying assumption:** EPA's due-diligence review was comprehensive enough across technologies, source categories, and states.
- **Failure scenario:** The analysis screened out relevant control developments or treated the absence of identified technologies as equivalent to a demonstrated feasibility review.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask EPA to disclose the source categories, technologies reviewed, and criteria used to decide when a technology is "reasonably available."

### Claim: Higher coating-usage exemptions will not increase emissions

- **Underlying assumption:** Michigan's emissions comparison accurately reflects real facility operations and the facilities likely to use the higher exemptions.
- **Failure scenario:** The state relied on narrow or atypical usage patterns, so higher thresholds could permit more uncontrolled VOC use than the summary implies.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA's section `110(l)` conclusion would benefit from a clearer public showing of baseline assumptions and scenario analysis.

### Claim: Negative declarations are complete

- **Underlying assumption:** Michigan's use of MAERS, MACES, district outreach, and stakeholder input captured all relevant sources in the Western Michigan areas.
- **Failure scenario:** Small or borderline facilities were missed, or facility classifications changed after the review.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA should show the screening logic with enough detail for the public to test whether any omitted sources should have triggered category controls.

### Claim: Moderate-area RACT approval can proceed even though the area is now Serious

- **Underlying assumption:** Approving the overdue Moderate package now still has practical regulatory value and does not mask unresolved Serious-area obligations.
- **Failure scenario:** The Moderate-only framing understates whether the approved rules are already outdated for the area's current classification.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask EPA to explain how this action fits with the later Serious-area requirements and whether any approved rules are interim only.

### Claim: The state-rule exclusions are appropriately carved out

- **Underlying assumption:** The exclusions for Rule `336.1601(a)`, Rule `336.1632(9)`, and the deferred NOx subrules do not create hidden gaps in enforceable control coverage.
- **Failure scenario:** The omitted provisions matter more than the preamble suggests, weakening practical enforceability or leaving unresolved regulatory holes.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA should explain why these carveouts and deferrals do not undermine the claimed overall RACT showing.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts

- EPA proposes to approve revisions and additions to Michigan Air Pollution Control Rules Parts 6 and 8 into the SIP for Western Michigan's 2015 ozone nonattainment areas.
- The proposal covers both VOC and NOx RACT requirements for Moderate nonattainment under Clean Air Act sections `182(b)(2)` and `182(f)`.
- EPA also proposes to approve Michigan Rules `336.1660` and `336.1662`, governing consumer products and architectural and industrial maintenance coatings, as SIP-strengthening measures rather than as RACT rules.
- EPA is not proposing to approve every submitted provision. It excludes Rule `336.1601(a)`, excludes Rule `336.1632(9)` while replacing the rest of Rule `336.1632`, and defers action on Rule `336.1841(8)`, `336.1842(10)`, and `336.1844(9)`.
- EPA states that the Western Michigan areas were reclassified from Marginal to Moderate effective November 7, 2022, and then reclassified to Serious effective January 16, 2025.
- EPA says Michigan submitted VOC revisions on March 7, 2024, supplemented on May 2, 2024, and submitted NOx revisions on May 5, 2025, supplemented on August 6, 2025.

### Inference

- This is a comparison-driven SIP approval more than a source-specific engineering rulemaking. EPA is largely deciding whether Michigan's chosen rule set is good enough to count as RACT, not designing new federal limits line by line in the preamble.
- The highest-value public comment opportunities are therefore about transparency, comparison methodology, omitted provisions, and whether a Moderate-area package is sufficiently explained given the area's later Serious reclassification.

### Evidence Quotes

- "Effective January 16, 2025, the EPA determined that the Western Michigan nonattainment areas failed to attain by the Moderate area attainment date and the areas were reclassified to Serious by operation of law."

## Step 10 — Regulatory Incentives

- Michigan has an incentive to preserve a rule package that is administratively workable and broadly comparable to neighboring states rather than materially more stringent.
- EPA has an incentive to rely on state-developed comparison tables and a TSD rather than restating every technical comparison in the preamble.
- Regulated entities benefit when older rules remain acceptable through a "no new reasonably available controls" finding.
- Public commenters therefore have the most leverage when they ask for the hidden comparison benchmarks, emissions scenarios, and category-by-category reasoning.
