### Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

#### Claim A

**Claim**
Low use of appeals indicates a problem with fairness or perceived retaliation risk.

**Underlying assumption**
Observed low appeal volume is driven materially by dissatisfaction with the process, not by successful informal resolution or low dispute frequency.

**Failure scenario**
If most disputes are resolved informally or if only a small subset of examinations generate appeal-worthy determinations, then the “1 percent” statistic becomes a weak indicator of process failure.

**Procedural implication**
The need for structural redesign would require stronger evidence connecting low appeal volume to the identified defects.

#### Claim B

**Claim**
An express de novo standard will improve confidence and usage of the appeals process.

**Underlying assumption**
Potential appellants understand, trust, and materially respond to announced review standards.

**Failure scenario**
If banks are more deterred by relationship-management concerns, confidentiality concerns, or cost and timing than by the review standard, de novo language may produce less change than predicted.

**Procedural implication**
Commenters can request evidence separating standard-of-review effects from other deterrents.

#### Claim C

**Claim**
Replacing the Ombudsman with a mixed Appeals Board increases independence.

**Underlying assumption**
A board that includes the Chief National Bank Examiner and two Comptroller-appointed term appointees will be perceived as more independent than the current Ombudsman-centered structure.

**Failure scenario**
If regulated parties view the Chief National Bank Examiner's presence, the Comptroller's appointment power, outside-employment possibilities for term appointees, and the Comptroller's override authority as offsetting features, the board may not produce the confidence gain claimed.

**Procedural implication**
The final rule record should explain why the selected board design improves independence relative to the alternatives the OCC itself identifies.

#### Claim D

**Claim**
Publishing redacted decisions and dissents will improve transparency and consistency.

**Underlying assumption**
Published decisions will be detailed and frequent enough to guide future appellants without disclosing supervisory information.

**Failure scenario**
If redactions are heavy, publication is delayed, or opinions are too fact-specific to generalize, the transparency benefit may be thinner than described.

**Procedural implication**
Commenters can ask for minimum content standards, publication timing, and indexing commitments.

#### Claim E

**Claim**
The proposal does not significantly burden a substantial number of small entities.

**Underlying assumption**
Small banks will not materially increase appeals activity or incur high enough appeal costs under the new process.

**Failure scenario**
If the proposal succeeds in encouraging more small-entity appeals, the same success mechanism could also raise compliance and representation costs for those institutions.

**Procedural implication**
The small-entity analysis should reconcile “greater use of appeals” with “no significant small-entity impact.”

### Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

#### Observed Facts

The OCC proposes to replace guidance-based procedures for appealing material supervisory determinations with codified procedures in 12 CFR part 4. The proposal would:

- create an Appeals Board made up of the Chief National Bank Examiner and two nonrenewable term appointees;
- require de novo review by the Deputy Comptroller and the Appeals Board;
- allow stays of supervisory determinations during appeal when specified conditions are met;
- shift the Ombudsman from final decisionmaker to liaison, anti-retaliation, complaint, and reporting functions;
- require publication of redacted board decisions and dissents; and
- preserve a broad list of appealable and nonappealable matters while clarifying several categories.

The NPRM grounds the rule in the Riegle Community Development and Regulatory Improvement Act requirement for an independent intra-agency appellate process and anti-retaliation safeguards.

#### Inference

This is a governance-and-process rule rather than a technical prudential rule. Its central question is not whether appeals should exist, but whether the OCC has adequately shown that this particular combination of board composition, review standard, publication, stay authority, and Ombudsman redesign is the best way to improve fairness and confidence.

### Step 3 — Agency Argument Tree

**Regulatory objective**  
Increase confidence in, independence of, and efficiency of OCC appeals of material supervisory determinations.

**Problem definition**  
Few appeals are filed; current standards are unclear; institutions may fear retaliation or believe the OCC usually wins.

**Data used**  
Historical appeal counts, uphold rates, examples from fair-lending and Shared National Credit appeals, statutory requirements, and OCC experience administering the current guidance.

**Analytical method**  
Institutional-design reasoning: identify perceived weaknesses, compare with other agencies' review standards, and redesign the appeals structure to reduce perceived dependence and increase transparency.

**Alternatives considered**  
The NPRM explicitly asks for comment on alternative Appeals Board compositions, whether to keep the Ombudsman as decisionmaker, whether to use a different standard of review, whether term lengths should be longer, and whether appointees may serve part time with outside employment.

**Agency conclusion**  
Codified de novo review, a new Appeals Board, publication of decisions, enhanced stay authority, and a refocused Ombudsman will make the process fairer and more trusted.

**Missing links / logical jumps**

- The NPRM shows low appeal volume and high OCC win rates, but the causal bridge from those facts to the proposed institutional design is only partly demonstrated.
- The proposal claims an independence gain while retaining several internal-control features, including OCC leadership participation, Comptroller appointment authority, attorney review for legal conformity, and explicit Comptroller override power.
- The economic discussion assumes more appeals will be filed, but the record excerpt does not clearly show why those additional appeals will be socially beneficial on net, beyond the general fairness rationale.

### Step 5 — Missing Alternatives Detector

#### Objective: increase independence and confidence in appeals

**Alternatives evaluated**

- Keep the Ombudsman as decisionmaker.
- Use different Appeals Board compositions.
- Use different terms for appointees.
- Use a more deferential standard than de novo.

**Plausible alternatives not analyzed in depth**

- Three external members with no OCC line official on the board.
- A board with fixed ethics and disclosure restrictions that prohibit outside employment.
- Staggered multi-year terms with a removal-for-cause standard to reduce perceived dependence.
- Publication of anonymized precedent summaries even where full opinions cannot be released.

**Evidence suggesting feasibility**

The OCC itself asks whether the board could be composed differently and whether longer terms, staggered terms, or different members would better ensure independence.

#### Objective: improve fairness without undermining supervisory responsiveness

**Alternatives evaluated**

- Maintain the Deputy Comptroller first-level review.
- Add stay authority based on specified criteria.
- Allow expedited treatment for certain matters.

**Plausible alternatives not analyzed in depth**

- Automatic temporary administrative stays pending threshold review.
- A narrower pilot program limited to particular appeal categories.
- Separate standards for legal issues versus factual supervisory determinations.

**Evidence suggesting feasibility**

The proposal already differentiates among appeal categories and explicitly seeks comment on stay criteria, timelines, and expedited procedures.

### Step 6 — Technical Coherence

This rule does not rely on engineering or scientific modeling, but it does rely on institutional-effect predictions.

**Observed Fact**
The NPRM consistently ties its reforms to four recurring themes: independence, clarity, retaliation protection, and transparency.

**Inference**
The proposal is internally coherent at the conceptual level: each major reform maps to one of those themes.

**Primary coherence risk**
The claimed independence gains are partially offset by the retained internal hierarchy. The same text that highlights independence also preserves:

- Chief National Bank Examiner membership on the Appeals Board;
- direct Comptroller appointment of term appointees;
- Comptroller authority to decide recusal deadlocks and overturn Appeals Board decisions; and
- possible part-time appointees with outside employment.

That does not make the proposal incoherent, but it does mean the “more independent” claim needs a more explicit limiting explanation.

### Step 7 — Legal Grounding

**Observed Fact**
The NPRM repeatedly invokes 12 U.S.C. 4806 and related provisions requiring an independent intra-agency appellate process, expeditious decisions, and anti-retaliation safeguards.

**Assessment**
The proposal appears legally grounded in the OCC's statutory authority to structure its internal appeals process. The basic move from guidance to regulation, the use of de novo review, publication of anonymized decisions, and anti-retaliation provisions all fit comfortably within that framework.

**Uncertain point**
The statute requires independence, but the NPRM does not fully resolve how much internal OCC control is compatible with that requirement. The proposed design may still be lawful, but commenters can reasonably ask the OCC to explain why Comptroller override authority and OCC leadership participation do not materially undercut the “independent” character of the process.

### Step 8 — Procedural Normalcy

**Classification:** Normal

**Observed Fact**
The NPRM provides statutory background, describes the current process, identifies perceived weaknesses, proposes specific regulatory text, and asks extensive targeted questions on controversial design choices.

**Inference**
This looks like a normal NPRM for an agency governance rule. The main record issue is not irregular procedure; it is whether the agency has adequately supported the institutional assumptions behind its chosen design.

### Step 9 — Failure Modes

- Banks may still avoid appeals if relationship-management concerns matter more than formal review standards.
- A board with short-term appointees may experience turnover, delays, or inconsistent precedent.
- Outside-employment arrangements for part-time term appointees could create perceived or actual conflict concerns.
- Publication may be too heavily redacted to create meaningful precedent.
- The Comptroller's retained override authority could weaken confidence in board independence even if rarely used.

### Step 10 — Regulatory Incentives

**Potential incentives created by the rule**

- More institutions may appeal borderline supervisory determinations because de novo review and stay opportunities improve the expected value of appealing.
- Institutions may frame requests to fit the stay criteria, especially cost and public-interest considerations.
- OCC supervisory staff may face pressure to build more fully documented records anticipating de novo review and published decisions.

**Potential gaming concerns**

- Appellants may characterize matters to fit appealable categories while avoiding excluded enforcement-related categories.
- If term appointees can hold outside employment, parties may perceive incentives or affiliations that create skepticism about neutrality.

### Step 11 — Evidentiary Strength

**Overall assessment:** Moderate

The NPRM's legal and procedural descriptions are strong. The weaker area is empirical support for the rule's behavioral predictions.

**Observed Fact**
The OCC supplies historical appeal counts and outcome rates, explains the current process, and identifies similarities and differences with other banking regulators.

**Inference**
That is enough to justify rulemaking interest, but not enough by itself to show that the proposed specific structure is the best or only reasonable solution.

**Main weakness**
The proposal treats several important propositions as plausible rather than demonstrated:

- that review-standard ambiguity is a major deterrent;
- that the board structure will materially increase perceived independence;
- that more appeals will improve the system enough to justify added cost and complexity; and
- that small entities will not face meaningful burdens despite the expected increase in appeal activity.

### Step 13 — Litigation Vulnerabilities

This NPRM does not present an obvious major APA defect on its face, but three themes could matter if left underexplained in a final rule:

- inadequate explanation for choosing this board composition over identified alternatives;
- unsupported assumptions that the proposal meaningfully improves independence and confidence; and
- insufficient reconciliation of expected increased appeal activity with the small-entity certification.

These are better framed as record-strengthening comment opportunities than as primary adversarial attacks at the proposal stage.

### Step 16 — Evidence Requests

- Survey or interview evidence showing why supervised institutions forgo appeals under the current process.
- Comparative analysis of actual outcomes under other federal banking agencies' appeal structures, not just their formal standards.
- Detailed rationale for the chosen Appeals Board composition against the alternative configurations listed in the NPRM.
- Ethics and recusal framework for part-time or externally employed term appointees.
- Small-entity sensitivity analysis showing how many additional appeals by smaller institutions the OCC assumes and what advisory or legal costs those appeals would impose.
- Publication protocol describing timing, indexing, minimum content, and redaction principles for Appeals Board opinions and dissents.

### Step 17 — High-Leverage Comment Angles

#### 1. Ask the OCC to prove the deterrence theory

Request concrete evidence that low appeal usage reflects process design defects rather than informal resolution patterns or a low underlying rate of appealable disputes.

#### 2. Press for a fuller independence comparison

Ask the OCC to compare the proposed board against at least two alternatives: a fully external board and a strengthened Ombudsman model. The proposal already identifies these as realistic options.

#### 3. Ask for conflict safeguards if outside employment is allowed

Request explicit disqualification, disclosure, and cooling-off rules for term appointees, especially if the OCC adopts part-time service or permits private-sector work.

#### 4. Ask the OCC to reconcile increased appeals with the RFA certification

Request a short sensitivity analysis showing how more appeals, more stays, and more formal review time affect small institutions differently from large ones.

#### 5. Ask for transparency standards, not just publication

Request deadlines, searchable precedent summaries, and minimum explanatory content for published decisions so that the publication requirement has practical value.

## Bottom Line

This is a serious and procedurally normal NPRM, not a thin or obviously pretextual one. The best comment opportunities are not attacks on the OCC's authority to revise its appeals process, but requests that it better prove three things: why current appeal use is low, why this exact board structure is meaningfully more independent, and why the expected increase in appeals does not create undercounted burden for smaller institutions.

The representative comments available in the export are mostly supportive, including a longer organizational comment favoring greater independence and transparency. That means the highest-value contribution from a new commenter is likely not broad endorsement alone, but targeted requests for stronger evidence and cleaner institutional design choices before finalization.
