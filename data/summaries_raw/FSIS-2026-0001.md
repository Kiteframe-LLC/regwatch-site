## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim: Current HACCP categories no longer fit present-day operations well
- **Underlying assumption:** The 1996 employee-and-sales thresholds no longer capture meaningful differences in present-day establishment scale, resource availability, or ownership structure.
- **Failure scenario:** If the old categories still predict compliance capacity and regulatory burden reasonably well, changing the framework could add complexity without improving agency targeting.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters should test whether the current categories actually fail in identifiable settings rather than assuming age alone makes them obsolete.

### Claim: Ownership structure matters for classifying establishments
- **Underlying assumption:** Affiliation with a larger parent or network can materially change the resources available to a nominally small plant.
- **Failure scenario:** If ownership data are incomplete, unstable, or hard to verify, a corporate-affiliation metric could become hard to administer and easy to dispute.
- **Procedural implication:** FSIS needs comments on what ownership indicators are both meaningful and administrable.

### Claim: Production volume may be a better primary metric for some regulatory purposes
- **Underlying assumption:** Production or slaughter volume maps more closely than employee count to the operational realities that matter for sampling frequency, implementation burden, or oversight intensity.
- **Failure scenario:** Volume alone could misclassify establishments with unusual risk profiles, product complexity, or labor intensity.
- **Procedural implication:** Comments can help FSIS distinguish when volume is the right proxy and when it is not.

### Claim: Aligning with SBA standards may create unfair advantages
- **Underlying assumption:** SBA employee thresholds are too high or otherwise misaligned with the operational realities FSIS needs to regulate.
- **Failure scenario:** If SBA standards improve cross-program consistency without distorting inspection or compliance decisions, rejecting them could preserve fragmentation for little gain.
- **Procedural implication:** FSIS would benefit from comments that compare the costs of alignment against the costs of maintaining a distinct FSIS-specific system.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

**Observed Facts**
- This document is an ANPRM, not a proposed rule with draft regulatory text.
- FSIS is asking whether and how it should revise the establishment size definitions it uses across oversight, regulatory analysis, phased compliance, reduced-fee eligibility, sampling frequencies, and related assistance functions.
- The agency describes two main sizing approaches already in use: traditional HACCP categories based on employee count and annual sales, and several volume-based categories based on processed pounds or head slaughtered.
- The notice highlights a practical tension between establishment-level measures and corporate-affiliation measures.
- FSIS asks eight broad sets of questions covering economic impacts, SBA alignment, additional categories, production and product characteristics, ownership structure, outside uses of FSIS size labels, record collection, and data sources.
- The notice relies on authority under the FMIA, PPIA, EPIA, and the Regulatory Flexibility Act context, but it does not propose regulatory text or a quantified impact analysis.

**Evidence quotes**
- "This advance notice of proposed rulemaking (ANPR) requests stakeholder input on whether FSIS should update its establishment size definitions and, if so, how."
- "These size definitions are applied to individual establishments and do not account for whether the establishment is part of a larger corporate structure."
- "FSIS has also relied on various production volume thresholds."

**Inference**
- The practical issue is not simply terminology. FSIS is deciding which size metrics should drive regulatory tailoring, assistance eligibility, and possibly the distribution of compliance burdens across independent plants, vertically integrated firms, and mid-sized establishments.

## Step 9 — Failure Modes

- FSIS could adopt a more complex classification system that produces marginal analytical benefits while imposing high reporting and verification burdens.
- Ownership-sensitive categories could be underinclusive if corporate support is informal, or overinclusive if they ignore operational independence.
- Volume-based categories could favor large but specialized establishments or disadvantage low-volume plants with unusually high product complexity.
- If one size framework is used for too many regulatory purposes, the final approach may fit none of them particularly well.
- If FSIS relies heavily on self-reported data without clear audit rules, the system could become inconsistent across establishments.

## Step 10 — Regulatory Incentives

- A revised system that accounts for ownership could reduce incentives for larger firms to structure affiliates so they appear small for assistance or phased-compliance purposes.
- A system tied too tightly to production thresholds could incentivize establishments to manage output around category breakpoints.
- More granular categories could help FSIS target assistance and sampling more precisely, but they could also create category-management behavior if regulatory consequences are significant.

1. Ask FSIS to separate the purposes of establishment-size definitions and explain whether a single label really should control economic analysis, grants or assistance, fee treatment, and sampling consequences.

3. Ask FSIS to define what kinds of corporate support or affiliation should matter, and which should not, so commenters can test administrability instead of debating ownership in the abstract.
4. Ask FSIS to explain how it would prevent category thresholds from becoming stale again, for example through periodic review or indexed updates.
5. Ask FSIS to provide a low-burden comment template or public dataset references so small and very small establishments can participate without proprietary analytics.

## Step 18 — Decision Brief

### Leverage Point 1: One size definition may not fit every FSIS function
- **Key sentence from the notice:** "FSIS has also relied on various production volume thresholds."
- **Routine description situated in context:** The ANPRM itself shows that FSIS already uses different metrics in different programs, which weakens the assumption that one universal size label is necessarily best.
- **Regulatory effectiveness, statutory compliance, procedural adequacy:** A comment pushing for purpose-specific definitions could improve regulatory fit without requiring FSIS to defend a single all-purpose threshold system.
- **Real-world consequence of invoking the leverage:** This could reduce the risk that establishments are misclassified for some functions merely because the agency wanted uniform labels.
- **Worth a comment?** High

### Leverage Point 2: Ownership matters, but only if it can be defined and verified
- **Key sentence from the notice:** "These size definitions are applied to individual establishments and do not account for whether the establishment is part of a larger corporate structure."
- **Routine description situated in context:** FSIS has identified a real concern, but the agency has not yet shown what ownership evidence it can reliably collect or how it will distinguish meaningful support from nominal affiliation.
- **Regulatory effectiveness, statutory compliance, procedural adequacy:** A comment can help FSIS build a record on administrability, which is likely to matter as much as conceptual fairness.
- **Real-world consequence of invoking the leverage:** Better definitions could prevent both underinclusive treatment of vertically supported plants and overinclusive treatment of operationally independent establishments.
- **Worth a comment?** High

### Leverage Point 3: FSIS needs to show classification consequences before choosing thresholds
- **Key sentence from the notice:** "FSIS is seeking public input to help inform potential changes to how the Agency defines establishment sizes to better reflect how establishments operate and are structured."
- **Routine description situated in context:** The agency is appropriately seeking input early, but the record would be much stronger if commenters and FSIS could compare how candidate systems would actually re-sort establishments.
- **Regulatory effectiveness, statutory compliance, procedural adequacy:** This is a constructive record-building request, not an attack on the ANPRM process.
- **Real-world consequence of invoking the leverage:** Reclassification modeling could reveal whether a proposed system changes outcomes meaningfully or just reshuffles labels.
- **Worth a comment?** High
