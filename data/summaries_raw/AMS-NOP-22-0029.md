## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim A

**Claim:** Carbon dioxide meets OFPA criteria for the proposed crop uses.

**Underlying assumption:** The cited record adequately shows low environmental/human-health risk and lack of commercially available alternatives for both irrigation-water acidification and greenhouse atmospheric adjustment.

**Failure scenario:** Natural carbon dioxide, sulfur-burner approaches, or other allowed inputs are in fact more available or workable than the NPRM implies, or byproduct sourcing is not consistently verifiable across suppliers.

**Procedural implication:** The necessity and compatibility findings for the carbon-dioxide listings would need tighter evidence or narrower annotations.

### Claim B

**Claim:** A doubled FDA withdrawal period plus veterinarian order is a sufficient meloxicam safeguard.

**Underlying assumption:** Producers, veterinarians, and certifiers can determine and verify the doubled period consistently across species and uses.

**Failure scenario:** FDA baselines are not uniform or readily usable in organic compliance records, especially for bovine milk/meat uses or extra-label contexts.

**Procedural implication:** The final rule could face administrability objections unless AMS specifies clearer floors or examples.

### Claim C

**Claim:** Removing methionine limits improves flock health and reduces burdens without undermining organic principles.

**Underlying assumption:** Existing numeric caps drive protein overfeeding and paperwork more than they constrain inappropriate synthetic dependence.

**Failure scenario:** Large producers use the deregulatory change to substitute synthetic methionine more broadly than the record anticipates, weakening incentives to pursue feed-based or natural alternatives.

**Procedural implication:** AMS may need clearer guardrails on nutritional necessity, monitoring, or post-change review.

### Claim D

**Claim:** The sodium nitrate listing should be renewed and treated as affirming current law.

**Underlying assumption:** The sunset-history explanation and the 20 percent nitrogen cap are enough to justify reinstatement without additional current market or soil-quality analysis.

**Failure scenario:** The prior expiration changed producer behavior or created interpretive uncertainty that the NPRM does not squarely analyze.

**Procedural implication:** Commenters could ask AMS to explain the practical consequences of the expired status and why reinstatement is still justified on the present record.

### Claim E

**Claim:** The rule will not have a significant economic impact on a substantial number of small entities.

**Underlying assumption:** Added flexibility and reduced recordkeeping outweigh compliance, sourcing, veterinary, and certifier costs.

**Failure scenario:** Small organic operations face nontrivial new sourcing verification or veterinary-order burdens, especially where specialized inputs are thinly available.

**Procedural implication:** The RFA certification would look underexplained unless AMS offers more concrete burden estimates or examples.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts

* The NPRM would add synthetic carbon dioxide to § 205.601(a) and § 205.601(j), with the § 205.601(j) listing limited to byproduct-sourced carbon dioxide.
* The NPRM would renew sodium nitrate at § 205.602(h) with the existing 20 percent crop-nitrogen cap.
* The NPRM would add meloxicam to § 205.603(a) with veterinarian-order and doubled-withdrawal requirements.
* The NPRM would remove flock-average quantitative limits from the methionine allowance in § 205.603(d)(1).
* The agency cites OFPA and NOSB recommendation procedures as the rule’s legal backbone.
* The NPRM asks targeted questions about source restrictions for carbon dioxide and withdrawal details for meloxicam.

### Inference

This is a multi-substance maintenance-and-expansion rule for the National Organic Program. Its main vulnerability is not lack of statutory authority; it is that several necessity, source-verification, and burden conclusions are summarized at a high level while the underlying technical and market evidence remains mostly outside the supplied record.

* “AMS agrees with the NOSB recommendation to allow methionine as an organic poultry feed additive without limits on the amount of methionine that may be added to organic poultry rations.”

## Step 6 — Technical Coherence

**Assessment:** Generally coherent, but some implementation details remain under-explained.

The carbon-dioxide proposal is internally coherent in distinguishing irrigation-water pH adjustment from indoor atmospheric adjustment. The main uncertainty is not scientific impossibility but record visibility on supply, source verification, and whether the two uses should have matching annotations.

The meloxicam proposal is coherent as a pain-management allowance, but its compliance logic depends on external FDA withdrawal benchmarks and veterinary practice. That creates a practical translation problem for producers and certifiers.

The methionine proposal is coherent as deregulatory relief, yet the text does not show much evidence about how producers will avoid simply substituting synthetic supplementation for other feed-management changes.

### Strongest leverage point: **Carbon-dioxide source limits and proof of necessity**

**Key NPRM sentences**

* “AMS also agrees with the NOSB's conclusions that allowed natural and synthetic alternatives to the specific petitioned uses of synthetic carbon dioxide are not commercially available.”
* “AMS requests comments about whether the proposed listing in 7 CFR 205.601(a) should include the same annotation limiting the sources of carbon dioxide proposed in § 205.601(j).”

**Routine description in context**
This is a normal National List amendment rule, but the carbon-dioxide piece does important line-drawing work. The agency is adding a new synthetic input to organic crop production while partly relying on a byproduct/recycling rationale to justify compatibility.

**Regulatory effectiveness / statutory compliance / procedural adequacy**
If AMS relies on byproduct sourcing to show environmental compatibility, commenters can reasonably ask why that logic applies clearly to greenhouse use but remains optional for irrigation-water use. That is a high-leverage NPRM question because the agency is already inviting comment on it.

**Real-world consequence of invoking the leverage**
A focused comment could push AMS to tighten the listing, adopt a more enforceable traceability standard, or produce a clearer explanation of when non-byproduct sourcing would still satisfy OFPA criteria.
