## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim

Ratings are inflated (99% ≥ “Fully Successful”).

Underlying assumption
Inflation reflects **evaluation system failure**, not workforce quality or rating standards.

Failure scenario
High ratings could arise from:

* strong workforce performance
* conservative rating scales
* mission-specific performance cultures

Procedural implication
If inflation is not caused by system design, structural reforms (forced distribution, rating level removal) may not correct the problem.

---

### Claim

Removing Level 2 rating categories improves performance differentiation.

Underlying assumption
Fewer rating levels produce clearer distinctions.

Failure scenario
Agencies may compensate through **informal distinctions** or narrative evaluations.

Procedural implication
The change may shift, not solve, differentiation problems.

---

### Claim

Forced distribution authority improves accountability.

Underlying assumption
Performance ratings should follow a **statistical distribution**.

Failure scenario
Small work units or specialized roles may not support a meaningful distribution.

Procedural implication
Agencies may artificially downgrade ratings to meet distribution targets.

## Step 18 — Decision Brief

### Leverage Point

**Forced distribution authorization**

Key sentence

> “OPM proposes… removing the prohibition of a forced… distribution of performance rating levels.”

Routine description
The rule allows OPM to impose standardized rating distributions across agencies to correct perceived rating inflation.

Regulatory effectiveness
Uncertain. Forced distributions may improve differentiation but risk artificial rating outcomes.

Statutory compliance
Likely permissible under CSRA authority, though implementation scope could be contested.

Procedural adequacy
Evidence linking the mechanism to improved performance management appears limited.

Real-world consequence
Could significantly alter federal employee evaluation dynamics and manager discretion.
