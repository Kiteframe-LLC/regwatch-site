## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim: Illinois' Q/d screening captured the right set of sources

- **Underlying assumption:** A unit-level `Q/d` threshold of four and source-level threshold of ten are reasonable screens for sources that matter for visibility.
- **Failure scenario:** Smaller or differently configured units below those thresholds still contribute materially to downwind Class I visibility impairment, or 2016-based screening misses newer operational realities.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask EPA to show sensitivity testing or explain why alternative thresholds would not change the set of sources requiring four-factor review.

### Claim: Existing controls at Baldwin, Newton, Powerton, and Kincaid made new four-factor review unnecessary

- **Underlying assumption:** Existing MPS, CPS, BART, and permit requirements are stable, sufficiently stringent, and representative of future operating conditions through 2028.
- **Failure scenario:** Existing controls leave headroom for feasible additional reductions, or historic emissions consistency masks future dispatch or fuel-use changes.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA may need to show why "existing effective controls" is not simply a shortcut that avoids a fuller comparison of tighter options.

### Claim: Additional controls for the nine selected units are not cost-effective

- **Underlying assumption:** Illinois' cost inputs, expected reductions, and remaining-useful-life assumptions are accurate and appropriately conservative.
- **Failure scenario:** Different retrofit configurations, better capture assumptions, or different useful-life assumptions materially improve the cost-effectiveness picture.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters have leverage to request the underlying engineering and cost worksheets rather than accept bottom-line `$ / ton` values.

### Claim: Illinois adequately documented its technical basis

- **Underlying assumption:** Appendix A, appendix B, LADCO materials, and EPA's TSD are publicly accessible and detailed enough for meaningful comment.
- **Failure scenario:** The public-facing record exposes only summaries, leaving commenters unable to test source-selection, modeling, and cost assumptions.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA's documentation finding may be vulnerable if meaningful public review depends on materials that are hard to locate or not machine-readable in the surfaced docket package.

### Claim: A fully approved regional haze SIP satisfies Prong 4 for the cited NAAQS

- **Underlying assumption:** The 2024 regional haze approval adequately addresses the same visibility-protection concerns relevant to the 2012 PM2.5 and 2015 ozone infrastructure submissions.
- **Failure scenario:** The regional haze analysis is incomplete, delayed, or not fully coextensive with the infrastructure visibility obligations.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask EPA to explain the mapping between the regional haze record and the specific infrastructure-SIP obligations instead of relying on a high-level guidance statement.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts

- EPA proposes to approve Illinois' June 3, 2024 regional haze SIP revision for the second implementation period.
- Illinois has no Class I areas within its borders, so the rule focuses on Illinois sources that affect Class I areas in other states.
- Illinois used LADCO's `Q/d` methodology and selected nine units for fresh four-factor analyses while treating seven other units as already subject to effective existing controls.
- EPA says Illinois' source-selection record, four-factor analyses, interstate consultation, and modeling support satisfy `40 CFR 51.308(f)`.
- EPA also proposes to approve portions of Illinois' 2012 PM2.5 and 2015 ozone infrastructure SIPs on the theory that an adequately approved second-period regional haze SIP satisfies the visibility-protection requirement in `CAA section 110(a)(2)(D)(i)(II)`.

### Inference

- The most commentable issue is not whether EPA may approve a SIP in principle, but whether the public-facing record shows enough of the source-selection, cost, and modeling logic to justify EPA's concurrence.

- EPA may finalize a source-selection methodology without adequately showing why different `Q/d` thresholds or different emissions years would not materially change the set of reviewed units.
- EPA may ratify "existing effective controls" at seven units without giving the public enough information to test whether those controls remain sufficient through 2028.

## Step 10 — Regulatory Incentives

- Illinois has an incentive to rely on existing controls and avoid costly new controls where it can credibly argue reasonable progress is already being made.

- Downwind states have an incentive to push for broader source review or tighter controls where Illinois emissions affect their Class I areas.
- Commenters therefore have the most leverage by asking EPA to surface hidden assumptions and comparative benchmarks, not by asserting general opposition to SIP approvals.
