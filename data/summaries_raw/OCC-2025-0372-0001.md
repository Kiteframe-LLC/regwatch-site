## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim A

**Claim:** Regulatory clarity and simplification will stimulate issuance.
**Underlying assumption:** Uncertainty is a major binding constraint on market entry or expansion.
**Failure scenario:** Issuance is constrained instead by economics, bank-risk appetite, market concentration, AML/compliance cost, or state/federal overlap.
**Procedural implication:** If the causal driver is wrong, the rule’s claimed benefit narrative is underexplained.

### Claim B

**Claim:** New York law is a suitable baseline for non-OCC regulated issuers.
**Underlying assumption:** New York is the right proxy for the “without-rule” world.
**Failure scenario:** Many likely entrants would not otherwise operate under New York’s regime, or would structure around other states or nonbank pathways.
**Procedural implication:** Incremental costs and benefits could be materially misstated.

### Claim C

**Claim:** Either Option A or Option B for reserve diversification is appropriately calibratable.
**Underlying assumption:** The OCC has enough empirical basis to choose percentage thresholds or safe-harbor levels.
**Failure scenario:** Thresholds are judgment calls rather than evidence-driven breakpoints.
**Procedural implication:** Threshold justification becomes vulnerable as arbitrary line-drawing.

### Claim D

**Claim:** Overlapping reserve-custody requirements can be managed by agreements and multi-regulator implementation.
**Underlying assumption:** Contracting plus regulator coordination is enough to avoid inconsistent obligations.
**Failure scenario:** Different regulators impose materially incompatible section 10 implementations.
**Procedural implication:** Operational feasibility and compliance burden may be underanalyzed.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts

* The rule implements portions of the GENIUS Act for entities under OCC jurisdiction. 
* It applies to stablecoin issuance, reserves, custody, applications/registrations, and capital requirements for specified OCC-regulated or OCC-supervised entities. 
* The rule leaves some statutory provisions uncodified on the ground that they are self-executing. 
* The rule contains alternative reserve-diversification structures and numerous requests for comment on calibration. 

### Inference

This is a hybrid legal-policy and prudential-risk rulemaking. Its main burden is not emissions-style modeling but line-drawing, baseline selection, and justification of supervisory thresholds in a novel market.

## Step 6 — Technical Coherence

**Assessment:** Mixed, with the main issue being not engineering incoherence but calibration opacity.

The proposal is internally coherent in structure: reserves, custody, monetization, and capital are linked. But the quantitative design appears only partly justified in the visible text. The rule repeatedly asks whether thresholds should be different, which suggests the agency may not yet have shown why the proposed thresholds are the right ones rather than merely workable candidates. 

## Step 7 — Legal Grounding

**Observed fact:** The NPRM repeatedly grounds itself in the GENIUS Act and identifies the Act as conferring OCC rulemaking and supervisory authority over defined entities and activities. 

**Assessment:** Statutory grounding appears facially strong for implementing provisions expressly delegated by the Act.

**Uncertain area:** The decision not to codify self-executing provisions may be legally permissible, but it creates a clarity tradeoff. A commenter could ask whether omission of codification undermines administrability for regulated entities even if the statute is self-executing.

## Step 18 — Decision Brief

### Strongest leverage point: **Baseline and threshold justification**

**Key NPRM sentences**

* New York baseline sentence. 
* Rule-stimulates-issuance sentence. 
* Option A / Option B calibration discussion. 

**Routine description in context**
This is a prudential implementation rule under a new statute. That means the agency will need to justify not just legal authority, but why its chosen counterfactual and risk thresholds are reasonable. In a novel market, line-drawing can be lawful, but it still must be explained.

**Regulatory effectiveness / statutory compliance / procedural adequacy**
If the baseline is wrong, the incremental burden analysis is distorted. If the reserve thresholds are underexplained, the final rule risks looking like supervisory intuition dressed as quantified regulation.

**Real-world consequence of invoking the leverage**
A comment here can force the OCC either to produce the missing analytical basis or to soften, tier, or better justify its quantitative requirements.

**Worth a comment?** **High**
