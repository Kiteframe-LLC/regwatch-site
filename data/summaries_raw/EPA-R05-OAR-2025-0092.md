## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim: Water cover is not workable because the solvents are water miscible

- **Underlying assumption:** The cited solvent blend behaves in practice exactly as EPA describes under operating conditions at this facility.
- **Failure scenario:** The blend composition, operating temperature, contamination, or maintenance practice differs enough that miscibility or surface behavior is not as assumed.
- **Procedural implication:** If the solvent behavior is more complicated than the preamble suggests, EPA may have rejected a listed control option without enough record support.

### Claim: Meeting the freeboard ratio would require a 108-inch freeboard height

- **Underlying assumption:** EPA and IDEM used the correct tank dimensions and interpreted the freeboard-ratio requirement correctly for Keystone's specific equipment.
- **Failure scenario:** The ratio calculation omitted feasible tank redesign, operational changes, or partial equipment replacement.
- **Procedural implication:** The infeasibility showing could be too facility-specific and underexplained to justify a SIP-approved alternative.

### Claim: Raising the roof or lowering the floor is cost prohibitive

- **Underlying assumption:** The agency has a defensible cost basis and did not just rely on bare assertion from the source.
- **Failure scenario:** No comparative cost table, payback analysis, or estimated emission-reduction denominator exists in the record.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can argue that EPA rejected an enumerated control option without transparent economic support.

### Claim: Refrigerated condensation and carbon adsorption are not feasible

- **Underlying assumption:** The cited worker-hazard, environmental-impact, and capital-cost concerns are material and not manageable through design or operation.
- **Failure scenario:** Similar facilities have installed such systems safely, or the cited concerns are generic and not quantified for Keystone.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA may have skipped intermediate alternatives or failed to explain why standard add-on controls are unreasonable here.

### Claim: An oil cover provides equivalent VOC control for an NMP/MEA blend

- **Underlying assumption:** Similar vapor densities and EPA's prior cleaner-technology guidance for heated NMP are enough to infer equivalent control for this mixed-solvent process.
- **Failure scenario:** Blend chemistry, agitation, temperature, throughput, or degradation reduces the oil layer's real-world control effectiveness.
- **Procedural implication:** The central equivalency finding could rest on analogy rather than demonstrated performance data.

### Claim: The SIP revision will not interfere with attainment or reasonable further progress

- **Underlying assumption:** Equivalent control at the tank level necessarily translates into no meaningful air-quality backsliding in the area.
- **Failure scenario:** Actual control efficiency is lower than expected, emissions vary by operating cycle, or there is no enforceable monitoring to ensure the oil layer is maintained.
- **Procedural implication:** The section 110(l) conclusion may be vulnerable if the implementation details and verification measures are thin.

Statutory compliance under section `110(l)` and the state RACT framework is easier to defend if EPA discloses quantitative support rather than relying mainly on analogy. Procedural adequacy improves if commenters and the public can review the technical basis directly.
