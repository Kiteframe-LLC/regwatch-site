## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Major claim: compliance burden likely exceeds benefits
- **Underlying assumption:** The agency has enough information about the compliance costs and enough confidence that the disclosure regime produces limited enforcement or deterrence value.
- **Failure scenario:** If disclosures materially help detect abusive basis-shifting transactions, then burden alone does not show net benefit from repeal.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask IRS to quantify enforcement benefits, deterrence value, and alternative narrower revisions before concluding repeal is superior.

### Major claim: 10,000 basis adjustments would be reported
- **Underlying assumption:** The underlying tax-return analysis is representative and the translation from threshold-crossing transactions to actual disclosures is reliable.
- **Failure scenario:** If the underlying population is smaller, duplicates across participants are miscounted, or many transactions would not satisfy related-party and "transaction of interest" conditions, the burden estimate may be overstated.
- **Procedural implication:** IRS should disclose methodology, sample year, and sensitivity bounds.

### Major claim: most participants would report rather than analyze applicability
- **Underlying assumption:** Regulated parties prefer over-reporting to threshold analysis because the compliance calculus is skewed toward disclosure.
- **Failure scenario:** Sophisticated taxpayers and advisors may instead litigate, seek advice, or apply narrower related-party analysis, reducing actual reporting volume.
- **Procedural implication:** The cost estimate depends heavily on an unsupported behavioral assumption.

### Major claim: repeal can be treated as retroactive to January 14, 2025
- **Underlying assumption:** Retroactive relief under section 7805(b)(7) is appropriate and does not create unaddressed enforcement or reliance problems.
- **Failure scenario:** Retroactive nullification may erase data already expected from the rule and create inconsistent treatment among parties that already complied versus those who relied on Notice 2025-23.
- **Procedural implication:** IRS should explain transition effects and record-retention consequences more directly.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

**Observed Facts**
- The NPRM would remove 26 CFR `1.6011-18`, which currently identifies certain partnership related-party basis adjustment transactions as transactions of interest.
- Affected parties are transaction participants and material advisors that otherwise would file Forms 8886 and 8918.
- Treasury and IRS say they may treat the eventual removal as effective retroactively to January 14, 2025, so the rule can be treated as though it "never [took] effect."
- The NPRM estimates annual filing-burden reduction of about $163 million.

**Inference**
- This is a deregulatory proposal focused more on reporting relief than on changing substantive tax liability rules.
- The strongest comment opportunities concern whether the agency adequately justified repeal, retroactivity, and the assumption that disclosure benefits are too small to justify the compliance burden.

**Evidence Quotes**
- "Section 1.6011-18 is removed."
- "participants and material advisors will be able to treat the Basis Shifting TOI Regulations as never having taken effect."
- "The Treasury Department and the IRS estimate that the aggregate economic effects... would be a reduction in filing burdens by $163 million."

## Step 3 — Agency Argument Tree

- **Regulatory objective:** Remove a reporting regime Treasury and IRS now view as too burdensome.
- **Problem definition:** The existing transaction-of-interest rule allegedly imposes complex, costly, and retroactive disclosure burdens on ordinary-course and tax-compliant activities.
- **Data used:** Tax return analysis, RAAS burden estimates, commenter-suggested wage rate, prior RFA data, and prior public comments.
- **Analytical method:** Estimate number of reportable basis adjustments, multiply by average participants and advisors, then multiply by hours and wage rates for Form 8886 and Form 8918 filings.
- **Alternatives considered:** Removal versus no-action baseline. No narrower amendments or threshold revisions are analyzed in this NPRM.
- **Agency conclusion:** Repeal the disclosure rule and treat repeal retroactively.

**Missing links / logical jumps**
- The NPRM asserts burden exceeds benefits, but does not quantify or meaningfully discuss the benefits side beyond a general description that disclosures could help identify abusive transactions.
- The estimate relies on several multiplier assumptions without a visible sensitivity analysis.
- The preamble does not meaningfully explain why revision, narrowing, or better targeting would be inferior to outright repeal.

- **Key sentence:** "Therefore, the Treasury Department and the IRS estimate that the aggregate economic effects... would be a reduction in filing burdens by $163 million."
- **Routine description:** Deregulatory NPRMs often quantify paperwork savings, but this estimate depends on several undisclosed assumptions.
- **Regulatory effectiveness / statutory compliance / procedural adequacy:** Procedurally adequate only at a high level; stronger support is needed for commenters to test the estimate.
- **Real-world consequence:** If the estimate is overstated, the repeal rationale may be weaker than presented.
- **Worth a comment?** High

### Leverage Point 2: Benefits-side analysis is underdeveloped
- **Key sentence:** "The Treasury Department and the IRS agree that the compliance burden... likely exceeds the benefits..."
- **Routine description:** An agency can change course, but a major reversal usually needs some engagement with the advantages of the prior rule.
- **Regulatory effectiveness / statutory compliance / procedural adequacy:** The current record is thin on benefits comparison.
- **Real-world consequence:** Full repeal may remove a disclosure tool without a transparent showing that its enforcement value is small.
- **Worth a comment?** High

### Leverage Point 3: Retroactive nullification deserves targeted comment
- **Key sentence:** "participants and material advisors will be able to treat the Basis Shifting TOI Regulations as never having taken effect."
- **Routine description:** Retroactive relief can be lawful, but it should be explained carefully because it changes reliance, records, and enforcement expectations.
- **Regulatory effectiveness / statutory compliance / procedural adequacy:** The NPRM states the intended result more clearly than its practical consequences.
- **Real-world consequence:** Ambiguity could affect parties that already complied or preserved information.
- **Worth a comment?** Moderate to high
