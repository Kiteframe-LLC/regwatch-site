## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim: Michigan's Part 8 NOx rules represent RACT

- **Underlying assumption:** Cross-state comparisons, ACT consistency, and Michigan's combustion-unit analysis are enough to show the adopted limits reflect reasonably available controls.
- **Failure scenario:** Michigan's rules lag more recent or more stringent combustion-source controls that EPA did not fully account for.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA may need to disclose the comparison table and category-specific benchmarks more fully so commenters can test whether the RACT conclusion is robust.

### Claim: No new reasonably available NOx controls have emerged

- **Underlying assumption:** EPA's due-diligence review was comprehensive enough across combustion technologies, unit sizes, and source categories.
- **Failure scenario:** The analysis screened out relevant control developments or treated absence of identified technologies as equivalent to a demonstrated feasibility review.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask EPA to disclose the technologies reviewed, how feasibility was assessed, and the time horizon of the screening.

### Claim: Deferred Part 8 subrules do not undermine approval of the rest of the package

- **Underlying assumption:** The omitted subrules are severable and their exclusion does not leave material gaps in enforceable NOx control coverage.
- **Failure scenario:** The deferred subrules matter more than the preamble suggests, weakening practical completeness or leaving important units without settled SIP treatment.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA should explain the practical effect of the deferred subrules and whether any interim compliance or emissions consequences follow.

### Claim: Moderate-area approval still makes sense after Serious reclassification

- **Underlying assumption:** Approving the overdue Moderate package now still has practical value and does not mask unresolved Serious-area obligations.
- **Failure scenario:** The Moderate-only framing understates whether the approved controls are already outdated for the area's current classification.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask EPA to explain how this action fits with the Serious-area SIP that became due on January 1, 2026.

### Claim: Alternative RACT procedures in Part 8 are sufficient

- **Underlying assumption:** The process for alternative NOx RACT and source-specific determinations will produce enforceable, timely controls where categorical rules do not fit.
- **Failure scenario:** The process is too open-ended or lacks enough public explanation to ensure equivalent control outcomes.
- **Procedural implication:** EPA may need to explain how alternative determinations will be reviewed and incorporated into the SIP.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts

- EPA proposes to approve Michigan Part 8 NOx rules into the SIP for the Western Michigan 2015 ozone nonattainment areas.
- The proposal covers NOx RACT requirements under Clean Air Act section `182(f)` as part of the broader Moderate-area RACT package.
- EPA says Michigan promulgated Part 8 rules for combustion engines, boilers, combustion turbines, miscellaneous process-specific combustion sources, and alternative NOx RACT procedures.
- EPA proposes to approve `R 336.1840`, `336.1841(1)-(7)`, `336.1842(1)-336.1842(9)`, `336.1843`, `336.1844(1)-336.1844(8)`, and `336.1845`.
- EPA is not proposing to act on `R 336.1841(8)`, `336.1842(10)`, or `336.1844(9)` in this action.
- EPA states that Western Michigan was reclassified from Marginal to Moderate effective November 7, 2022, and from Moderate to Serious effective January 16, 2025.
- EPA says Michigan submitted the NOx SIP revisions on May 5, 2025, and supplemented them on August 6, 2025.

### Inference

- This is a comparison-driven SIP approval more than a source-by-source engineering rulemaking. EPA is deciding whether Michigan's chosen NOx rule set is good enough to count as RACT, not deriving new federal limits in the preamble.
- The highest-value public comment opportunities are therefore about transparency, comparison methodology, deferred subrules, and how a Moderate-area approval fits with the area's later Serious classification.

## Step 10 — Regulatory Incentives

- Michigan has an incentive to preserve a rule package that is administratively workable and broadly comparable to neighboring states rather than materially more stringent.
- EPA has an incentive to rely on state-developed comparison tables and a TSD rather than restating every technical comparison in the preamble.
- Regulated entities benefit when EPA concludes that no newer or tighter reasonably available NOx controls need to be added.
- Public commenters therefore have the most leverage when they ask for the hidden comparison benchmarks, category-specific control logic, and the practical meaning of the deferred provisions.
