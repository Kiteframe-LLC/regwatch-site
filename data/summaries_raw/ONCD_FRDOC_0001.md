## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim: Banning attachments and web links is necessary to protect ONCD systems
- **Underlying assumption:** Accepting attachments or linked materials would create meaningful cybersecurity risk that cannot be mitigated through safer intake methods.
- **Failure scenario:** If ONCD can safely receive common file formats through screening or a secure upload workflow, the ban may unnecessarily burden requesters who need to identify records with precision.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask ONCD to justify the restriction more concretely or to offer a secure alternative for requesters who need to submit supporting descriptions or authorization materials.

### Claim: Requiring requests to be made in English is a workable procedural baseline
- **Underlying assumption:** English-only submission is necessary for orderly processing and does not materially impair public access.
- **Failure scenario:** Requesters with limited English proficiency may be deterred or may submit incomplete requests, increasing administrative closures instead of reducing burden.
- **Procedural implication:** ONCD should explain whether it considered multilingual assistance, plain-language guidance, or translation accommodations short of full foreign-language processing.

### Claim: Cross-referencing "approved ONCD processes" is sufficient for identity-protected submissions
- **Underlying assumption:** The outside webpage and agency processes are stable, accessible, and concrete enough that requesters can reliably know how to comply.
- **Failure scenario:** If the webpage changes, is unclear, or is temporarily unavailable, requesters may not know how to verify identity or avoid unnecessary retention of sensitive information.
- **Procedural implication:** A final rule should define the minimum approved submission channels in the regulation itself or at least require publicly accessible instructions with enough specificity to prevent confusion.

### Claim: The rule has no significant economic impact because it concerns agency administration
- **Underlying assumption:** The direct burdens on requesters, small news outlets, researchers, and small businesses seeking records are minor and mostly procedural.
- **Failure scenario:** If the rule's restrictions on format, language, or submission method increase legal or administrative costs for small requesters, the RFA certification may be underexplained.
- **Procedural implication:** Commenters can ask for a more concrete explanation of requester-side burdens even if ONCD reasonably concludes those burdens are modest.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

**Observed Facts**
- This is ONCD's first proposed set of FOIA and Privacy Act regulations.
- The proposal would add new `32 CFR` parts `2200` and `2201` covering FOIA requests, appeals, fees, fee waivers, Privacy Act access, amendment, accounting requests, and appeals.
- The rule centralizes authority in the ONCD General Counsel as Chief FOIA Officer and contemplates a FOIA Public Liaison and FOIA Requester Service Center.
- The draft imposes several requester-facing conditions, including written requests in English, no email attachments or web links, contact-information requirements, a 30-calendar-day response window to clarification requests before administrative closure, and identity-verification requirements for Privacy Act access.
- The preamble states the action is not significant under Executive Order `12866`, contains no PRA collection, and does not have significant RFA, federalism, or unfunded-mandate effects.

**Evidence quotes**
- "The Office of the National Cyber Director (ONCD) is issuing its first Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) and Privacy Act regulations."
- "All requests must be made in English."
- "To protect ONCD's computer systems, ONCD will not accept files sent as email attachments or as web links."
- "Failure to submit identifying information through an ONCD approved process may result in the failure to expunge your information..."

**Inference**
- The proposal is routine in the sense that agencies must have FOIA and Privacy Act procedures, but some of ONCD's chosen implementation details create practical access questions that are worth commenting on before they become binding.

## Step 9 — Failure Modes

- The no-attachment/no-link rule may make some requests harder to formulate precisely, especially when requesters need to provide lists, authorization materials, or supporting descriptions.
- The undefined "approved ONCD processes" language may create uncertainty about how to submit sensitive identity information safely.
- Administrative closures after nonresponse to clarification requests could fall disproportionately on less sophisticated or intermittent requesters.

## Step 10 — Regulatory Incentives

- Because the proposal forbids attachments and web links, requesters are incentivized to compress detail into email body text or physical mail, which may reduce clarity for complex requests.
- By leaving approved identity-submission channels outside the regulation, ONCD preserves operational flexibility but also reduces predictability for requesters.
