## Step 1 — Claim stress test

### Claim

CAA section 112(f)(2) allows only one residual-risk review within eight years.

**Underlying assumption**
The statutory structure forecloses later discretionary risk-based reviews.

**Failure scenario**
A reviewing court finds EPA’s new “best reading” underexplained, inconsistent with prior practice, or insufficiently responsive to the 2024 rationale.

**Procedural implication**
Rescission could be vulnerable as inadequately explained reversal.

---

### Claim

The revised 99.6% ARV standard is appropriate because shared infrastructure materially changes cost and operations.

**Underlying assumption**
New ARVs at existing facilities can commonly share ductwork / controls, and that materially affects cost-effectiveness.

**Failure scenario**
Facility-specific layouts, safety constraints, contamination segregation, or operational differences make sharing infeasible for a meaningful subset of sources.

**Procedural implication**
EPA may need clearer facility segmentation, sensitivity analysis, or an intermediate / conditional option.

---

### Claim

IRIS uncertainty is an additional reason to rescind the 2024 standards.

**Underlying assumption**
Uncertainty is large enough, and sufficiently decision-relevant, to support rollback rather than cautionary retention or narrower adjustment.

**Failure scenario**
Comments show the uncertainties were already known, bounded, litigated, and insufficient to negate use of IRIS for regulatory decision-making.

**Procedural implication**
EPA may appear to convert uncertainty into deregulation without a clear threshold for when uncertainty warrants repeal.

---

### Claim

PTE is not cost-effective and feasible.

**Underlying assumption**
The burden and impracticality of PTE are sufficiently established.

**Failure scenario**
The record contains only meeting summaries / stakeholder claims and lacks systematic engineering evidence by facility type.

**Procedural implication**
Weak support for rescinding a capture requirement that affects enforceability and emissions integrity.

---

### Claim

Facilities should be allowed to use either parametric monitoring or CEMS.

**Underlying assumption**
Parametric monitoring plus testing provides comparably reliable compliance assurance in practice.

**Failure scenario**
Source variability, upset conditions, maintenance drift, or parameter gaming produce worse detection than CEMS.

**Procedural implication**
Potential monitoring weakness and enforcement ambiguity.

## Step 2 — Essence of the rule

### Observed facts

EPA proposes to:

* rescind the 2024 risk-based standards adopted under CAA section 112(f)(2);
* revise the new-source ARV standard at facilities using at least 10 tpy EtO from 99.9% to 99.6%;
* allow compliance demonstration via either parametric monitoring/performance testing or CEMS;
* rescind the PTE requirement;
* make technical corrections. 

Affected entities are commercial sterilization facilities using EtO; EPA says the updated facility list contains **89 facilities**. 

Statutory authority invoked is CAA section 112, especially sections 112(d)(2)-(6) and 112(f)(2). 

### Inference

The proposal is not a narrow cleanup. It is a substantive rollback-plus-revision package that changes legal theory, technology stringency, monitoring architecture, and capture design in one rulemaking.

## Step 6 — Technical coherence

**Assessment:** **Mixed / partially coherent**

EPA’s ARV revision has an intelligible engineering logic: if new ARVs at existing facilities can share existing controls, prior cost estimates may have overstated costs. 

But coherence weakens where EPA moves from:

* acknowledged IRIS uncertainty,
* to repeal-supporting uncertainty,
* without a clearly stated decision rule;
  and from:
* implementation complaints,
* to rescission of PTE / monitoring requirements,
* without clearly shown engineering sensitivity analysis in the attached materials.

For an EPA technical rollback, the weakest apparent spots are the usual ones: **sensitivity analysis, facility heterogeneity, and intermediate options**.

## Step 18 — Decision brief

### Leverage point 1: PTE rescission

**Key sentence**
EPA proposes “to rescind the requirement that PTE be used to ensure complete capture of EtO.” 

**Routine description**
In a hazardous-air-pollutant rule, capture assumptions matter because downstream control percentages do not protect health if emissions are not fully captured upstream. A categorical removal of capture architecture needs engineering support, not just implementation concern.

**Why it matters**

* **Regulatory effectiveness:** capture failure can undercut nominal control efficiency.
* **Statutory compliance:** weak capture assumptions can destabilize whether standards actually achieve required reductions.
* **Procedural adequacy:** categorical rescission without segmented engineering analysis is vulnerable.

**Real-world consequence**
A weaker capture regime can make high-control percentages look protective on paper while allowing more fugitive EtO in practice.

**Worth a comment?** **High**

---

### Leverage point 2: Monitoring choice rollback

**Key sentence**
EPA proposes to allow facilities to choose between testing/parametric monitoring and CEMS. 

**Routine description**
Compliance architecture is an enforcement question, not just a burden question. If EPA is replacing continuous monitoring with periodic/parameter-based options, the record should show equivalent reliability across source types and operating conditions.

**Why it matters**

* **Regulatory effectiveness:** detection of excursions and drift.
* **Procedural adequacy:** needs comparative evidentiary support.
* **Adversarial interpretation risk:** easier for regulated parties to satisfy form without continuous verification.

**Real-world consequence**
Emission variability may go unseen between tests.

**Worth a comment?** **High**

---

### Leverage point 3: ARV 99.9% to 99.6% revision

**Key sentence**
EPA says the 99.6% option would allow shared infrastructure and reduce costs. 

**Routine description**
That is plausible, but it is configuration-sensitive. The issue is not whether sharing can occur; it is whether EPA has shown where, how often, and under what constraints.

**Why it matters**

* **Regulatory effectiveness:** one uniform standard may fit some facilities and misfit others.
* **Procedural adequacy:** a segmented alternative may be more defensible.
* **Alternative analysis:** EPA should test intermediate or conditional approaches.

**Real-world consequence**
A broad rollback may be justified by retrofit scenarios that do not represent the full population.

**Worth a comment?** **Moderate to High**

---

### Leverage point 4: IRIS uncertainty as repeal rationale

**Key sentence**
EPA says significant uncertainties in the 2016 IRIS value are an additional reason to support repeal. 

**Routine description**
Uncertainty is real, but agencies need a limiting principle. Otherwise uncertainty becomes a one-way ratchet toward rollback.

**Why it matters**

* **Statutory compliance:** risk assessment under uncertainty is common in air toxics regulation.
* **Procedural adequacy:** the agency should explain why this uncertainty warrants repeal rather than narrower calibration.
* **Administrative record:** commenters can force EPA to state a decision threshold.

**Real-world consequence**
Without a clear standard, future toxic-risk regulation becomes easier to undo by invoking unresolved scientific variation.

**Worth a comment?** **High**
