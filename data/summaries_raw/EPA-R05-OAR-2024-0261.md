## Step 1 — Claim Stress Test

### Claim A

**Claim:** The Q/d screening method is a reasonable way to decide which Illinois sources merit four-factor review.

**Underlying assumption:** Using 2016 inventory data and a Q/d threshold of four at facilities above 10 remains representative enough for 2028 visibility impacts.

**Failure scenario:** The 2016 base year or the threshold values miss materially important sources or newer operating patterns, especially where retirements, fuel switches, or operational changes alter comparative impacts.

**Procedural implication:** Commenters could ask EPA to justify the threshold numerically, show sensitivity to alternative thresholds, or explain why a source outside the screen would not matter.

### Claim B

**Claim:** Existing controls at Baldwin, Newton, Powerton, and Kincaid are already sufficient for reasonable progress in the second implementation period.

**Underlying assumption:** Historic emissions trends and enforceable MPS/CPS or permit limits are a reliable proxy for future performance through 2028.

**Failure scenario:** Existing controls perform inconsistently, or emissions rebound in ways not captured by annual averages or the summarized record.

**Procedural implication:** Commenters can request unit-level variability data, recent compliance histories, and a clearer explanation of why no incremental controls were modeled.

### Claim C

**Claim:** New controls for Prairie State, Marion, Rain CII Carbon, Holcim/Lafarge, and ADM are not cost-effective and therefore not necessary.

**Underlying assumption:** EPA and Illinois used a meaningful set of technically feasible controls and evaluated cost per ton in a way that fits regional-haze reasonable-progress practice.

**Failure scenario:** Intermediate options, operational limits, or source-specific retrofits were screened out too quickly, or the record lacks enough context to judge whether quoted dollar-per-ton figures are high or low for this program.

**Procedural implication:** This is a primary comment target because the record shows option lists and cost figures, but only limited justification for why those figures resolve the reasonable-progress question.

### Claim D

**Claim:** Projected 2028 visibility conditions below both adjusted and unadjusted URP glidepaths support approval.

**Underlying assumption:** The underlying modeling platform, 2016 base year, and source-apportionment methods remain sound and are not materially undermined by uncertainty.

**Failure scenario:** The favorable glidepath projection is sensitive to modeling assumptions or excludes meaningful emissions changes, sector shifts, or local conditions.

**Procedural implication:** Commenters can request clearer presentation of uncertainty ranges and the consequences if modeled conditions are less favorable than projected.

### Claim E

**Claim:** Illinois' older infrastructure SIP submissions for the 2012 PM2.5 and 2015 ozone NAAQS satisfy the visibility-protection prong.

**Underlying assumption:** The regional haze analysis and current visibility record adequately support the infrastructure transport finding despite the age of those SIP submittals.

**Failure scenario:** The NPRM treats the infrastructure approvals as derivative of regional haze without fully explaining the bridge between the two records.

**Procedural implication:** Commenters could ask EPA to explain the exact evidentiary path from the regional haze analysis to the interstate visibility transport approval.

## Step 2 — Essence of the Rule

### Observed Facts

* EPA proposes to approve Illinois' June 3, 2024 regional haze SIP revision for the second implementation period.
* EPA also proposes to approve portions of Illinois' September 29, 2017 and May 16, 2019 infrastructure SIP submissions for the 2012 PM2.5 and 2015 ozone NAAQS visibility prong.
* Illinois used LADCO analysis, 2016 inventory data, and a Q/d screening approach to identify sources for review.
* Illinois treated some units as retired or replaced with gas-fired units, treated several coal units as already effectively controlled, and conducted four-factor analyses for nine remaining units.
* The supplied TSD excerpt lists four-factor analyses for Prairie State, Southern Illinois Power Cooperative Marion, Rain CII Carbon, Lafarge Midwest/Holcim Grand Chain, and Archer Daniels Midland.
* The record says projected 2028 visibility conditions for 16 affected Class I areas are below both adjusted and unadjusted URP glidepaths.

### Inference

This is an approval-oriented EPA technical rulemaking that leans on state and regional modeling plus source-specific cost analyses to conclude that Illinois does not need additional controls for this implementation period. The strongest opportunities for public comment are about explanation quality, threshold choices, and the completeness of cost-effectiveness reasoning, not about basic legal authority.

## Step 6 — Technical Coherence

The rule is technically coherent at a high level. The source-selection method, retirement screen, existing-control analysis, and four-factor analyses fit EPA regional haze practice.

The main coherence issue is not contradiction. It is compression. The agency moves quickly from lists of possible controls and cost figures to the conclusion that additional controls are not necessary. That can be reasonable, but commenters would benefit from a clearer explanation of comparative decision criteria and sensitivity to changed assumptions.

**Observed Fact:** The 2019 EPA modeling TSD excerpt says the platform includes updated 2016 emissions, 2028 projected visibility impairment, model performance information, and uncertainties.

**Inference:** The modeling foundation appears real and mature, but the rule text shown here does not fully surface uncertainty analysis in a way ordinary commenters can readily assess.

## Step 9 — Failure Modes

* A screened-out source with lower Q/d or changed operations could still matter more than the method suggests.
* Existing-control assumptions for Baldwin, Newton, Powerton, or Kincaid could understate future emissions variability.
* Cost-effectiveness conclusions could shift if commenters supply lower-cost operational alternatives or more current control-cost information.
* The favorable glidepath comparison could prove fragile if modeled assumptions about sector trends or non-EGU emissions are off.
* The infrastructure visibility-prong approvals could be vulnerable if EPA does not clearly connect them to the current technical record.

## Step 10 — Regulatory Incentives

* The rule may create an incentive for sources to emphasize fleet-wide or historical averages rather than unit-specific worst-case performance.
* Threshold-based source selection can create incentives to defend screen design rather than engage with marginal sources just below the cutoff.
* Treating existing controls as sufficient may reduce pressure to evaluate incremental improvements unless commenters force more precise comparisons.

**Key sentences from the NPRM**

* “Illinois EPA determined that additional control measures are not necessary to make reasonable progress in the second implementation period and that pursuing additional emission reductions through new emission control equipment or emissions limitations is not cost-effective.”

**Routine description situated in context**

Regional haze SIP approvals often rise or fall on source selection and four-factor analysis. Here, Illinois used a threshold-driven screen and then concluded that existing controls or additional-control costs justify no new requirements. That is a recognizable EPA pattern, but it only works if the selection thresholds and decision criteria are sufficiently explained for public evaluation.

**Regulatory effectiveness, statutory compliance, procedural adequacy**

The action appears directionally consistent with CAA section 169A and EPA's regional haze framework, but procedural adequacy would improve if EPA explained why the chosen thresholds and cost figures are the right decision points rather than simply acceptable ones.

**Real-world consequence of invoking the leverage**

If commenters press this issue effectively, EPA may add threshold sensitivity analysis, fuller cost comparisons, or more source-specific reasoning. That would strengthen the final record whether or not the ultimate approval outcome changes.
